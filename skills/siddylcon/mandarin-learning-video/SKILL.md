# Mandarin Learning Video Maker — Chinese Language Lesson Videos

The Mandarin learner has downloaded three apps, bookmarked twelve YouTube channels, purchased a textbook that weighs more than some laptops, and can confidently say "nǐ hǎo" and "xièxie" — two phrases that represent approximately 0.0003% of functional Mandarin fluency and 100% of what most Western learners achieve before the tonal system convinces them that their mouth was not designed for this language, a conclusion that is completely wrong but understandable given that most Mandarin-learning resources either skip tones entirely (producing learners who say "I want to kiss you" when they meant "I want to ask you" because the difference between wěn and wèn is a tone that nobody taught them to hear) or obsess over tones so thoroughly in lesson one that the learner quits before learning their second word. Mandarin Chinese video content for language learning works because Mandarin is fundamentally a language where seeing and hearing happen simultaneously — the character 马 means horse but also appears in 妈 (mother), 骂 (scold), and 吗 (question particle), each distinguished by a tone that must be heard to be understood and a character that must be seen to be read, making video the only medium that delivers both channels at once. This tool transforms Mandarin instruction into polished language-learning videos — tone-training segments with visual pitch contours and native-speaker modeling, character-writing tutorials showing stroke order with the precision that beautiful handwriting requires, conversational dialogues in real-world scenarios with pinyin and character subtitles, cultural-context lessons explaining the social rules that textbooks mention in footnotes, HSK-aligned vocabulary building structured for exam preparation, and the immersive content that surrounds the learner with natural Mandarin at comprehensible speed. Built for Mandarin teachers creating digital course materials, language-learning channels building subscriber bases, HSK prep services producing structured study content, cultural organizations promoting Chinese language education, corporate training teams preparing employees for China assignments, and anyone whose Mandarin instruction needs the visual-auditory combination that only video provides.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Tone Training — Hearing and Producing the Four Tones
"Create a 6-minute tone training video for absolute beginners. The problem (0-20 sec): two sentences on screen, identical in pinyin except for tone marks. 'māo' (cat) vs 'máo' (hair/fur). A native speaker says both. 'These two words use the same sounds. The difference is the pitch of your voice. In English, pitch conveys emotion. In Mandarin, pitch conveys meaning. Get the tone wrong and you're saying a different word.' The four tones (20-150 sec): each tone gets its own segment with visual pitch contour. Tone 1 — high flat (20-50 sec): animated pitch line — straight, high. Speaker says 'mā' (mother). 'Tone 1 is high and flat. Imagine holding a note while singing. Don't let it drop.' Visual: the pitch line stays perfectly level. Practice word: 'mā' — mother. The speaker says it three times. The pitch contour displays in real-time. 'Now you try. Hold the pitch steady. High and flat.' Tone 2 — rising (50-80 sec): pitch line rises from mid to high. Speaker says 'má' (hemp). 'Tone 2 rises — like the pitch of your voice when you ask "what?" in English.' Say 'what?' — show the pitch contour. It rises. 'That's Tone 2. Your voice is already trained to do this.' Practice: 'má' three times with real-time pitch display. Tone 3 — dipping (80-115 sec): pitch line dips low then rises slightly. Speaker says 'mǎ' (horse). 'Tone 3 dips down and comes back up slightly. The key: go LOW. Most learners don't go low enough.' Show common mistake: a Tone 3 that doesn't dip enough — it sounds like Tone 2. 'If it doesn't feel uncomfortably low, you're not low enough.' Practice with exaggerated dip, then natural speed. Tone 4 — falling (115-150 sec): pitch line drops sharply from high to low. Speaker says 'mà' (scold). 'Tone 4 falls sharply — like saying "no!" to a dog. Short, decisive, downward.' Practice: 'mà' three times. 'Tone 4 is the easiest for English speakers because we use falling pitch for commands.' The mā-má-mǎ-mà drill (150-220 sec): all four tones in sequence. Speaker demonstrates slowly. Pitch contours for all four on screen simultaneously. 'Let's go through all four. Listen first.' All four, slowly. 'Now match me.' One at a time with pauses. Then in sequence. 'The classic Mandarin tongue twister: "māma mà mǎ de máo" — Mom scolds the horse's fur.' Hear it at natural speed. Then broken down word by word. Tone pairs (220-300 sec): 'Real Mandarin rarely uses single syllables. Tones change when combined.' Show tone sandhi: Tone 3 + Tone 3 = Tone 2 + Tone 3. 'nǐ hǎo' is actually pronounced 'ní hǎo.' 'This isn't an exception — it's a rule. Your mouth naturally wants to do this because two dips in a row is physically awkward.' Practice common pairs: 'nǐ hǎo' (hello), 'xièxie' (thank you), 'duìbuqǐ' (sorry). Each with pitch contour display. Close (300-360 sec): 'Tones feel impossible for the first week and automatic by the third month. The key: exaggerate early. Sound ridiculous. Your brain needs the extreme version before it can produce the subtle version.' Daily practice: 'Listen to 5 minutes of Mandarin daily — podcasts, TV, music. Don't try to understand. Just let your brain absorb the melody of the language. The tones will start to sound natural before you can produce them naturally.'"

### 2. Survival Phrases — Real-World Conversation
"Build a 5-minute survival Mandarin video for travelers. Opening (0-10 sec): 'Ten phrases that will get you through a week in China. No textbook. No grammar. Just the phrases that matter.' Phrase 1 (10-30 sec): 'Nǐ hǎo — Hello.' Speaker says it. Pinyin and characters on screen: 你好. 'You already know this one. Use it everywhere — shops, restaurants, hotels. The response will be the same: nǐ hǎo.' Phrase 2 (30-55 sec): 'Xièxie — Thank you.' 谢谢. 'Pronounced like "shyeh-shyeh." Use it constantly. Chinese culture values politeness and xièxie is free.' Phrase 3 (55-80 sec): 'Duōshao qián? — How much?' 多少钱？ 'Point at what you want, say this phrase. The vendor will type the price on a calculator or hold up fingers.' Show the scenario: at a market, pointing at fruit, asking the price. Phrase 4 (80-110 sec): 'Tài guì le — Too expensive.' 太贵了. 'The three most powerful words in any Chinese market. Say this with a smile, shake your head gently, and the price drops 20-40%.' Show the negotiation dance: ask price, react with tài guì le, vendor offers new price. Phrase 5 (110-135 sec): 'Zhège — This one.' 这个. 'Point and say zhège. Works in restaurants (point at the menu), shops (point at the product), anywhere you need to indicate "I want that thing."' Phrase 6 (135-160 sec): 'Wǒ yào — I want...' 我要. 'Wǒ yào zhège = I want this one. Wǒ yào kāfēi = I want coffee. The magic formula: wǒ yào + [thing].' Phrase 7 (160-185 sec): 'Cèsuǒ zài nǎr? — Where is the bathroom?' 厕所在哪儿？ 'The most important survival phrase in any language. Memorize it before you land.' Phrase 8 (185-210 sec): 'Bù yào — Don't want / No thanks.' 不要. 'Street vendors, taxi card offers, anything you need to decline politely but firmly. Say it with a smile and a wave.' Phrase 9 (210-240 sec): 'Tīng bù dǒng — I don't understand.' 听不懂. 'The honest phrase. When someone launches into rapid Mandarin and you've exhausted your ten phrases, this buys you patience and often a switch to simpler words or gestures.' Phrase 10 (240-270 sec): 'Màn yìdiǎn — A little slower.' 慢一点. 'If they're speaking Mandarin you almost understand, this phrase asks them to slow down. Often the difference between comprehension and confusion is speed, not vocabulary.' Review (270-300 sec): all ten phrases on screen with pinyin and characters. Quick-fire repetition — speaker says each, pauses for the viewer to repeat. Close: 'Ten phrases. Five minutes of practice. A week of survival in China. Bù kèqi — you're welcome.'"

### 3. Character Writing — Stroke Order Tutorial
"Produce a 4-minute character writing tutorial for beginners. Opening (0-15 sec): a brush writing a character in real-time — fluid, beautiful. 'Chinese characters look like art. They are art. But they're also a system — and the system has rules that make writing 50,000 characters manageable.' The basic strokes (15-80 sec): 'Every character is built from the same basic strokes — like every building uses the same bricks.' Eight basic strokes, each demonstrated: héng (horizontal —), shù (vertical |), piě (left-falling /), nà (right-falling \\), diǎn (dot), tí (rising), zhé (turning), gōu (hook). Each stroke animated with direction arrows. 'These eight strokes build every character in the Chinese language. Master these and you can write anything.' Practice: draw héng and shù. 'Horizontal before vertical. Left before right. Top before bottom. Outside before inside. These are the stroke-order rules.' Simple characters (80-180 sec): Character 1: 一 (yī — one). 'One stroke. One meaning. The simplest character.' Show the stroke — left to right. Character 2: 二 (èr — two). 'Two strokes. Top horizontal first, then bottom.' Character 3: 三 (sān — three). 'You see the pattern.' Character 4: 人 (rén — person). Two strokes: piě then nà. Show the stroke order — left stroke first. 'A person with two legs. See it?' Character 5: 大 (dà — big). Three strokes. 'A person with outstretched arms. 人 + arms = big.' Show the evolution: stick figure → character. Character 6: 山 (shān — mountain). Three strokes: center vertical, left vertical, right vertical. 'Three peaks. The character looks like what it means.' Character 7: 水 (shuǐ — water). Four strokes. Show the ancient pictograph: flowing water. Show the modern character. 'The character evolved from a picture of a river.' Compound building (180-260 sec): 'Characters combine to form new meanings.' 木 (mù — tree/wood). Show it: a tree with branches and roots. 林 (lín — forest). Two trees side by side. 'Two trees = forest.' 森 (sēn — dense forest). Three trees. 'Three trees = dense forest.' 'See the logic? Characters aren't random — they're building blocks.' Another example: 日 (rì — sun) + 月 (yuè — moon) = 明 (míng — bright). 'Sun + moon = bright. The logic is poetic.' Practice segment (260-300 sec): viewer practice. Show 人 with numbered stroke order. 'Pause the video. Get a piece of paper. Write 人 five times. Left stroke first, right stroke second.' Then 大: 'Write 大 five times. Horizontal first, then left stroke, then right stroke.' Then 山: 'Five times. Center, left, right.' Close: 'You've just written your first Chinese characters. They took 3,000 years to evolve and 3 minutes to learn. Tomorrow, write them again from memory. The hand remembers what the eye forgets.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Always show pinyin AND characters together** — Learners need both bridges simultaneously. The AI displays dual notation when pinyin_display is enabled.
2. **Use pitch contour visualization for tones** — Seeing the pitch shape helps learners reproduce it. The AI renders real-time contours when pitch_contour is enabled.
3. **Teach through real scenarios, not vocabulary lists** — "Point and say zhège" is immediately useful. The AI structures lessons around functional scenarios.
4. **Show character evolution from pictograph** — 山 looking like a mountain makes it memorable. The AI includes etymological visuals for characters.
5. **Include pause-and-practice prompts** — "Now you try" with silence is active learning. The AI inserts practice pauses after demonstrations.
