# Speed & Urgency

## Core Concept

Speed is the most underappreciated competitive advantage. Like the SR-71
Blackbird -- over 3,000 missiles fired at it, zero hits -- its only defense
was acceleration. In business, innovating so fast that competitors are
always copying what you did years ago is the ultimate moat.

Key principles:
- **Speed is both offense and defense** -- it creates opportunities AND
  protects against competition
- **A factory at 2x speed = 2 factories** -- aim for each unit of capacity
  to produce what normally requires 5-10x
- **Be a vector, not a scalar** -- direction matters as much as magnitude.
  High speed in the wrong direction is waste.
- **Every minute counts at scale** -- at $2B/week revenue, a 30-minute
  meeting improvement can produce $100M in impact

## Step-by-Step Application

### Step 1: Audit Current Speed

Ask: "How long does it take from idea to shipped product/feature?
Where is time being wasted?"

Map the timeline:
- Decision time: How long from "we should do X" to "we're doing X"?
- Build time: How long from start to first working version?
- Feedback time: How long until you know if it works?
- Iteration time: How long to incorporate feedback?

### Step 2: Set Aggressive Timelines

**"Work expands to fill the time allotted."** Whatever timeline you set,
it will never be less than that. So set the most aggressive timeline
possible.

This isn't about fake deadlines. It's about understanding that people
massively overestimate how long things should take. The aggressive timeline
forces creative problem-solving that comfortable timelines never do.

Guidelines:
- Take the team's estimate and ask: "What would we need to change to
  do this in half the time?"
- Don't accept "impossible" without specific physics constraints
- **"It's okay to scrap equipment or money. It's never okay to scrap time."**
- People don't understand exponential curves -- small calendar shifts
  produce enormous percentage differences in outcome

### Step 3: Parallelize Everything

Ask: "Which of these things MUST be sequential, and which can happen
simultaneously?"

Most organizations serialize work that could be parallel:
- Design, prototype, and manufacturing planning can overlap
- Multiple features can be developed simultaneously by different teams
- Testing can start before the full build is complete
- Procurement can happen in parallel with design

**Rule**: If there's no physics reason two things must be sequential,
they should be parallel.

### Step 4: Break Down the "Impossible"

When facing a seemingly impossible goal:

1. **Decompose it**: What are the 5-10 sub-problems?
2. **Identify which sub-problems are actually hard** vs. merely tedious
3. **Solve the hard ones first** -- if they're truly impossible, find
   out early
4. **Sprint on the tedious ones** -- these just need time and effort

Ask: "What specific thing makes this impossible? Is it a physics
constraint, or a resource/willingness constraint?"

### Step 5: Install Urgency Culture

Urgency isn't about panic. It's about valuing time as the scarcest
resource:

- **Default to action**: When two options aren't obviously different,
  pick one and go. "Better to pick a path and keep moving than vacillate
  endlessly."
- **Measure cycle time**: Make it visible. What gets measured improves.
- **Celebrate speed**: Praise fast shipping, not just quality.
- **Short meetings, standing up**: Time-box ruthlessly.
- **Maniacal urgency** is a feature, not a bug -- it keeps momentum
  when enthusiasm fades.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Speed without direction**: Moving fast in the wrong direction
  compounds the problem. Validate direction FIRST, then accelerate.
- **Confusing urgency with panic**: Urgency is calm, focused intensity.
  Panic makes things slower through mistakes.
- **Ignoring quality entirely**: Speed without minimum quality creates
  rework that's slower overall. Find the speed-quality balance.
- **Burning people out**: Sprints need to be time-bounded. Permanent
  sprinting is just bad planning.
- **Analysis paralysis disguised as "thoroughness"**: If you've been
  analyzing for longer than it would take to build a prototype, stop
  analyzing and start building.
