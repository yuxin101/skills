# Extreme Team Building

## Core Concept

A company is just a group of people pursuing a goal. Success depends on
how talented and hardworking the group is, multiplied by the alignment of
their direction.

Key principles:
- **Small teams outperform large ones** -- fewer people = less coordination
  overhead, faster decisions, higher ownership
- **Hire for intensity and agency, not credentials** -- someone who ships
  relentlessly beats someone with a perfect resume
- **The mission is the recruiting tool** -- the right people are attracted
  by the audacity of the goal
- **"Special Forces" approach** -- minimum passing grade is excellent. A
  small group of technically strong people will always beat a large group
  of moderately strong people
- **Money is not the constraint; exceptional people are** -- you can't just
  throw money at R&D. The factory producing excellent engineers doesn't exist.

## Step-by-Step Application

### Step 1: Define What You Actually Need

Before hiring, ask: "What specific outcomes do you need in the next
90 days? Not roles -- outcomes."

Common mistake: hiring for a role ("We need a Product Manager") instead
of an outcome ("We need someone who can take this from concept to shipped
in 8 weeks").

| Role (Don't Start Here) | Outcome (Start Here) |
|--------------------------|---------------------|
| "Frontend Engineer" | "Ship the dashboard by Q2" |
| "Marketing Lead" | "Get first 1,000 users in 60 days" |
| "Operations Manager" | "Reduce fulfillment from 5 days to 1" |

### Step 2: Identify the Right Profile

For each outcome, ask: "What kind of person has already done something
like this, or could figure it out?"

Key traits to prioritize:
1. **Agency**: Do they act without being told? Do they figure things out?
2. **Intensity**: Willing to work at extreme pace when mission demands it?
3. **First-principles thinking**: Can they reason from fundamentals, or
   do they only follow established playbooks?
4. **Building track record**: Have they actually built and shipped things?
   (Not just planned, analyzed, or consulted)
5. **Mission alignment**: Do they genuinely care about what you're building?
6. **Attitude over skills**: "Skills can be taught. Attitude changes
   require a brain transplant."

### Step 3: Test, Don't Interview

Traditional interviews test the ability to interview. Better approaches:

- **Trial project**: Paid, time-boxed project that mirrors actual work.
  2-5 days is enough.
- **Past work deep-dive**: Ask them to tell the story of their hardest
  technical problem in detail. The real doer knows the details; the faker
  doesn't. "Anything can look good on PowerPoint."
- **Reference backdoor**: Talk to people who worked with them, not just
  their chosen references. Judge character by their associates.
- **Speed signal**: How fast do they respond? How fast do they produce?
  Speed of initial communication often predicts speed of work.

### Step 4: Structure for Speed

Once you have the team:

- **Minimize layers**: 1-2 layers max. Every layer adds latency.
- **Everyone thinks like the chief engineer**: Everyone needs system-level
  understanding. Bad local optimizations happen when people only see their
  piece.
- **Clear ownership**: Every project has exactly one owner. Shared
  ownership = no ownership.
- **Short cycles**: Ship something every 1-2 weeks. Long cycles hide
  problems.
- **Direct communication**: Anyone can talk to anyone. No "go through
  your manager" culture.
- **Separate design, engineering, and manufacturing = dysfunction.** Keep
  these functions integrated.

### Step 5: Remove Obstacles Relentlessly

The leader's primary job is clearing the path. Ask regularly:

"What's slowing you down? What's blocking you? What bureaucratic nonsense
should I eliminate?"

Common obstacles to kill:
- Unnecessary meetings (delete most of them)
- Approval chains (push authority down)
- Process theater (reports nobody reads, metrics nobody uses)
- Tool sprawl (consolidate)
- Unclear priorities (one priority, not five)

### Step 6: Maintain Standards Ruthlessly

- **Address underperformance within 24 hours**, not at the next review.
- **Praise specifically**: "Great job" is useless. "The way you structured
  the data pipeline to handle backpressure was exactly right" is useful.
- **Exit fast**: If someone isn't working out after genuine effort to
  course-correct, move quickly. Keeping the wrong person hurts everyone.
- **All bad news should be given loudly and often. Good news can be said
  quietly and once.**

### Step 7: One Key Metric

Give teams one key metric to focus on. "Video games without a score are
boring." If everyone is optimizing for different metrics, alignment is
impossible.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Hiring for comfort**: People like yourself feel safe but create blind
  spots. Hire for complementary strengths.
- **Over-hiring**: 5 intense people outperform 20 average ones. Resist
  the urge to throw bodies at problems.
- **Confusing activity with output**: Busy != productive. Measure what
  shipped, not hours worked.
- **Neglecting culture**: Culture forms whether you design it or not.
  Be intentional or inherit chaos.
- **Forgetting the human element**: Intensity is powerful but has a cost.
  Sustainable intensity > burnout. High-performing teams still need
  recovery time and psychological safety.
