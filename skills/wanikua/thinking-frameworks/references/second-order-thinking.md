# Second-Order Thinking

**Second-order thinking** asks "And then what?" It pushes beyond the immediate, obvious consequences of an action to consider the cascading effects — the consequences of consequences. Popularized by Howard Marks (Oaktree Capital), it's the difference between a novice chess player (who sees one move ahead) and a grandmaster (who sees five). Most bad decisions come from optimizing for first-order effects while ignoring the second- and third-order effects that ultimately dominate.

## Step 1: Identify the Action or Decision

- What is the **proposed action, decision, or change** being considered?
- What is the **intended first-order effect** (the obvious, immediate result)?
- Who is the decision-maker, and what is their time horizon?

## Step 2: First-Order Effects (Immediate)

*What happens right away?*

- List all **direct, immediate consequences** of this action.
- For each: Who is affected? How? Positively or negatively?
- These are what most people consider. They are usually obvious and often positive (that's why the action is being considered).
- Time horizon: days to weeks.

## Step 3: Second-Order Effects (Then What?)

*What do the first-order effects cause?*

For each first-order effect, ask **"And then what happens?"**

- How will **other actors respond** to the first-order effects?
  - Competitors, customers, regulators, markets, employees, the public
  - People are not passive — they adapt, game the system, and find workarounds
- What **behavioral changes** will the first-order effects incentivize?
  - Follow the incentives: if you change the reward structure, behavior will shift
- What **equilibrium shifts** occur?
  - Markets, supply chains, social norms, power dynamics
- Time horizon: weeks to months.

## Step 4: Third-Order Effects (And Then?)

*What do the second-order effects cause?*

Push one more level deeper:

- What **systemic changes** emerge from the accumulated second-order effects?
- Are there any **feedback loops** that amplify or dampen the effects?
- Do any **tipping points** get crossed — where gradual change becomes sudden transformation?
- What **new problems** are created by solving the original problem?
- What **norms, cultures, or institutions** shift over the long term?
- Time horizon: months to years.

## Step 5: Map the Chains

Organize the cascade visually:

```
Action → 1st Order Effect A → 2nd Order Effect A1 → 3rd Order Effect A1a
                              → 2nd Order Effect A2 → 3rd Order Effect A2a
       → 1st Order Effect B → 2nd Order Effect B1 → ...
```

- Identify which chains are **most consequential** (highest impact × highest probability).
- Identify which chains involve **irreversible** changes (these deserve extra attention).

## Step 6: Historical Parallels

- Has a similar action been taken before? What happened?
- Examples of famous second-order failures:
  - Cobra bounty in colonial India (paying for dead cobras → cobra farming)
  - Rent control → housing shortage → higher prices for non-controlled units
  - Antibiotics overuse → antibiotic resistance
  - Social media engagement optimization → polarization → erosion of trust
- What **analogous situation** provides a warning or a model?

## Step 7: The "Who Adapts?" Test

For every proposed action, identify:
- Who will **benefit** from the first-order effect and **lobby to preserve** it?
- Who will be **harmed** and seek **workarounds or countermeasures**?
- What **perverse incentives** does this action create?
- How will **smart, self-interested actors** game this system?

## Step 8: Net Assessment

- **First-order**: Does the action achieve its intended goal? (Usually yes — that's why it's proposed.)
- **Second-order**: Do the downstream effects support or undermine the goal?
- **Third-order**: Is the world better or worse off after all cascading effects?
- **Time-weighted**: When do positive effects peak? When do negative effects peak? Is there a temporal mismatch (short-term gain, long-term pain)?

## Step 9: Recommendations

- Should the action be taken given the full cascade of effects?
- If yes, what **mitigations** can address the negative second/third-order effects?
- If no, what **alternative action** has better second-order properties?
- What **early warning signals** should be monitored to detect whether negative cascades are materializing?

---

First-order thinkers look for simple, easy answers. Second-order thinkers ask "And then what?" The best decisions often look *wrong* in the short term because they sacrifice first-order benefits to avoid second-order disasters.
