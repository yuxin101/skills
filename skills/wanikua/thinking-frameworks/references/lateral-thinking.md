# Lateral Thinking

**Lateral thinking**, coined by Edward de Bono, is about solving problems through indirect, creative approaches rather than traditional step-by-step logic. Where vertical (logical) thinking digs the same hole deeper, lateral thinking digs a new hole in a different place. It deliberately disrupts established patterns of thought to discover solutions that are invisible from within the conventional frame.

---

Analyze the following problem using **lateral thinking** techniques. Break out of the obvious path.

**Problem / Topic:**
$ARGUMENTS

---

## Step 1: State the Obvious Solution

- What is the **conventional, expected** approach to this problem?
- Why does everyone default to this approach?
- What pattern of thinking does it follow?
- Explicitly acknowledge it — then set it aside. We're looking elsewhere.

## Step 2: Challenge the Problem Definition

- Is this actually the **right problem** to solve?
- What if the problem doesn't exist — what if it's a symptom of something else entirely?
- Restate the problem in at least **3 radically different ways**:
  - From a different stakeholder's perspective
  - As the opposite problem
  - At a different level of abstraction (zoom in or out)
- What problem would you **love** to have instead of this one?

## Step 3: Apply Provocation (Po)

De Bono's "Po" technique — make deliberately absurd statements to escape mental ruts:

- **Reversal**: What if we did the exact opposite? State the reversal and explore where it leads.
- **Exaggeration**: What if we took a key element to an extreme (infinitely large, infinitely small, infinitely fast)?
- **Distortion**: What if we changed the normal sequence or relationship between elements?
- **Wishful thinking**: "Wouldn't it be nice if..." — state something seemingly impossible, then find a stepping stone to reality.
- **Random entry**: Pick a random concrete noun (object, animal, place). Force a connection between it and the problem. What new ideas emerge?

For each provocation, don't judge — follow the idea for at least 3 steps to see where it leads.

## Step 4: Analogies from Distant Domains

- How is this problem solved in **nature**? (Biomimicry)
- How is this problem solved in a **completely unrelated industry**?
- How would a **child** approach this? A **comedian**? An **artist**? A **military strategist**?
- What if this were a problem in a **video game** — what cheat code or exploit would you use?
- Find at least 2 distant analogies and extract transferable principles.

## Step 5: Remove Constraints

- List every constraint you're operating under (time, money, technology, regulations, social norms).
- For each constraint, ask: **"What if this constraint didn't exist?"**
- Which constraints are **real** and which are **self-imposed**?
- What becomes possible when you remove the top 2 constraints?

## Step 6: Shift the Entry Point

- Instead of starting from the beginning, start from:
  - **The end**: What does the solved state look like? Work backward.
  - **The middle**: What's the most interesting or unusual element? Start there.
  - **The user's emotion**: What feeling do we want to create?
  - **The constraint**: What if the biggest limitation became the biggest feature?

## Step 7: Harvest Ideas

- From all the provocations and explorations above, identify:
  - **Unexpected connections** that emerged
  - **Partial ideas** that could be combined
  - **Principles** that could be applied even if the specific provocation was absurd
- Select the **top 3 most creative and promising ideas**
- For each, outline how it could be made practical:
  - What's the kernel of genius in this idea?
  - What would a realistic first version look like?
  - What makes it genuinely better than the conventional approach (not just different)?

---

Lateral thinking is not about being random or silly — it's about using deliberate techniques to escape the gravity of established patterns. The best lateral solutions often seem obvious *in retrospect*, but were invisible from within the conventional frame.
