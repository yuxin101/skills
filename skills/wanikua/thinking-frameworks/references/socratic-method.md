# Socratic Method

**The Socratic Method** is the art of systematic questioning to probe the depth and validity of beliefs, expose contradictions, and guide thinking toward clearer understanding. Named after Socrates, who claimed to know nothing and instead asked questions that revealed others didn't know as much as they thought. It doesn't lecture — it illuminates through inquiry.

---

**Problem / Topic:**
$ARGUMENTS

---

## Round 1: Clarifying Questions — What Do We Actually Mean?

Start by questioning the core concepts and terms.

- What exactly do we mean by [key term]? Define it precisely.
- Can you give a concrete example? And a counter-example?
- Is this concept clear enough that two people would apply it the same way?
- Are there ambiguities or multiple valid interpretations?
- How is this different from [similar concept]?
- What would someone who disagrees say this means?

## Round 2: Probing Assumptions — What Are We Taking for Granted?

Dig into the foundations of the claim or position.

- What are we **assuming** to be true here?
- Why do we believe that assumption? What's the basis?
- What if that assumption were **false** — what changes?
- Is this assumption **universal** or only true in certain contexts?
- Are we confusing **correlation with causation** anywhere?
- Is there a **circular argument** hidden here (assuming what we're trying to prove)?

## Round 3: Probing Evidence and Reasons — How Do We Know?

Challenge the evidence and reasoning.

- What **evidence** supports this position?
- How was that evidence obtained? Is the method reliable?
- Is this evidence **sufficient** to support the conclusion, or merely consistent with it?
- Could the same evidence support a **different conclusion**?
- What evidence would **disprove** this position? Does such evidence exist?
- Are we relying on **anecdote** where we need **data**, or vice versa?
- Is the reasoning **deductive** (guaranteed if premises are true) or **inductive** (probable but not certain)?

## Round 4: Considering Alternative Viewpoints — What Would Others Say?

- What would a **thoughtful critic** say in response?
- What is the **strongest version** of the opposing argument?
- How would someone from a **different background, culture, or discipline** see this?
- If we accepted the opposing view, what would follow?
- Is there a **third position** that transcends both sides?

## Round 5: Probing Implications and Consequences — What Follows?

Trace the claim to its logical conclusions.

- If this is true, what **else** must also be true?
- What are the **practical consequences** of accepting this position?
- Does this lead to any **contradictions** or **absurd conclusions** (reductio ad absurdum)?
- How does this apply to **edge cases** or **extreme situations**?
- If we apply this principle **consistently**, are we comfortable with all the results?
- What **precedent** does this set?

## Round 6: Meta-Questions — Questioning the Question

- Is this even the **right question** to ask?
- **Why** is this question important? What depends on the answer?
- What makes this question **hard**? Where does the difficulty actually lie?
- Are we capable of answering this with current knowledge, or is it fundamentally **uncertain**?
- What **better question** should we be asking instead?

## Synthesis: What Have We Learned?

After the Socratic examination:

- What beliefs **survived** the questioning and are stronger for it?
- What beliefs were **weakened** or **revealed as unjustified**?
- What **new questions** emerged that we hadn't considered before?
- What do we now know that we **don't know** (known unknowns)?
- What is the most **intellectually honest** conclusion we can draw?
- What would Socrates say: did we find **wisdom**, or did we discover **the limits of our knowledge**?

---

The Socratic method is not about winning arguments — it's about discovering truth through disciplined inquiry. The most valuable outcome is often not a better answer, but a better question.
