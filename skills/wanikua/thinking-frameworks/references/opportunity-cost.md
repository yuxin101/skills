# Opportunity Cost Thinking

**Opportunity cost** is the most fundamental concept in economics and one of the most neglected in everyday decision-making. The true cost of any choice is not what you pay — it's **what you give up**. Every dollar, hour, and unit of attention spent on X is a dollar, hour, and unit of attention *not* spent on its best alternative. People instinctively focus on direct costs and benefits while ignoring the invisible cost of the road not taken. This blindness leads to systematically poor resource allocation.

---

Analyze the following decision using **opportunity cost thinking**. Make the invisible costs visible.

**Decision / Topic:**
$ARGUMENTS

---

## Step 1: Define the Decision and Resources at Stake

- What **decision** is being made?
- What **scarce resources** are being allocated? Be exhaustive:
  - 💰 **Money** — How much capital is being committed?
  - ⏰ **Time** — Whose time, how much, and for how long?
  - 🧠 **Attention** — What cognitive bandwidth and management focus does this consume?
  - 👥 **People** — What human talent is being dedicated?
  - 📊 **Data / Information** — What information-gathering capacity is being used?
  - 🔗 **Relationships** — What social/political capital is being spent?
  - 🚪 **Optionality** — What future options are being closed off?
  - 📈 **Reputation** — What reputational risk is being taken?

## Step 2: Identify the Alternatives

*The opportunity cost is defined by the **best** alternative use of the same resources.*

- List **all realistic alternatives** for these resources (including "do nothing / save / wait"):
  1. [Alternative A]: Description
  2. [Alternative B]: Description
  3. [Alternative C]: Description
  4. [Status quo / Do nothing]: Description
  5. [Wait and decide later]: Description
- Don't just list the obvious alternatives — think about **radically different** uses of the same resources.
- Which alternatives are **mutually exclusive** with the proposed decision?

## Step 3: Evaluate Each Alternative

For the proposed decision AND each major alternative, estimate:

| Option | Expected Return | Risk Level | Time to Payoff | Reversibility | Strategic Value |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Proposed decision | | | | | |
| Alternative A | | | | | |
| Alternative B | | | | | |
| Do nothing | | | | | |

- Be honest: Is the proposed decision being compared against **strong alternatives**, or only against obviously bad ones?
- What is the **expected value** of each option? (Probability × outcome)
- What is the **risk-adjusted return**?

## Step 4: Calculate the Opportunity Cost

**Opportunity Cost = Value of the Best Forgone Alternative**

- What is the **single best alternative** to the proposed decision?
- What is its expected value?
- The **true cost** of the proposed decision = direct cost + opportunity cost.
- Does the proposed decision still look good when the opportunity cost is included?

### Concrete Examples:
- "Spending $100K on Project X means not spending $100K on Project Y, which has a projected ROI of 40%. So the true cost of Project X is $100K + $40K = $140K in value."
- "Spending 6 months building Feature A means not building Feature B, which would serve 3x more users."
- "Attending this 2-hour meeting means not spending 2 hours on deep work that could produce [specific deliverable]."

## Step 5: Hidden and Non-Obvious Opportunity Costs

Look for costs people typically miss:

### Time Opportunity Costs
- What could you **learn** in the time this takes?
- What **relationships** could you build?
- What **health or personal** investments are being sacrificed?
- Is this the **right time** for this — or would the same resources be more valuable later?

### Attention Opportunity Costs
- What are you **not thinking about** because this consumes your attention?
- Decision fatigue: does this decision crowd out other important decisions?
- Context-switching cost: does this fragment your focus?

### Optionality Costs
- What **future options** does this decision eliminate?
- Does this create **lock-in** (technical, contractual, strategic)?
- What is the **value of waiting** for more information before committing?

### Compounding Costs
- If the alternative would **compound** over time (investment, skill-building, relationship), the opportunity cost grows exponentially with the time horizon.
- A small difference in return, compounded over years, becomes enormous.

## Step 6: The "Compared to What?" Test

For every claimed benefit of the proposed decision, ask **"Compared to what?"**

- "This will generate $1M in revenue." → Compared to what? What would the same resources generate elsewhere?
- "This will save 20 hours per week." → Compared to what? What would those 20 hours produce in the best alternative use?
- "This will make users happier." → Compared to what? Would a different investment make them even happier?

Nothing has absolute value. Everything has only **relative** value compared to alternatives.

## Step 7: Sunk Cost Check

Before proceeding, verify this isn't a sunk cost trap:

- Are past investments (time, money, emotion) **influencing** this decision?
- **Sunk costs are irrelevant**. Only future costs and benefits matter.
- Reframe: "If I were starting fresh today, with no prior investment, would I make this choice?"
- Would you **restart** this project/investment from scratch if you lost all progress? If no, the sunk cost is influencing you.

## Step 8: Decision

- Given the full opportunity cost analysis, is the proposed decision still the best use of resources?
- If yes, what makes it clearly superior to the best alternative?
- If no, what alternative should be pursued instead?
- What **partial reallocation** might capture value from both the proposal and the best alternative?
- What information would **change** the opportunity cost calculation?

---

The most common decision-making error is not choosing badly among known options — it's **failing to consider the alternatives at all**. Opportunity cost thinking forces you to ask the question that matters most: "Is this the *best* use of these resources, or just *a* use?"
