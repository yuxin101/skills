# Inversion Thinking

**Inversion thinking** flips the problem around. Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", it asks "How would I guarantee failure?" — then avoids those things. Charlie Munger's favorite mental model: "Invert, always invert." Carl Jacobi, the mathematician, said it best: "Man muss immer umkehren" — one must always think in reverse. Many of the hardest problems become clearer when approached backward.

---

Analyze the following problem using **inversion thinking**. Think backward to move forward.

**Problem / Topic:**
$ARGUMENTS

---

## Step 1: State the Goal

- What does **success** look like for this problem?
- What is the desired outcome, stated as clearly and concretely as possible?
- What metrics or criteria define success?

## Step 2: Invert — Engineer Failure

Now **deliberately** try to achieve the opposite. Ask:

**"If I wanted to guarantee failure / make this as bad as possible, what would I do?"**

Generate a thorough list of failure strategies:

- What **decisions** would guarantee the worst outcome?
- What **assumptions**, if wrong, would be catastrophic?
- What **behaviors** would sabotage the effort?
- What **environmental conditions** would doom this to failure?
- What **has caused similar projects/efforts to fail** in the past?
- What **common mistakes** do people make in this domain?
- What would a **perfectly incompetent** person do?
- What **shortcuts** would look tempting but lead to disaster?

Be creative and thorough — the more failure modes you identify, the more you can avoid.

## Step 3: Classify the Failure Modes

Organize the failure modes:

| Failure Mode | Probability | Impact | Reversible? | Currently Protected Against? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ... | High/Med/Low | High/Med/Low | Yes/No | Yes/Partially/No |

- Which failures are **most likely**?
- Which failures are **most catastrophic**?
- Which failures are **both likely AND catastrophic**? (These are priority #1)
- Which failures are **currently unprotected**?

## Step 4: Invert Back — Build the Anti-Failure Plan

For each major failure mode, define the **opposite behavior**:

- Failure mode → Prevention strategy
- Bad assumption → What to verify instead
- Toxic behavior → Healthy alternative
- Environmental risk → Contingency plan

The best strategy often isn't "do the right things" — it's **"systematically avoid doing the wrong things."**

## Step 5: Apply to Specific Sub-Problems

Take the most critical aspect of the problem and apply a focused inversion:

- **Invert the constraint**: What if the thing you think is a limitation is actually an advantage?
- **Invert the sequence**: What if you did the last step first?
- **Invert the stakeholder**: What would make users/customers *hate* this? (Then don't do that.)
- **Invert the metric**: What if you optimized for the opposite metric? What insight does that reveal?
- **Invert the question**: Instead of "Why should we do this?" ask "Why should we NOT do this?"

## Step 6: The Obituary Test

Imagine the project/decision has **already failed catastrophically**. Write its obituary:

> "[Project] was launched on [date] with high hopes. It failed because [reasons]. The warning signs were there: [signs]. In retrospect, the team should have [actions]. The cost of failure was [impact]."

- What story feels most **plausible**?
- What **warning signs** are already visible today?
- What **should have been done** that isn't being done?

## Step 7: Synthesis — Forward Plan Informed by Inversion

Now synthesize a forward-looking plan:

1. **Must-avoid list**: The top 5 things that would cause failure (from Step 2-3)
2. **Guardrails**: Specific protections, checks, or circuit breakers for each
3. **Monitoring**: Early warning signals that indicate you're drifting toward a failure mode
4. **Positive actions**: What the inversion analysis suggests you *should* do (the inverse of the failure modes)

---

Inversion doesn't replace forward thinking — it complements it. The forward approach tells you what to pursue; inversion tells you what to avoid. The wise path is informed by both. As Munger says: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
