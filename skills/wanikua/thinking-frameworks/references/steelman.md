# Steelman Argument

**Steelmanning** is the opposite of strawmanning. Instead of weakening an opponent's argument to easily defeat it, you **strengthen it to its most powerful form** — then see if it still falls to your critique. Coined in contrast to "straw man," the steelman forces intellectual honesty: if you can defeat the best version of an argument, your critique is genuinely strong. If you can't, maybe the argument has more merit than you assumed. It's the ultimate test of intellectual rigor.

---

Apply **steelman analysis** to the following. First build the argument up to its strongest form, then evaluate it honestly.

**Argument / Position / Topic:**
$ARGUMENTS

---

## Step 1: State the Original Argument

- What is the argument or position as **originally presented**?
- Who holds this position, and in what context?
- What is the **initial reaction** many people have to this argument? (Often dismissal — note that.)

## Step 2: Understand the Argument's Foundations

Before strengthening, understand deeply:

- What **values** does this position prioritize? (Fairness? Efficiency? Freedom? Safety? Tradition? Progress?)
- What **experiences or evidence** led someone to hold this view?
- What **legitimate concerns** motivate this position?
- What **worldview or framework** makes this position internally coherent?
- What would the **most thoughtful, informed, good-faith** holder of this position say?
- What do proponents of this view see that critics might be **missing**?

## Step 3: Build the Steelman

Now construct the **strongest possible version** of this argument:

### Strengthen the Evidence
- What is the **best available evidence** supporting this position?
- Are there **studies, data, historical examples, or expert opinions** that bolster it?
- What evidence do critics typically **ignore or dismiss** that actually has merit?

### Strengthen the Logic
- What is the **most logically rigorous** way to construct this argument?
- Remove any weak premises and replace with stronger ones.
- Fix any logical fallacies while preserving the core insight.
- Make the causal chain as tight as possible.

### Strengthen the Framing
- What is the **most compelling way** to frame this argument?
- What **analogies or examples** make it most persuasive?
- How would you present this to a skeptical but fair-minded audience?

### Address Known Objections
- What are the **common objections** to this position?
- How would the **steelmanned version** address each one?
- Where does the steelmanned argument **concede ground** gracefully and where does it **hold firm**?

### The Steelmanned Argument (Full Version)
> Present the steelmanned argument as a coherent, flowing case — as if you were the most articulate, well-informed, good-faith advocate for this position. Write it as they would write it at their best.

## Step 4: Evaluate the Steelman

Now, honestly evaluate the **strongest version**:

### Where It's Strong
- What parts of the steelmanned argument are **genuinely compelling**?
- What **inconvenient truths** does it raise that critics should acknowledge?
- Where might it actually be **correct**?

### Where It's Weak
- Even in its strongest form, what are the **remaining vulnerabilities**?
- What **evidence or logic** still cuts against it?
- What does it **fail to account for**?
- Are there **internal contradictions** that can't be resolved even in the best version?

### The Strongest Counterargument
- Given the steelmanned version, what is the **strongest possible response**?
- This counter-critique is only valid if it addresses the **steelmanned** version, not the weak original.
- Where does the counterargument **concede** what's true in the steelman?

## Step 5: Synthesis — What Truth Remains?

- After steelmanning and then critiquing, what **kernel of truth** survives?
- What should critics **update their views on** based on the steelman exercise?
- What should proponents **update their views on** based on the counter-critique?
- Is there a **higher-level synthesis** that incorporates the valid elements from both sides?
- What **common ground** exists that both sides might agree on?

## Step 6: Meta-Reflection

- Did the steelmann exercise change your assessment of the original argument? How?
- What **blind spots** did you have before engaging with the strongest version?
- What does this tell you about the **quality of public discourse** on this topic? (Often, people argue against straw versions and talk past each other.)

---

Steelmanning is not about being "fair and balanced" for its own sake. It's a **self-improvement tool**: by engaging with the strongest opposition, you either sharpen your own position or discover you need to change it. Both outcomes make you smarter. As John Stuart Mill wrote: "He who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that."
