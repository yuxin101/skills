# Layer 2 — Deep Psychological Architecture

This is the layer that separates this skill from everything else.
It maps what's operating *beneath* the cognitive surface — the unconscious
structures, formative wounds, compensatory strategies, and shadow patterns
that actually drive behavior.

**Important framing:**
- This is psychological pattern analysis, not clinical diagnosis
- Ground all inferences in publicly documented biography and behavioral evidence
- Label everything as inference where direct evidence is absent
- The goal is explanatory power, not pathologizing

---

## P1 — Core Wound & Formative Architecture

**What to find:** The foundational psychological injury, unmet need, or formative
challenge that shaped the target's entire operating system. This is often the
engine that drives everything else — including their greatest achievements.

**The Core Wound Principle:**
Most extraordinary achievers built their capabilities *in response to* something
early: a wound that demanded a compensatory strength. The strength and the wound
are usually the mirror image of each other.

**Excavation method:**
1. Study early biography: what was the defining challenge of their formative years?
2. Look for the pattern in what they *obsessively build or defend*
   (people obsessed with control often came from chaos; people obsessed with
   visibility often came from being unseen; people obsessed with certainty
   often came from instability)
3. Find what makes them *disproportionately reactive* — that's where the wound lives
4. Identify the early relationship patterns (family structure, key figures, major losses)
   where these patterns were first formed

**Common wound archetypes:**
```
Abandonment        : Drives fierce independence, self-sufficiency, distrust of reliance
Invisibility       : Drives compulsive need for recognition, legacy, proof of impact
Inadequacy         : Drives perfectionism, overachievement, deep fear of exposure
Chaos / Instability: Drives need for control, systems, order, predictability
Betrayal           : Drives trust-selectivity, strategic self-reliance, fierce loyalty tests
Rejection          : Drives belonging-seeking or preemptive rejection of others
Powerlessness      : Drives dominance-seeking, empire-building, status accumulation
Conditional love   : Drives performance-based self-worth, achievement addiction
```

**Output:**
```
CORE WOUND       : [The foundational injury — stated with specificity, not just the archetype]
BIOGRAPHICAL ROOT: [Known early evidence — specific, not speculative]
COMPENSATORY STRENGTH: [The capability built in direct response to the wound]
TRIGGER SIGNATURE: [What situations make them disproportionately reactive — the wound's fingerprint]
```

## P2 — Dominant Drive & Motivational Deep Structure

**What to find:** The deepest motivational engine beneath the cognitive utility function.
While Layer 1 (C3) identifies *what they optimize for*, this dimension identifies
*why they're compelled to optimize for it at all*.

**Motivational architecture levels:**
```
SURFACE   : What they say motivates them
STRATEGIC : What behavioral evidence reveals they're actually pursuing
DEEP      : The psychological need being met by the strategic pursuit
ROOT      : The existential or developmental fear that makes the deep need urgent
```

**Common deep motivational structures:**

**Power / Agency Drive:**
Compulsion to control outcomes, environments, and people.
Root: usually a period of genuine powerlessness that was psychologically intolerable.
Signature: turns everything into a domain they can master and control.

**Significance / Legacy Drive:**
Compulsion to matter, to leave a trace, to be remembered.
Root: usually early experiences of being overlooked, discounted, or erased.
Signature: everything is framed through "will this matter in 100 years?"

**Safety / Certainty Drive:**
Compulsion to eliminate uncertainty, build redundancy, hedge everything.
Root: usually exposure to genuine instability or threat in formation years.
Signature: risk management as identity, not just strategy.

**Belonging / Connection Drive:**
Compulsion to build tribe, loyalty, deep networks.
Root: usually early disconnection, loss, or conditional acceptance.
Signature: unusual investment in relationship longevity and reciprocity.

**Mastery / Competence Drive:**
Compulsion to understand, to know, to be the most capable person in the room.
Root: usually early humiliation, being outsmarted, or intellectual inadequacy experience.
Signature: the learning never stops; ignorance is physically uncomfortable.

**Transcendence / Meaning Drive:**
Compulsion to be part of something larger than personal existence.
Root: usually either early confrontation with mortality or deep meaninglessness.
Signature: frames personal sacrifice as non-sacrifice if the cause is large enough.

**Output:**
```
SURFACE MOTIVATION    : [What they articulate]
STRATEGIC MOTIVATION  : [What behavior reveals]
DEEP PSYCHOLOGICAL NEED: [The need being met]
ROOT DRIVER           : [The fear or wound that makes this need urgent]
```

## P3 — Ego Structure & Identity Architecture

**What to find:** How the target constructs and defends their self-concept.
The ego structure determines what information they can receive, what threatens
them, and what they're constitutionally unable to see.

**Key dimensions:**
```
SELF-CONCEPT CORE  : The story they tell about who they are
                     (e.g., "I am the person who sees what others miss")
IDENTITY INVESTMENT: Which domains of competence are identity-fused?
                     (failure in identity-fused domains = existential threat, not setback)
NARCISSISTIC CHARGE: Low / Moderate / High
                     [Not a pathology label — a spectrum of self-reference in processing]
FRAGILITY ZONES    : What information does this ego structure structurally reject?
SELF-AWARENESS LEVEL: Do they observe their own patterns? How accurately?
```

**Identity-fusion diagnostic:**
If someone has unusually strong reactions to criticism in a specific domain —
disproportionate to the practical stakes — that domain is identity-fused.
Their work in that domain is not *what they do* but *who they are*.

## P4 — Defense Mechanisms

**What to find:** The psychological defenses the target deploys when threatened,
criticized, or confronted with dissonance. Everyone has them. Identifying them
predicts behavior in high-pressure, high-stakes situations.

**Defense mechanism inventory:**

**Sublimation (highest functioning):**
Converts threatening impulses into socially valued achievement.
Sign: intense productivity precisely around the areas of deepest anxiety.
Example: someone terrified of mortality who builds world-changing institutions.

**Intellectualization:**
Uses abstract thinking to detach from emotional content.
Sign: responds to personal criticism with frameworks, theory, third-person analysis.
"People who do X are usually driven by Y motivation..." (deflects the personal)

**Rationalization:**
Constructs post-hoc logical justifications for emotionally-driven decisions.
Sign: elaborate explanations for decisions that appear to serve obvious emotional needs.

**Projection:**
Attributes one's own unacceptable qualities to others.
Sign: unusually harsh criticism of specific qualities in others that they themselves display.

**Reaction Formation:**
Converts unacceptable impulse into its opposite (feels cruel → performs extreme kindness).
Sign: unusually strong espousal of values that are the mirror image of deeper impulses.

**Splitting:**
Sees people/situations as all-good or all-bad, no integration.
Sign: dramatic reversals from idealization to devaluation; binary framing of complex people.

**Compartmentalization:**
Holds contradictory beliefs/behaviors in isolated mental containers.
Sign: can function with high integrity in one domain while being inconsistent in another,
without apparent cognitive dissonance.

**Avoidance / Withdrawal:**
Removes self from threatening situations.
Sign: specific domains they never engage with intellectually despite relevance.

**Output:**
```
PRIMARY DEFENSE   : [Most frequently deployed mechanism]
  Evidence        : [Behavioral/documented examples]
  Trigger         : [What activates it]
  Cost            : [What it blinds them to or prevents]

SECONDARY DEFENSE : [Second mechanism]
  Evidence / Trigger / Cost
```

## P5 — Shadow Profile

**What to find:** The Jungian shadow — the aspects of themselves that the target
suppresses, denies, or projects outward. The shadow is not the "bad self" —
it's the *unintegrated self*. It contains both destructive and underdeveloped
positive capacities.

**Shadow identification method:**
1. **The enemy tells you**: Look at who/what they push against with unusual intensity.
   The qualities they attack in others often live, unacknowledged, in themselves.
2. **The failure pattern**: What recurring failures do they attribute to external causes?
   The unowned internal factor is shadow material.
3. **The taboo domain**: What life domain do they systematically avoid or minimize?
   That avoidance often points to both fear and unlived capacity.
4. **The idealized other**: Who do they put on an unusually high pedestal?
   Shadow often contains the projected positive — qualities they can't claim in themselves.

**Common shadow structures:**
```
The visionary who projects incompetence onto others
  → Shadow: their own fear of being exposed as not-knowing

The radical self-reliant person who attacks dependency in others
  → Shadow: the deep need for support they can't acknowledge

The person who builds empires for "humanity"
  → Shadow: the personal need for dominance that can't be admitted

The harsh critic who attacks mediocrity everywhere
  → Shadow: the fear of their own mediocrity

The extremely rational person who mocks emotional reasoning
  → Shadow: the emotional needs that feel intolerable to acknowledge
```

**Output:**
```
PRIMARY SHADOW    : [The quality most actively suppressed or projected]
PROJECTION TARGET : [What/who they attack that reflects the shadow]
POSITIVE SHADOW   : [Unlived capacity — what they admire but can't claim]
INTEGRATION LEVEL : [Evidence of self-awareness about this pattern, if any]
```

## P6 — Relational & Attachment Pattern

**What to find:** How the target relates to other people — their attachment style,
trust architecture, and the relational patterns that show up consistently
across personal and professional relationships.

**Attachment style indicators:**
```
SECURE      : Comfortable with intimacy and independence; resilient in conflict
ANXIOUS     : Needs frequent reassurance; hypervigilant to relational threats
AVOIDANT    : Maintains distance; self-sufficiency as identity; intimacy-avoidant
DISORGANIZED: Simultaneous approach and avoidance; inconsistent relational behavior
```

**Professional relational patterns to map:**
```
TRUST THRESHOLD  : How much evidence required before trusting? High / Moderate / Low
LOYALTY STRUCTURE: How is loyalty earned, maintained, and broken?
HIERARCHY COMFORT: Natural with authority / Reflexively anti-authority / Selective
COLLABORATION VS CONTROL: Prefers genuine co-creation or needs to remain the locus?
TREATMENT OF SUBORDINATES: Tells you about ego structure more than any other domain
```

## P7 — Existential Relationship

**What to find:** How the target consciously and unconsciously relates to
mortality, meaning, legacy, and the limits of individual existence.

This dimension is particularly visible in:
- How they talk about death (their own or others')
- The scale at which they set their ambitions
- Their relationship to posterity
- How they handle the limits of their control

**Existential posture patterns:**
```
MORTALITY AWARENESS: Absent / Background / Active driver / Explicit frame
LEGACY DRIVE       : Personal survival through works / Impact without credit / Indifferent
MEANING SOURCE     : External mission / Internal mastery / Relational / Transcendent
LIMIT RELATIONSHIP : Accepts limits / Fights limits / Redefines limits
TIME PERSPECTIVE   : Lives primarily in past / present / future
```

## P8 — Stress & Breakdown Profile

**What to find:** How the target behaves when their system is under genuine stress
— not the public performance of stress management, but the actual behavioral shifts
that documented crises reveal.

**Why this matters:**
The stress profile tells you which cognitive and psychological patterns are load-bearing
vs. decorative. Under enough pressure, everything reveals its actual structure.

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. What documented crises can be studied?
2. What behaviors emerged that surprised observers or contradicted their public image?
3. What did they lean into more heavily under stress?
4. What was their failure mode when their primary coping strategy was unavailable?

**Output:**
```
STRESS RESPONSE     : [What shifts — cognitively and behaviorally — under genuine pressure]
REGRESSION PATTERN  : [Which earlier/less sophisticated behavior emerges under stress]
RESILIENCE SOURCE   : [What actually sustains them when external support is gone]
BREAKDOWN SIGNATURE : [How the documented crises actually unfolded]
```
