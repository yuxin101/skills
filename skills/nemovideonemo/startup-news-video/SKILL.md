# Startup News Video Maker — Startup and Venture Capital News Videos

The funding announcement press release follows a template so rigid that it could be generated by a Markov chain trained on TechCrunch articles from the past decade: the startup "is thrilled to announce" a funding round led by a firm that "shares our vision," the CEO provides a quote about "accelerating growth" that was actually written by the PR agency, the round size is either impressively large or conspicuously absent (the latter meaning it was embarrassingly small), and the company plans to use the funds for "product development and team expansion" — a description so generic that it applies to literally every company that has ever raised money, leaving the reader with no understanding of what the startup actually does, whether the valuation makes sense, or why this particular funding round matters in a landscape where hundreds of startups raise money every week. Startup news video content cuts through the press-release theater by providing the analysis that raw announcements omit — who funded it and what their track record predicts about the outcome, how the valuation compares to peers at the same stage, what the competitive landscape looks like for the problem the startup claims to solve, and the honest assessment of whether this company is building something meaningful or riding a trend wave that will recede in 18 months. This tool transforms startup developments into polished news and analysis videos — funding-round breakdowns contextualizing the numbers behind the headlines, founder-interview profiles revealing the human story behind the pitch deck, market-map analyses showing where a startup fits in its competitive landscape, startup-failure post-mortems extracting lessons from companies that didn't make it, accelerator and demo-day coverage highlighting emerging companies, and the venture-capital trend reports that explain where the money is flowing and why. Built for startup journalists covering the ecosystem, VC analysts producing deal-flow content, founder communities sharing knowledge and news, accelerator programs promoting portfolio companies, angel investors evaluating opportunities, and anyone whose audience follows the startup world and needs context that press releases systematically withhold.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Funding Round Breakdown — Behind the Numbers
"Create a 4-minute analysis of a major startup funding round. The headline (0-15 sec): '[Startup] raised $120M Series C at a $1.2B valuation.' The numbers on screen: round size, valuation, lead investor, key participants. 'A new unicorn. Let's look behind the number to see if the horn is real.' The company (15-55 sec): what they actually do — in plain language. 'They build AI-powered supply-chain optimization for mid-market manufacturers. In English: they help factories figure out what to order, when to order it, and how much to keep in stock.' The problem they solve: 'A mid-market manufacturer ($50-500M revenue) typically has 15-25% of their capital tied up in inventory. Half of that inventory is either wrong (they'll never sell it) or mistimed (they'll sell it but not for months).' The product: 'Their software connects to the manufacturer's ERP system, analyzes historical data, and predicts demand. They claim to reduce excess inventory by 30-40% within 6 months.' The numbers (55-120 sec): the financial context. Revenue: '$28M ARR, up from $11M a year ago.' Growth: '155% year-over-year.' 'At $1.2B valuation on $28M ARR, that's a 43x revenue multiple.' Put it in context: 'The median SaaS multiple in 2026 is 12x. A 43x multiple implies the market believes this company will maintain hyper-growth for 3+ years.' Net retention: '135% — meaning existing customers spend 35% more each year.' 'A 135% net retention is exceptional. It means the product expands within organizations after initial adoption — departments that didn't originally use it start using it.' The investors (120-175 sec): 'Led by [Fund] — a firm with a specific thesis on vertical AI for manufacturing.' Their track record: '3 exits in manufacturing-tech in the past 5 years, average 8x return.' 'When a specialist firm leads, it signals domain expertise — they understand the market and chose this company after evaluating alternatives.' Participating investors: '[Fund B] (generalist, $2B AUM) and [Fund C] (corporate VC from a major manufacturer).' 'The corporate VC is notable — a major manufacturer investing means they see the product working from the customer side.' The competitive landscape (175-220 sec): market map on screen. 'Three categories of competition.' '1. Legacy ERP vendors adding AI features (slow, expensive, mediocre).' '2. Other AI startups in supply chain (3-4 at similar stage).' '3. In-house data science teams at large manufacturers (expensive, hard to scale).' 'The startup's advantage: purpose-built for mid-market. The ERP vendors target enterprise. The other startups are competing for the same enterprise customers. The mid-market is underserved.' The assessment (220-240 sec): 'Is the valuation justified? At 155% growth and 135% NRR, the metrics are genuinely strong. The 43x multiple is aggressive but defensible if growth sustains for 2 more years.' 'The risk: mid-market customers churn faster than enterprise. The 135% NRR needs to hold as the customer base scales from 200 to 2,000.' 'My take: this is one of the better-justified unicorn valuations I've analyzed this quarter. The specialist lead, the corporate VC participation, and the mid-market positioning all reduce risk. Worth watching.'"

### 2. Startup Post-Mortem — Lessons From Failure
"Build a 5-minute post-mortem analyzing why a well-funded startup failed. Opening (0-15 sec): the company logo. A line through it. 'This company raised $85 million, hired 340 people, had customers in 12 countries, and shut down last month. Here's what went wrong — and what every founder can learn from it.' The rise (15-70 sec): the timeline — animated. '2021: founded by two ex-[Big Tech] engineers. The idea: AI-powered recruitment that eliminates bias from hiring.' '2022: $8M seed round. The product worked in demos. Impressive accuracy on bias detection.' '2023: $27M Series A. 40 customers, mostly mid-market tech companies.' '2024: $50M Series B at $400M valuation. 200 customers. 180 employees. The press called them "the future of hiring."' 'At this point, everything looked right: growing revenue, growing team, growing press coverage.' The cracks (70-160 sec): 'Three problems were growing faster than the revenue.' 'Problem 1 — Unit economics. The product required extensive customization for each client. Average implementation: 3 months, $180K in engineering time. Average annual contract: $95K.' Show the math: '$180K to acquire, $95K per year in revenue. Payback period: 23 months — and the average customer churned at 18 months.' 'They were spending more to get each customer than the customer was worth. The faster they grew, the more money they lost.' 'Problem 2 — The bias promise. The core marketing claim: "eliminate hiring bias." Customers expected zero bias. The product reduced measurable bias by 40-60%. That's significant — but "reduced" and "eliminated" are different words, and the marketing used "eliminated."' 'When a customer's hiring was challenged legally, the AI-reduced-but-not-eliminated bias became a liability rather than a defense.' 'Problem 3 — The team scaling trap. From 40 employees to 340 in 18 months. The culture that built the product couldn't absorb 300 new people. Knowledge became siloed. Decision-making slowed. The founders spent more time managing than building.' The end (160-220 sec): '2025: revenue stalled at $18M ARR. Churn accelerated to 35% annually. The Series C fundraise failed — three firms passed after due diligence revealed the unit economics.' 'The board pushed for cuts. 200 employees laid off. The remaining 140 tried to rebuild.' 'Three months later, two anchor clients left simultaneously. Revenue dropped below the point of viability. The company shut down.' The lessons (220-280 sec): 'Lesson 1: Unit economics are not a later problem. If it costs more to acquire a customer than that customer is worth, growth is a treadmill running in reverse.' 'Lesson 2: Never promise "eliminate" when you deliver "reduce." Overmarketing creates expectation gaps that erode trust.' 'Lesson 3: Headcount is not a growth metric. Adding 300 people in 18 months without the infrastructure to integrate them creates organizational debt that compounds faster than revenue.' 'Lesson 4: The Series B valuation trap. A $400M valuation on $12M ARR set expectations that required perfect execution. When execution stumbled, the gap between valuation and reality killed the fundraise.' Close (280-300 sec): '$85 million. 340 people. A genuine technology that reduced hiring bias by 40-60%. Gone — not because the product didn't work, but because the business around the product didn't work.' 'The obituary isn't the technology. It's the unit economics, the over-marketing, and the scaling speed. Three fixable problems that went unfixed until it was too late.'"

### 3. Market Map — Where the Money Is Flowing
"Produce a 5-minute venture capital trend report for the current quarter. Opening (0-10 sec): 'Where is venture capital flowing in Q1 2026? The data tells a story that's different from the headlines.' The macro view (10-55 sec): total VC investment this quarter. '$48.2 billion globally — up 12% from Q4 2025.' 'But the distribution is revealing.' Animated chart: '42% went to AI companies. 18% to healthcare. 12% to fintech. The remaining 28% split across everything else.' 'AI is not a sector — it's the sector. Nearly half of all venture dollars went to companies with AI in their pitch deck. But within AI, the money moved.' Show the shift: 'Q1 2025: 60% of AI funding went to foundation-model companies. Q1 2026: 70% goes to application-layer companies.' 'The infrastructure bet is maturing. The application bet is beginning.' Sector deep-dive 1 — AI applications (55-130 sec): 'The three hottest AI application categories this quarter.' '1. AI for healthcare ($4.2B): drug discovery, diagnostic imaging, clinical-trial optimization. The regulatory path is clearer than it was 2 years ago and the results are becoming undeniable.' Highlight round: '[Company]: $380M Series D for AI-powered drug discovery. Their first AI-discovered drug entered Phase 2 trials.' '2. AI for legal ($1.8B): contract analysis, case research, compliance automation. Law firms are adopting faster than anyone expected.' '3. AI for education ($1.1B): personalized tutoring, content generation, assessment automation.' Sector deep-dive 2 — Climate tech (130-195 sec): '$6.8 billion this quarter — a record.' 'The surprise: it's not solar and wind anymore. Those are mature. The money is going to:' '1. Carbon capture ($2.1B) — industrial-scale CO2 removal.' '2. Grid-scale storage ($1.8B) — batteries that store renewable energy for days, not hours.' '3. Industrial decarbonization ($1.4B) — reducing emissions from cement, steel, and chemical manufacturing.' 'Climate tech has evolved from "nice to have" to "regulatory mandate" in multiple markets.' The warning signals (195-250 sec): 'Two trends to watch carefully.' '1. Pre-revenue valuations are inflating again. The median seed valuation rose 30% this quarter. Companies with no revenue are raising at $20-30M valuations.' 'We saw this in 2021. It ended badly. The froth indicators are returning.' '2. Tourist investors. Hedge funds and family offices that entered VC during the boom, left during the correction, and are returning now. Tourist capital chases trends and exits at the first downturn.' The outlook (250-290 sec): 'My read on the next two quarters:' 'AI application companies with real revenue will continue to raise easily.' 'AI infrastructure companies will face harder fundraises as the market consolidates.' 'Climate tech will sustain momentum due to regulatory tailwinds.' 'Consumer and social startups remain in the funding desert.' Close (290-300 sec): '$48.2 billion in one quarter. The money is speaking clearly: AI applications, climate technology, and healthcare. Everything else is competing for attention and losing.' End card."

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Contextualize valuations with multiples** — "43x revenue" means more than "$1.2B." The AI calculates and displays comparative multiples.
2. **Analyze investors, not just the round** — A specialist lead signals different confidence than a generalist. The AI profiles lead investors.
3. **Show unit economics honestly** — "$180K to acquire, $95K annual contract" is the real story. The AI structures unit-economics analysis.
4. **Extract transferable lessons from failures** — "Unit economics are not a later problem" serves every founder watching. The AI distills universal lessons.
5. **Use market maps for competitive context** — Where a startup sits relative to alternatives determines its viability. The AI renders positioning maps when competitive_map is enabled.
