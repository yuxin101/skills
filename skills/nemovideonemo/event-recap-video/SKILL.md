# Event Recap Video Maker — Event Highlight and Summary Videos

The event was three days long, had 47 sessions, 12 keynote speakers, a networking reception that lasted four hours, and the single most important moment — the one that will define whether anyone remembers this event next year — was a 90-second exchange during a panel Q&A that nobody thought to film because the official cameras were pointed at the stage and the moment happened in the audience. Event recap videos solve the fundamental problem of live experiences: they happen once, they happen to everyone differently depending on which sessions they attended and which conversations they had in the hallway, and without video documentation, the collective memory of a 2,000-person conference reduces to a handful of LinkedIn posts and a branded tote bag within approximately three weeks. This tool transforms raw event footage into polished recap videos — opening-day energy captures with crowd arrivals and venue reveals, keynote highlight compilations with the quotable moments extracted and titled, attendee-testimonial montages showing genuine reactions, sponsor-integration segments that acknowledge partners without feeling like commercials, networking-moment compilations proving that the hallway conversations were as valuable as the stage presentations, and the closing-ceremony emotional crescendo that makes everyone who attended feel proud and everyone who didn't feel like they need to attend next year. Built for event production companies delivering post-event content, conference organizers marketing next year's event, corporate teams recapping internal summits, nonprofit organizations documenting fundraising galas, music festival creators building anticipation for next season, and anyone responsible for proving that an event was worth the investment of time, money, and attention.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Conference Recap — 3-Day Tech Summit
"Create a 5-minute recap of a 3-day tech conference. Opening (0-20 sec): time-lapse of the venue setup — empty ballroom to fully staged conference in 30 seconds. Badge printing, banner unfurling, chairs arranging, screens testing. The first attendee walks through the doors. Title card: event name, dates, location, attendee count. Day 1 — Energy (20-120 sec): the registration rush — badge scanning, swag bags, the first coffee of the conference (the most important logistical element). Keynote 1: the opening speaker — 3-4 quotable moments pulled from a 45-minute talk, each shown as a clip with the quote as a text overlay. 'The speaker said 47 things but these 4 are what people tweeted.' Breakout sessions: rapid cuts between different rooms — the AI panel, the startup pitch session, the hands-on workshop. Show the variety without lingering — the recap should convey breadth. Hallway conversations: 'Half the value of a conference happens between sessions.' Show the genuine networking — two people at a coffee station, a group around a demo booth, the impromptu whiteboard session. Day 2 — Depth (120-220 sec): the day when the content gets serious. Keynote 2 highlights — the controversial take that made the room murmur. Panel discussion: the exchange between two speakers who disagreed publicly and respectfully. 'This 90-second exchange was the intellectual highlight of the conference.' Show it edited tightly. The expo floor: booths, demos, the product launch that drew a crowd. Attendee testimonials — 4 people, each 8-10 seconds: 'I came for the keynotes, I'm staying for the community.' 'This is the third year. Each year the quality goes up.' 'I met my cofounder at this event last year.' The evening event: the reception — different energy. Music, drinks, the venue transformed. 'The networking reception is where the business cards turn into relationships.' Day 3 — Closing (220-280 sec): the final keynote — the emotional one. The moment the speaker's voice caught. The standing ovation. The announcement: next year's dates and location — the crowd reaction. Awards or recognition segment — the winner's face, the handshake, the applause. Closing montage (280-300 sec): rapid-fire — every handshake, every laugh, every lightbulb moment captured across three days. The venue emptying — chairs being stacked, badges in recycling bins, the banner coming down. 'Three days. [X] attendees. [Y] sessions. [Z] conversations that will become partnerships, companies, and friendships. See you next year.' End card: event logo, date, and 'Early bird tickets: [URL]'."

### 2. Fundraising Gala — Nonprofit Event
"Build a 4-minute gala recap for donor communication. Opening: the venue at golden hour — chandeliers, table settings, centerpieces. 'Tonight isn't about dinner. It's about what dinner makes possible.' Guest arrivals (0-40 sec): the red carpet (or the event equivalent) — guests arriving, dressed up, the energy of an evening with purpose. Show the volunteers — 'Behind every gala are the people who set the tables, printed the programs, and rehearsed the run of show six times.' The cause (40-80 sec): a 40-second mini-documentary within the recap — the organization's mission, the people it serves, the specific program this gala funds. Real footage of the program in action. A beneficiary's face. 'This is who tonight is for.' Dinner and program (80-160 sec): the dinner service — 'Not shown: the kitchen producing 400 identical plates in 12 minutes.' The program: the executive director's speech — the key statistics, the emotional story, the ask. Show the room listening — the faces that shift from polite attention to genuine engagement. The live auction: the auctioneer's energy, the paddles going up, the competitive bidding on the big-ticket item. Show the winning bid — the number, the reaction. 'That single bid funds [specific program outcome].' The fund-a-need segment: the graduated asks — $10,000 paddles, $5,000, $1,000, $500. Show the paddles rising at each level. The running total on the screen climbing. The moment it hits the goal — the room erupts. Entertainment and dancing (160-210 sec): the band or performer — the moment the dance floor opened. Show the donor who was in a three-piece suit during dinner now dancing with complete abandon. 'The formality lasted through dessert. The real event started at 9 PM.' Impact closing (210-240 sec): the total raised — animated number climbing to the final figure. 'Tonight raised $[amount]. Here's what that means:' — specific outcome graphics. '$X = meals for Y families.' '$X = scholarships for Y students.' End on the beneficiaries again — the faces the money serves. 'Thank you. From everyone helped by your generosity tonight — thank you.' End card: organization logo, total raised, next year's gala date."

### 3. Music Festival — Weekend Highlights
"Produce a 3-minute music festival recap. Opening: the gates opening — the crowd streaming in, the energy of thousands of people walking toward the same experience. Drone shot: the festival grounds from above — stages, tents, food vendors, the organized chaos of a temporary city. 'Welcome to [festival name]. Three days. Four stages. Eighty-two artists. One shared heartbeat.' Day 1 highlights (0-60 sec): the opening act — the energy of the first note hitting the crowd. Show the crowd from the artist's perspective and the artist from the crowd's perspective. Quick cuts between stages — the indie act on the small stage, the DJ building a set on the electronic stage, the headliner's production revealing itself as the sun sets. 'Day 1 is about discovery. The band you've never heard of at 2 PM becomes your favorite by 4 PM.' Day 2 — the peak (60-130 sec): the crowd has settled in — the outfits are more creative, the groups have merged, strangers are now friends. Show the festival culture: the art installations, the craft vendors, the food truck line that's always long because it's always worth it. The headliner: the production — lights, lasers, pyro. The crowd — 40,000 people singing the same lyric. Slow-motion of the crowd during the chorus — hands up, faces lit by stage lights, the collective joy. 'This is the moment you can't replicate at home. This is why people stand in a field.' Day 3 — the emotional close (130-160 sec): the tiredness is visible but the energy persists. The closing headliner — the career-defining performance. The final song: the crowd knows it's ending. Lighters and phone flashlights. The artist acknowledging the moment. The last note. The silence before the cheering begins. Festival life (160-180 sec): rapid montage — the campsite mornings, the sunset from the hill, the body paint, the inflatable dinosaurs, the proposal (there's always a proposal), the lost and found tent, the cleanup crew who makes it all possible. 'A festival is a temporary world where the social contract is kindness, the currency is vibes, and the only schedule that matters is the set times.' End: drone pulling back from the grounds as the lights turn off, one stage at a time. '[Festival name] 2026. See you next year. Tickets on sale [date].'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Extract quotable moments from long speeches** — The 4 tweetable lines from a 45-minute keynote are the recap's backbone. The AI identifies and titles quotable moments when speaker_labels is enabled.
2. **Include hallway and networking moments** — Staged presentations are expected; genuine connections are compelling. The AI intercuts formal sessions with informal networking footage.
3. **Show the venue transformation** — Empty to full to empty tells the complete story. The AI bookends recaps with setup and teardown time-lapses.
4. **Use attendee testimonials as social proof** — 8-10 seconds each, diverse voices, genuine reactions. The AI formats testimonial segments with speaker identification.
5. **End with the next event CTA** — The recap should convert nostalgia into tickets. The AI places registration information in the closing frames.
