# Football Coaching Video Maker — Soccer Training and Tactics Videos

The coach drew a formation on the whiteboard that looked like a constellation, said "press high and transition quickly," and eleven players ran onto the pitch with eleven different interpretations of what that meant — because tactical instruction without visual demonstration is how you get a left back playing as a second striker and a center mid standing where the goalkeeper should be. ## Example Prompts

### 1. Tactical Analysis — High Press Explained
"Create a 7-minute video explaining the high press with professional match examples. Opening (0-15 sec): match clip — a team wins the ball in the opponent's third and scores within 4 seconds. 'That goal started with a press. Not running at the ball — a coordinated system where every player cuts a passing lane simultaneously. Here's how it works.' The trigger (15-80 sec): animated pitch diagram — 4-3-3 formation. 'The press doesn't start with the striker. It starts with a trigger — a cue that tells the entire team to press simultaneously.' Show triggers: goalkeeper passes to the center-back (trigger: the ball is now in a predictable location), the center-back's first touch is heavy (trigger: they need an extra second), the center-back turns toward the touchline (trigger: they've reduced their own passing options). 'Without a trigger, pressing is just running. With a trigger, pressing is a trap.' The shape (80-200 sec): animated movement. 'The striker doesn't press alone. Watch the chain reaction.' Striker curves run to cut the pass back to the goalkeeper → pressing midfielder sprints to close the center-back → winger on the ball side closes the fullback → opposite winger tucks in to cut the switch → the back line pushes up to compress space. Show the entire team moving as one unit — the pitch shrinks from 105m to effectively 40m of usable space. 'The press works because there's nowhere to play. Every passing lane is covered simultaneously. The player on the ball has 2 seconds before they're closed down and 0 safe passes.' Match examples (200-340 sec): three real-game situations with telestration. Example 1: successful press → turnover → shot within 6 seconds. Draw the passing lanes that were cut, the pressing run that forced the error, the transition. Example 2: press beaten by a long ball over the top. 'This is the risk. If the opponent plays long over the press, your defense is high and exposed. The press only works if the back line is brave enough to hold the line.' Show the defensive line and the space behind it. Example 3: press recycled — first wave doesn't win the ball, team resets shape, presses again on the next trigger. 'You don't press once. You press in waves. The second wave is often more effective because the opponent thinks they've escaped.' Counter-pressing (340-400 sec): 'Gegenpressing — pressing immediately after losing the ball.' Animated: your team has the ball, loses it, the closest 3-4 players immediately swarm the new ball carrier. 'The idea: the opponent is disorganized in the first 5 seconds after winning the ball. Their shape hasn't set. Press them before they can transition.' Show: Klopp-era Liverpool winning the ball, losing it, winning it back within 6 seconds. Closing: the opening goal replayed — now with the press annotated. 'One trigger. Six coordinated runs. Two seconds of pressure. One turnover. One goal. That's not chaos — it's choreography.'"

### 2. Skill Tutorial — First Touch Under Pressure
"Build a 5-minute skill tutorial on receiving the ball under pressure. Opening: slow-motion of a professional player receiving a 40-yard pass with a defender on their back, killing the ball dead with one touch, and turning into space. 'That touch decided the next 10 seconds of play. A bad first touch here and you lose the ball. A good one and you've eliminated a defender without dribbling past them.' The technique (0-80 sec): body-position overlay on slow-motion footage. 'Open your body before the ball arrives. Most players face the ball — that means after receiving, they need to turn. If you open your hips 45 degrees before the ball arrives, the first touch IS the turn.' Show: closed body (facing the ball, two touches needed to turn) vs open body (angled, one touch to receive and face forward). 'The decision happens before the ball arrives. Scan — check your shoulder, see the defender's position, decide which way to turn — THEN receive.' The cushion: 'Don't stop the ball. Redirect it. The foot moves with the ball on contact, absorbing the pace and pushing it into the space you've already identified.' Show in slow-motion: foot meeting ball, slight withdrawal on contact, ball rolling into the next stride. Drills (80-240 sec): three progressive drills with cone layouts animated. Drill 1 — Wall pass and receive: 'Pass against a wall. As the ball returns, open your body and redirect it to the left or right. Alternate sides.' Show the cone setup: player 5 meters from wall, two cones 3 meters apart marking the redirection targets. '50 reps each side. The goal: one touch, ball ends up within 1 meter of the target cone.' Drill 2 — Partner pressure: 'Partner feeds the ball from 10 meters. A passive defender stands behind you. Receive, turn away from the defender, play a pass to a target.' Show the setup: feeder, receiver, passive defender, target player. 'Progress: passive → semi-active (defender jockeys but doesn't tackle) → full pressure.' Drill 3 — Rondo application: 'Four attackers, one defender in a 5×5 grid. Every touch must be first-time or a controlled receive-and-turn. If the defender wins the ball, the player who lost it becomes the defender.' Show the grid layout. 'This drill forces scanning, body positioning, and touch quality under real pressure.' Game context (240-300 sec): match clip — a midfielder receiving between the lines (between opponent's midfield and defense). 'This is the highest-pressure receive in football. You have a defender behind you, a midfielder closing from behind, and maybe 1.5 seconds.' Show the scan (head check over shoulder), the body opening, the first touch into space, the forward pass. 'The technique is the same at every level. The time you have to execute it decreases as you go up.' Closing: the opening touch replayed — 'One touch. The defender is eliminated. The attack is launched. And it all started with checking over the shoulder before the ball arrived.'"

### 3. Session Plan — Complete Training Session for U-14
"Produce a 6-minute video showing a full 75-minute training session for under-14 players focused on building from the back. Opening: the session card on screen — Warm-up (10 min), Technical (15 min), Possession (15 min), Game-related (20 min), Scrimmage (15 min). 'Theme: building out from the back. Every activity connects to the same game moment — receiving the ball under pressure and playing forward.' Warm-up (0-60 sec): dynamic stretches with ball — dribble in a grid, coach calls a number, players form groups of that number and pass between themselves. 'Warm-up should include a ball and decision-making. Running laps teaches nothing about football.' Show the grid setup: 20×20 meters, 16 players. Technical (60-140 sec): passing pattern animated on pitch diagram. Triangle passing: player A passes to B, B sets back to A, A plays forward to C, rotate positions. 'Focus: the weight of the set-back pass and the first touch of the forward receiver.' Show the pattern from above, then from pitch level showing body positioning. 'Coaching point: B's body is open before receiving — they already know A is behind them because they scanned before the ball arrived.' Possession (140-220 sec): 4v4+3 (4 attackers vs 4 defenders with 3 neutral players who always play with the team in possession). Grid: 25×25 meters. 'The neutral players create overloads, simulating the numerical advantages a team has when building from the back.' Show the setup. 'Coaching point: the first pass after winning the ball should go to a neutral player — simulating the goalkeeper or center-back who has time.' Game-related (220-320 sec): 7v7 with directional play on a half-pitch. One team builds from the goalkeeper through the thirds to score. The other team presses. 'This is the session's key activity — it replicates the game moment in a realistic shape.' Show the setup: GK+3 defenders vs 3 pressing attackers, with midfielders joining as the ball progresses. 'Freeze play when the build-up succeeds or fails. Show the players where the space was. Use the telestration to draw the passing lane they missed.' Scrimmage (320-360 sec): free play, 8v8 on a small-sided pitch. 'The coach observes whether the session's coaching points appear in free play. If the center-back receives and plays forward under pressure instead of going long — the session worked.' Closing: the session card again with coaching points summarized — 'Scan before receiving. Open body position. First pass forward when possible. Seventy-five minutes, one theme, four connected activities. That's how a training session tells a story.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show the trigger before the movement** — "The center-back's touch is heavy" → then the press activates. Cause before effect makes tactical concepts learnable. The AI sequences trigger identification before coordinated-movement animations.
2. **Animate full-team shape, not just the ball carrier** — The press works because all 10 outfield players move simultaneously. The AI renders all player positions in pitch-diagram animations when you describe team-wide movements.
3. **Use telestration on real match footage** — Drawing passing lanes and pressing runs over actual game footage is more convincing than diagrams alone. The AI layers telestration overlays when you describe specific match moments.
4. **Progress drills from isolated to game-realistic** — Wall pass → partner pressure → rondo → small-sided game follows the pedagogical progression. The AI sequences drill demonstrations in increasing complexity when you describe progressive drills.
5. **Include the failure example** — "Press beaten by a long ball" teaches when NOT to press. The AI includes counter-examples with tactical annotations when you describe how a tactic can be defeated.
