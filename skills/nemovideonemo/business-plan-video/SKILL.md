# Business Plan Video Maker — Business Strategy and Plan Videos

The business plan is a 42-page document that the founder spent three months writing, the advisor spent forty minutes reading, and the investor spent exactly zero minutes reading because investors don't read business plans — they watch pitch videos, listen to founders talk, and skim executive summaries, a reality so well-documented that the entire Y Combinator application process deliberately avoids asking for a traditional business plan in favor of short written answers and a one-minute video, because the people who evaluate businesses for a living figured out decades ago that the ability to communicate a business clearly in minutes matters more than the ability to document it exhaustively in pages. Business plan video content exists because the plan itself is a thinking tool for the founder and a communication tool for everyone else, and communication in 2026 means video: a 5-minute visual walkthrough of a business model is absorbed faster, shared more easily, and remembered longer than a PDF that requires the reader to care enough to open it, scroll past the table of contents, and maintain attention through sections titled "Market Analysis" that contain the same TAM/SAM/SOM framework every business plan has contained since the concept was invented. This tool transforms business plans into polished strategy videos — executive-summary openings that communicate the entire business in 60 seconds, market-opportunity visualizations that make the size and trajectory of the opportunity tangible, business-model breakdowns that show how money flows from customer to company, competitive-landscape maps positioning the company against alternatives, financial-projection animations that make 5-year forecasts comprehensible rather than aspirational, and the go-to-market strategy sequences that prove the founder knows how to acquire the first 100 customers, not just the theoretical millionth one. Built for startup founders communicating their vision to investors and partners, small-business owners applying for loans and grants, MBA students presenting capstone projects, corporate intrapreneurs pitching internal ventures to leadership, franchise applicants demonstrating business understanding, and anyone whose business plan needs to reach someone who will never read 42 pages but will watch a 5-minute video.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Full Business Plan — 5-Minute Strategic Overview
"Create a 5-minute business plan video for a direct-to-consumer sustainable clothing brand. Executive summary (0-40 sec): the entire business in 40 seconds. 'We make workwear from recycled ocean plastic. The clothes look better than fast fashion, last 3x longer, and cost 20% more — a premium our customers pay gladly because they can scan a QR code on every garment and see exactly which beach the plastic came from, which facility processed it, and which worker sewed it.' Animated: ocean plastic → processed fiber → finished garment → QR code scan showing supply chain. 'The executive summary is the trailer for the business plan. If the viewer stops here, they understand the company.' The problem and opportunity (40-100 sec): the market context. Fast fashion produces 92 million tons of waste annually. Consumer sentiment shift: 73% of millennials willing to pay more for sustainable products (cite source). But sustainable fashion has a perception problem: it looks like burlap sacks and hemp sandals. 'The gap: consumers want sustainable clothing that looks like regular clothing. The brands that close this gap capture the $8.25 billion sustainable fashion market.' Animated market graph: sustainable fashion market trajectory from $6.5B (2024) to projected $15B (2030). The business model (100-160 sec): how the company makes money — visual flowchart. Revenue streams: direct-to-consumer website (primary, 70%), wholesale partnerships with specialty retailers (25%), corporate uniform contracts (5%). Unit economics animated: average garment cost to produce: $18. Average selling price: $65. Gross margin: 72%. Customer acquisition cost: $22. Customer lifetime value: $340 (average customer buys 5.2 items over 3 years). LTV/CAC ratio: 15.5x. 'The unit economics work because the product creates repeat customers. Someone who buys one pair of recycled-ocean-plastic pants and gets compliments on them buys the shirt next.' Competitive landscape (160-210 sec): positioning map — two axes. X-axis: price (affordable → premium). Y-axis: sustainability (greenwashing → fully transparent supply chain). Plot competitors: fast fashion brands (cheap, not sustainable), luxury sustainable brands (expensive, sustainable but exclusive), greenwash brands (cheap, pretend-sustainable). Our position: mid-premium, fully transparent. 'The competitive map shows white space. Nobody occupies the intersection of genuinely sustainable and accessible price point with full supply chain transparency.' Go-to-market strategy (210-260 sec): the first 1,000 customers. Phase 1 (months 1-3): Instagram and TikTok content showing the ocean-plastic-to-clothing transformation — the visual story is inherently shareable. Micro-influencer partnerships with 10 sustainability creators, each given a custom collection. Phase 2 (months 4-6): launch QR supply chain feature, create user-generated content campaign where customers share their garment's ocean story. Phase 3 (months 7-12): wholesale partnerships with 5 specialty retailers, corporate pilot with 2 companies. 'The go-to-market is sequential and specific. Each phase builds on the previous one.' Financial projections (260-300 sec): 5-year animated chart. Year 1: $320K revenue, -$180K net (investment phase). Year 2: $1.2M revenue, $80K net (break-even). Year 3: $3.8M, $420K net. Year 4: $8.5M, $1.4M net. Year 5: $15M, $3.2M net. Key assumptions stated: 40% YoY customer growth, 72% gross margin maintained, CAC stable at $22. 'The projections are aggressive but grounded. Each assumption is testable and the model breaks if any two assumptions fail simultaneously — that's the honest stress test.'"

### 2. Lean Business Model — 3-Minute Canvas Walkthrough
"Build a 3-minute video walking through a Business Model Canvas for a B2B SaaS analytics platform. Opening: the blank canvas — nine boxes on screen. 'Every business can be explained in nine boxes. Here's ours.' Customer Segments (0-20 sec): the box fills in. 'Mid-market e-commerce companies, 50 to 500 employees, doing $10M to $100M in annual revenue. They've outgrown Google Analytics but can't afford or implement enterprise solutions like Adobe Analytics.' Value Proposition (20-45 sec): 'Enterprise-grade analytics at mid-market prices with mid-market implementation time. Full analytics suite deployed in 2 days, not 6 months.' The pain: 'These companies are making million-dollar decisions based on Google Analytics data that was designed for blogs.' Channels (45-60 sec): 'Content marketing (SEO-driven blog targeting "Google Analytics alternatives for e-commerce"), LinkedIn ads targeting e-commerce VPs, partnership with Shopify and BigCommerce as a recommended analytics app.' Revenue Streams (60-80 sec): 'SaaS subscription: $499/month (Growth), $999/month (Pro), $2,499/month (Enterprise). Average contract: $1,100/month. Annual contracts with 15% discount to improve retention.' Key Activities (80-100 sec): 'Product development (50% of team), customer success (30%), sales and marketing (20%). The product IS the business — everything else enables it.' Key Resources (100-115 sec): 'Engineering team (8 people), proprietary data pipeline (processes 2B events/day), customer success playbooks that reduce churn from industry average 8% to our 3.2%.' Key Partners (115-130 sec): 'AWS for infrastructure, Shopify/BigCommerce for distribution, three implementation partners for enterprise onboarding.' Cost Structure (130-155 sec): animated breakdown. Engineering: $1.2M/year. Infrastructure: $180K/year. Sales & marketing: $400K/year. G&A: $220K/year. Total: $2M/year. Breakeven: 152 customers at average contract. Current: 89 customers. Gap: 63 customers at current growth rate = 7 months. Close (155-180 sec): the completed canvas on screen — all nine boxes filled, the business visible as a system. 'Nine boxes. The entire business. Every assumption testable, every box improvable.'"

### 3. Financial Projections — Investor-Ready Number Walk
"Produce a 4-minute financial projections video showing a 5-year forecast with sensitivity analysis. Opening: the headline number. 'In 5 years, this business generates $28M in revenue with 22% net margin. Here's every assumption behind that number and what happens when they're wrong.' Revenue model (0-60 sec): build the revenue formula visually. Revenue = Customers × Average Revenue Per Customer × 12 months. Year 1 customers: 120. ARPU: $850/month. Revenue: $1.22M. Show each year's customer growth: 120 → 280 → 580 → 1,100 → 1,900. ARPU grows from $850 to $1,200 as product matures and enterprise tier adoption increases. Revenue line builds year by year: $1.22M → $3.36M → $8.35M → $16.5M → $28.1M. 'The growth rate decreases each year: 133% → 107% → 88% → 70%. We're modeling deceleration, not hockey-stick fantasy.' Cost structure (60-120 sec): animated waterfall chart. Cost categories stack up: COGS (hosting + infrastructure) scales at 15% of revenue. Engineering grows from $800K to $3.2M (headcount from 6 to 22). Sales & marketing: 30% of revenue in years 1-2, declining to 20% by year 5. Customer success: $120K per CSM, scaling with customer count (1 CSM per 80 customers). G&A: 10% of revenue, declining to 7% at scale. 'Every cost category has a scaling rule. Nothing is a round number pulled from optimism.' Profitability timeline (120-170 sec): the margin chart. Year 1: -35% net margin (investment). Year 2: -8% (approaching breakeven). Year 3: +10% (profitable). Year 4: +17%. Year 5: +22%. 'Breakeven happens in month 26. That's 26 months of burn. Total capital required: $4.2M. That's the real raise number — not the revenue projection, not the TAM, the burn-to-breakeven number.' Sensitivity analysis (170-240 sec): three scenarios animated. Base case: as above. Bull case: 20% faster customer acquisition, ARPU $100 higher → Year 5: $38M revenue, 26% margin. Bear case: 20% slower acquisition, 2% higher churn → Year 5: $18M revenue, 14% margin. 'The bear case is still a profitable business. That's the point of sensitivity analysis — not to scare investors, but to show that the business works even when assumptions fail.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Open with the executive summary — the entire business in 60 seconds** — If the viewer watches nothing else, they understand the company. The AI front-loads the core value proposition.
2. **Animate financial projections** — Numbers that build on screen are absorbed faster than static tables. The AI renders year-by-year progressions when financial_animations is enabled.
3. **State your assumptions explicitly** — "40% YoY customer growth" is testable. "We'll grow fast" is not. The AI labels assumptions alongside projections.
4. **Show competitive positioning visually** — A 2×2 map communicates market position faster than paragraphs. The AI generates positioning maps from competitor descriptions.
5. **Include sensitivity analysis for investor audiences** — Bull/base/bear scenarios show you've stress-tested the model. The AI renders scenario comparisons when sensitivity_analysis is enabled.
