# Stock Market Video Maker — Stock Analysis and Trading Education Videos

The brokerage app made it so easy to buy stocks that a person who couldn't explain what a P/E ratio means now owns seven individual stocks, three of which were purchased because the ticker symbol was memorable and one because a coworker said "it's going to the moon" in the break room — a phrase that has preceded more portfolio losses than any single piece of financial advice in history. Stock market content on YouTube ranges from rigorous fundamental analysis that could pass as a CFA study session to charts covered in so many colored lines they look like a toddler's art project, presented by someone in a rented Lamborghini saying "This pattern never fails" about a pattern that fails regularly. The creators who build lasting audiences are the ones who show their methodology transparently, display their losing trades alongside the winners, and explain that "past performance does not indicate future results" isn't just a legal disclaimer — it's the most important sentence in finance. This tool transforms market analysis, stock research, and trading concepts into polished education videos — animated candlestick charts with labeled patterns and volume profiles, fundamental-analysis breakdowns with revenue and earnings data visualized as growing bar charts, sector-rotation diagrams showing where money flows during economic cycles, portfolio-construction frameworks with risk-allocation visualizations, earnings-report breakdowns with actual numbers from filings, and the structured argument format that takes viewers from "I saw this stock" to "Here's what the data says" without pretending to know the future. Built for finance educators building analysis libraries, registered investment advisors creating client content, trading-education platforms producing course material, financial news channels covering earnings and events, stock-analysis communities documenting thesis development, and anyone who understands that the most honest sentence in market analysis is "I could be wrong, and here's what that would look like."

## Example Prompts

### 1. Stock Analysis — Fundamental Deep-Dive
"Create a 10-minute fundamental analysis video of a large-cap tech company. Opening (0-20 sec): the stock chart on screen — 5-year view showing the price journey. 'This company is up 180% in 3 years. Everyone owns it. The question isn't whether it's a good company — it is. The question is whether it's a good investment at this price.' Revenue analysis (20-120 sec): animated bar chart — quarterly revenue over 3 years. 'Revenue grew from $74B to $96B annually — a 29% increase. But look at the growth rate: 15% in Q1, 12% in Q2, 8% in Q3, 6% in Q4. The growth is decelerating.' Overlay: growth-rate trend line sloping downward. 'Decelerating growth isn't bad — it's inevitable for companies at this scale. But the stock is priced for accelerating growth. That's the mismatch.' Margins (120-200 sec): gross margin trend line (72% → 68%). 'Four percentage points of margin compression over 2 years. Why? Rising compute costs for AI infrastructure and increased competition in cloud services.' Operating margin: 32% → 28%. 'When the top line decelerates and margins compress simultaneously, earnings growth stalls.' Show the earnings: animated EPS chart flattening. Valuation (200-320 sec): P/E ratio comparison — this company vs sector average vs 5-year historical average. 'Current P/E: 34x. Sector average: 25x. Its own 5-year average: 28x. At 34x, the market is pricing in growth that the recent quarters aren't delivering.' Forward P/E using analyst estimates: 'If they hit consensus estimates, forward P/E is 29x. If they miss by 10%, it's 32x at current prices.' Show the sensitivity table: price targets at different P/E and earnings scenarios. Bull case (320-380 sec): 'The AI tailwinds are real. If the new product cycle drives reacceleration to 15% growth and margins stabilize, the stock is fairly valued here.' Bear case: 'If growth continues decelerating to 5% and margins compress another 2 points, the stock is 30% overvalued at current prices.' Show both scenarios on the same chart — the range of outcomes. Thesis (380-480 sec): 'This is a world-class company at a premium price. The investment decision depends on your time horizon. 10-year holders will likely be fine — the company will grow into the valuation. 12-month traders need the AI catalyst to materialize or the stock revisits $X.' Portfolio context: 'At 34x earnings, this should be a 3-5% position, not a 20% concentration.' Closing: 'I hold this stock at a 4% allocation. My stop is a 15% margin compression. That's my thesis — show me the margins.' Disclaimer overlay."

### 2. Chart Pattern Education — Technical Analysis Basics
"Build a 7-minute technical analysis basics video for intermediate investors. Opening: 'Technical analysis doesn't predict the future. It identifies probabilities based on price patterns and volume. If someone says a pattern "guarantees" a move, they're selling you a course, not teaching you analysis.' Candlestick basics (0-80 sec): animated candle forming — 'Open at the bottom of the body, close at the top for green. The wicks show the high and low — where price went but couldn't stay.' Show: a big green candle with small wicks (strong bullish conviction), a candle with long wicks and small body (indecision — the doji), a red candle with long lower wick (hammer — sellers pushed down, buyers pushed back). Support and resistance (80-180 sec): animated chart with horizontal lines. 'Support: a price level where buyers consistently step in. Resistance: a price level where sellers consistently take profits.' Show price bouncing off support three times — 'The more times a level holds, the stronger it is. The more times it's tested, the more likely it eventually breaks.' Volume confirmation: 'Price breaks resistance on high volume = real. Price breaks resistance on low volume = fake-out. Volume is the lie detector of technical analysis.' Moving averages (180-260 sec): 50-day and 200-day moving averages on a chart. 'The 50-day shows the short-term trend. The 200-day shows the long-term trend. When the 50 crosses above the 200 — the "golden cross" — it's a bullish signal.' Show the golden cross and death cross on historical charts with what happened after each. 'It works until it doesn't. In 2020, the death cross fired in March and the market rallied 100% from that point. Context matters more than the signal.' Trend lines and channels (260-340 sec): draw a trend line connecting higher lows in an uptrend. 'A trend continues until it doesn't — but trading with the trend has better odds than trading against it.' Show a price channel — support and resistance trend lines parallel to each other. 'Trade the bounces within the channel. The breakout from the channel — either direction — is the big move.' Putting it together (340-420 sec): a real chart (anonymized) — walk through the analysis. 'Here's support at $142, resistance at $168, the 50-day crossing above the 200-day, and volume expanding on the breakout attempt. The technical picture says: bullish above $168 with volume. Below $142, the thesis fails.' Closing: 'Technical analysis is one tool. Combine it with fundamentals and you have a framework. Use it alone and you're reading tea leaves with a chart overlay.' Disclaimer."

### 3. Market Concept — Dollar-Cost Averaging vs Lump Sum
"Produce a 5-minute video comparing DCA vs lump sum investing with real historical data. Opening: 'You just received $12,000. Do you invest it all today or $1,000 per month for 12 months? Everyone has an opinion. Let's look at what the data actually says.' The math (0-100 sec): historical back-test animated. '$12,000 lump sum into the S&P 500 on January 1, 2015. vs. $1,000/month for 12 months in 2015.' Show the growth curves diverging — lump sum ahead because 2015 was an up year. Now show 2022: lump sum invested January 1, 2022 — the market dropped 20%. DCA spread the purchases across the decline and bought more shares at lower prices. Results: 'In rising markets, lump sum wins about 68% of the time over 12-month periods.' Show the Vanguard research: animated bar — 68% lump sum wins vs 32% DCA wins across 80+ years of data. The psychology (100-180 sec): 'Lump sum wins mathematically. DCA wins emotionally.' Animated scenario: you invest $12,000 lump sum. The next day, the market drops 10%. Your $12,000 is now $10,800. 'Mathematically, this is temporary. Emotionally, you feel like you made a $1,200 mistake and you'll check the portfolio every hour for a month.' DCA: you invest $1,000. The market drops. You invest another $1,000 at a lower price. 'You feel smart. You feel disciplined. You feel like the drop is an opportunity, not a punishment.' The real answer (180-260 sec): 'The best strategy is the one you actually execute.' Decision framework on screen: 'If you can invest and not check for 5 years → lump sum. If a 20% drop the next day would make you sell everything → DCA. If the money is coming from paychecks anyway → DCA by default. If the money is a windfall and you have high risk tolerance → lump sum.' Hybrid option: '60% lump sum + 40% DCA over 4 months. You capture most of the statistical advantage while reducing the regret of bad timing.' Closing: 'Both strategies beat leaving cash in a savings account at 0.5%. The worst decision is not investing because you're paralyzed by the lump-sum-vs-DCA debate while the money earns nothing.' Disclaimer."

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show both the bull and bear case** — One-sided analysis destroys credibility. The AI generates split-scenario visualizations when you describe both the optimistic and pessimistic outcomes with specific price/earnings targets.
2. **Animate charts progressively** — Candlesticks forming one by one, moving averages drawing across the screen, volume bars building up — progressive animation helps viewers follow the price story. The AI builds charts frame by frame when chart_animations is enabled.
3. **Include the "what would change my mind" statement** — "My thesis fails below $142" or "If margins compress another 2 points" shows intellectual honesty. The AI highlights thesis-invalidation levels with contrasting graphics.
4. **Use real historical data for strategy comparisons** — "In 2022, DCA outperformed because the market dropped 20%" is more convincing than hypothetical scenarios. The AI renders back-test results from the specific years and data you provide.
5. **Always display the disclaimer prominently** — Financial content requires it. The AI places "not financial advice" overlays at the opening and closing when disclaimer is enabled.
