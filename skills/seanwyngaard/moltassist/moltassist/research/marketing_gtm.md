##  CRITICAL ALERT: The Name "Pulse" Is Compromised

Before anything else: **OpenAI launched a feature called "ChatGPT Pulse" in September 2025.** It's described as "a proactive, card-based morning briefing that synthesizes your Memory, chat history, feedback, and opt-in connectors (like calendar and mail) to surface what matters before you even ask." -- TechCrunch, September 25, 2025.

This is the biggest competitor validation you could ask for -- OpenAI independently arrived at the same concept. But it also means the name "Pulse" is now directly associated with OpenAI's proactive AI product in the public mind.

Additional conflicts:
- `pulse-app.ai` -- emotion/mental health AI app
- `trypulse.app` -- exists, AI assistant with 7-day free trial
- `pulseai.io` -- ECG diagnostics AI (medical, different space)
- `getpulse.ai` -- loan underwriting AI
- `get-pulse.ai` -- another loan AI

**Recommendation: Rename before launch.** See Section 7 for alternatives.

The competition validates the market. The name needs to change.

## 1. Website Strategy -- Landing Page Architecture

### The Core Job of the Landing Page

The landing page has one job: make a skeptical person feel the thing before they understand the thing. Most AI products demo the interface. Pulse needs to demo the *feeling* -- the relief of not having to remember, the surprise of being looked after.

Benchmark landing pages studied: Linear, Superhuman, Notion, Loom, Arc Browser.

**What the best ones do:**
- Linear: Single powerful statement + gorgeous product screenshot above the fold. Proof first, explanation second.
- Superhuman: "The fastest email experience ever made." One claim, no hedging. Social proof in exact numbers (180,000-person waitlist).
- Arc: Emotional, opinionated. "A browser that thinks like you." Sells the feeling, not the feature list.
- Loom: Product GIF right in the hero. You understand it in 4 seconds without reading.
- Notion: "Write, plan, share. With AI at your side." Clean. No jargon.

---

### Full Landing Page Content Outline

#### HERO SECTION
**Headline:**
> "Most AI waits for you to ask.
> [Product name] doesn't."

**Subheadline (max 25 words):**
> "The AI that checks in on your life -- your calendar, your deals, your deadlines -- and reaches out before things slip."

**Hero visual:**
A phone/desktop mockup showing a notification. Not a chat interface. A push notification or card that says something like:
> "Your 3pm call with Marcus is in 20 minutes. His company raised a Series A last week. Might be worth mentioning."

This is the demo moment. One notification tells the whole story.

**CTA buttons:**
- Primary: `Join the waitlist` (no friction)
- Secondary: `See how it works` (anchors to demo section)

**Trust signal beneath CTA:**
> "No credit card. No setup wizard. Just [name]."

---

Short, punchy. Use real-world scenarios:

> "You forgot the follow-up. You missed the window. You were busy being busy."
>
> "AI tools are brilliant -- when you remember to use them. Most people don't."
>
> "The average professional switches between 10 apps daily. Nobody has time to check an AI. But everyone has time to read a notification."

One stat to include: **Knowledge workers spend ~28% of their workday on email alone** (McKinsey). This primes the pain.

Three-card layout, not a wall of text:

   *You define what matters. Pulse monitors it, 24/7, without being asked.*

## 2. The Demo Problem -- Showing a Proactive AI

### The Core Challenge

You cannot demo Pulse the way you demo Notion or Figma. You can't say "here's the interface -- look how fast it is." The product's value proposition is *absence of action on the user's part*. The whole point is they don't need to do anything. So you're demoing a feeling, a behavior change, an outcome.

This is the hardest type of product to demo. But it's been solved before.

### How Others Have Solved It

**Nest Thermostat (2011)**
The product learns from your behavior passively. Demo strategy: "A week of footage showing the house adjusting itself." They showed the *result* (comfortable home, lower bill) not the input (adjusting schedules). Apply to Pulse: show the **result** (nailed the meeting, caught the deal) not the input (recipe configuration).

**Apple Watch Activity Rings**
Ambient coaching product. The demo shows a person's day being quietly interrupted by a gentle tap. Not a dashboard -- a tap. One notification in an ad = the whole concept.

**Grammarly**
Demoed by showing *what your writing looked like before and after*. The AI is invisible; the outcome is visible. Apply to Pulse: show *what a user's week looked like before and after*.

**Superhuman**
Used a 1:1 onboarding call as the demo. Everyone who signed up got a personal walk-through. Expensive but legendary. **Consider for Pulse's first 100 users.**

---

### Proposed Demo Strategy for Pulse

#### 1. The "A Week in the Life" Video (Primary Demo Asset)
**Format:** 90-second narrated screen/phone recording. Split-screen:

Left side: "Alex without Pulse" -- Alex checking 5 apps, missing a follow-up, scrambling before a meeting.

Right side: "Alex with Pulse" -- Alex's phone delivers 3 quiet, well-timed cards throughout the day. No scrambling. Alex walks into the meeting knowing the context.

No chat interface. No "type your question." Just cards arriving at the right time.

**Production:** Can be done with Figma + screen recording + CapCut. Budget: $0-$500. This is your most important marketing asset.

#### 2. The Interactive Waitlist Demo
On the waitlist landing page: a simulated demo. User enters their job title, the page shows them a fake "what Pulse would have told you today" card based on their role.

Input: "I'm a Sales Account Executive"
Output:
> *"Good morning. You have a demo at 2pm with Brightpath Ltd. They visited your pricing page 3 times last week. Their fiscal year ends in March -- that's in 11 days."*

This is fake data, obviously disclosed as a demo scenario. But it makes the value immediately tangible. Converts browsers to signups.

Technical build: ~1 day of work. Simple JS, pre-written scenarios per role. 5-8 job titles.

#### 3. Screenshot-First Social Proof
The easiest demo: **screenshots of real notifications**. Like Notion's tweet screenshots. When users start using Pulse and share notifications that were uncannily useful -- amplify those. "Pulse told me my best customer was about to churn before I noticed. Here's the screenshot."

One real-world proof point beats any demo video.

#### 4. The "24-Hour Challenge" Content Format
YouTube/TikTok: Creator sets up Pulse, goes about their day, films the notifications as they arrive. Real-time reaction content. The notifications are the story -- not the setup.

## 4. Launch Strategy

### Sequencing Overview

```
PHASE 0 -- Pre-build (Now -> Month 1)
 Name decision (CRITICAL -- see Section 7)
 Landing page + waitlist live
 "Why we're building this" founder post (Twitter/LinkedIn)
 Recruit 20 design partners (not beta users -- partners)

PHASE 1 -- Private Alpha (Month 1-2)
 20-50 design partners (hand-picked, daily feedback)
 Superhuman-style onboarding calls for every user
 Build the Recipe Library with real users
 Collect testimonials + screenshots

PHASE 2 -- Public Beta (Month 2-3)
 Waitlist opens to all
 BetaList submission ($0 or $129 expedited)
 Hacker News "Show HN" post
 Product Hunt "upcoming" listing
 Creator outreach -- first 3 YouTube/TikTok partnerships

PHASE 3 -- Launch (Month 3-4)
 Product Hunt official launch (Tuesday-Thursday, 12:01 AM PST)
 App Store listing (iOS first, Android later)
 Press kit + 3 journalist outreach targets
 Email sequence to full waitlist
 Launch day social push

PHASE 4 -- Post-Launch Growth (Month 4+)
 Recipe marketplace soft launch
 First creator partnerships
 LinkedIn/YouTube content cadence
 Referral programme
```

---

### Waitlist Strategy

**The referral loop (Dropbox-style):**
- Sign up -> get position #847 in the queue
- Share your link -> move up for every signup
- Top 100 referrers get lifetime Pro access

This mechanic makes waitlist growth self-perpetuating. Dropbox grew from 100k to 4M users with this approach in 2009. Still works.

**The onboarding email sequence (5 emails over 2 weeks):**
1. Day 0: "You're on the list" + your position + referral link
2. Day 2: "Here's what Pulse is actually doing while you wait" -- behind the scenes
3. Day 5: "3 recipes our beta users love" -- builds anticipation
4. Day 9: "The problem we're solving (and why it matters)" -- founder story
5. Day 14: "You're in" (or: "Here's where you are in line")

---

### Product Hunt Launch Specifics

**Benchmarks:**
- Product Hunt: 5M+ monthly visitors, 3-5% conversion, 24-hour traffic spike
- BetaList: 200K monthly visitors, 12-15% conversion, steady traffic over weeks

**PH strategy:**
- Schedule for Tuesday or Thursday (highest traffic days)
- First comment = founder's story post (personal, vulnerable, specific)
- Thumbnail must show a notification, not a dashboard
- Target: Top 5 of the day -> coverage spike -> waitlist bump
- Notify every person in your network via personal DM at 12:01 AM PST launch day

**Don't sleep on:**
- IndieHackers post (long-form, genuine)
- Reddit: r/productivity, r/artificial, r/SideProject
- Hacker News Show HN (rules: must be something you made, must be real, no marketing language)

---

### App Store Strategy

iOS App Store ranking factors: conversion rate, ratings velocity in first 30 days, keyword density.

**ASO (App Store Optimisation) keywords to target:**
- "AI personal assistant"
- "smart notifications"
- "AI reminder app"
- "proactive AI"
- "daily briefing app"

**Review velocity:** Ask your first 100 users for a review inside the app at the moment they experience the "wow" -- typically right after their first hit notification. Don't ask on launch or after 5 minutes of use.

## 5. Pricing Psychology

### Market Benchmarks

| Product | Price | Model | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| ChatGPT Plus | $20/mo | Subscription | Reactive only |
| Superhuman | $30/mo | Subscription | 180k waitlist at $30 |
| Notion Business | $20/user/mo | Subscription | Team focus |
| Reclaim AI | $8-12/mo | Subscription | Calendar AI |
| Notion AI (add-on era) | $8-10/mo | Add-on | |
| Copilot for M365 | $30/user/mo | Enterprise | |

**Freemium conversion benchmarks (2024-2025):**
- Freemium average: 2-5% free-to-paid
- Freemium top performers: 5-7%
- Free trial (opt-in): 8-12% good, 15-25% great
- Free trial for professionals: 20%+ (CrazyEgg, 2025)

---

### Recommended Model: **Time-limited Free Trial**

**Not freemium.** Here's why:

1. Pulse's value requires proactive behaviour -- a crippled free tier that just lets users "try" recipes without them actually running is a ghost product. Users won't understand the value.
2. Free trial at 14 days forces a decision. Users who experience the "wow moment" (first uncanny notification) during those 14 days convert at high rates.
3. Freemium attracts people who never pay. Given Pulse's infrastructure costs (LLM calls, integrations running continuously), free users are expensive.

**The model:** 14-day free trial -> Pro required. No credit card upfront for the trial.

---

### Pricing Tiers

#### Free Trial (14 days)
- Full Pro access
- All integrations
- Up to 10 active recipes
- Unlimited notifications

#### Personal -- $12/month ($96/year -- 33% discount)
*Best for individuals, freelancers, founders*
- Unlimited recipes
- 5 integrations
- Daily briefings
- Mobile notifications
- 30-day history

#### Pro -- $19/month ($152/year -- 33% discount)
*Best for power users and professionals*
- Everything in Personal
- Unlimited integrations
- Custom recipe creation (no-code builder)
- Priority AI (faster processing)
- Slack/Teams delivery
- 12-month history
- Recipe Marketplace access (buy + install)

#### Teams -- $28/user/month ($224/year)
*Best for small teams*
- Everything in Pro
- Shared team recipes
- Team context (e.g., shared CRM, shared calendar)
- Admin dashboard
- Onboarding support

---

### Pricing Psychology Tactics

1. **Annual anchoring**: Show the annual price first. "$96/year" feels less than "$12/month  12."
2. **Middle-tier pull**: Price Personal at $12, Pro at $19. The jump is small enough that Pro becomes the obvious choice. Most SaaS companies make 60-70% of revenue from their mid tier.
3. **Avoid a $9.99 trap**: Round numbers ($12, $19, $28) signal confidence. $9.99 signals Amazon.
4. **The value comparison**: In the pricing section, add: "One forgotten follow-up costs more than a year of Pro." Concrete, non-defensive.
5. **Teams pricing per seat**: This is more aggressive per-user but justifiable because team recipes are infrastructure for the whole team.

## 6. The Recipe Marketplace -- Platform Strategy

### Is This a Real Business?

Yes. And the precedents are strong:

**Notion templates on Gumroad:**
- Thomas Frank: $100,000 in a single month from Notion templates
- "Easlo": $239,000+ in Notion templates via Gumroad
- Gumroad creator Pascio: 250,000+ templates sold, $275,000+ total

**What made those work:**
1. The platform (Notion) did the distribution -- templates lived inside the product
2. Creators had audiences who already trusted them in specific niches
3. Templates solved a real configuration pain ("I don't know how to set this up")

**Pulse recipes are the same concept with higher leverage:** A recipe doesn't just give you a template to fill in -- it gives you an *active agent* running in the background. That's 100x more valuable than a Notion template.

---

### Marketplace Model

**Revenue split recommendation:**
- 70% to creator / 30% to Pulse
- Industry standard: Gumroad takes 10%, Shopify takes 2.9%+30 per transaction
- The 30% take is justified by: distribution, trust, integration hosting, AI compute for running the recipe

**Creator tiers:**
1. **Community Creator** -- anyone can publish free recipes. No revenue share.
2. **Verified Creator** -- reviewed by Pulse team. Can sell. 70/30 split.
3. **Expert Partner** -- co-marketed, featured placement. Custom terms.

**Pricing for recipes:**
- Individual recipes: $5-$29 one-time
- Role packs (10-20 recipes): $29-$99 one-time
- Monthly creator subscriptions ("all my recipes"): $9-$19/month

**Who creates?**
- Sales coaches who want to sell their methodology as a live system
- Executive coaches packaging their frameworks
- Niche community leaders (e.g., real estate agents, recruiters, startup operators)
- Productivity creators (Thomas Frank, Ali Abdaal, Tiago Forte audience)

---

### The Platform Flywheel

```
Great recipes attract users
-> More users = more valuable marketplace
-> More value = more creators want to publish
-> More creators = better recipes
-> Better recipes attract more users
```

This is the Figma Community / Notion Template Gallery flywheel. Once it starts spinning, distribution compounds.

**Minimum viable marketplace:** You need 20 high-quality recipes across 5 roles at launch. Build these internally before opening to creators.

---

### Risks

1. **Recipe quality**: Low-quality recipes that don't fire correctly will kill trust in the platform. Require testing before publishing.
2. **Creator gaming**: Don't let creators spam low-effort recipes to farm the marketplace.
3. **IP/privacy**: Recipes that pull sensitive data need clear permission scoping.

## 7. Brand Voice & Name

###  Is "Pulse" the Right Name?

**Short answer: No. Not anymore.**

Beyond the domain conflicts, OpenAI's September 2025 launch of "ChatGPT Pulse" means that anyone searching "Pulse AI" will find OpenAI. In brand terms, you're playing on someone else's turf before you've even started.

From the domain check:
- `pulse.app` -- likely taken or squatted
- `getpulse.ai` -- exists (different product)
- `trypulse.app` -- exists (competitor)
- `pulse-app.ai` -- exists (emotion AI)

**Verdict: Change the name. The concept is excellent. The name is compromised.**

---

### 5 Alternative Name Suggestions

#### 1. **Nudge**
`getnudge.ai` -- concept of a gentle, timely push. "AI that nudges you forward." Immediate intuitive understanding. Risk: small overlap with behavioural economics tool (Nudge Theory), but that's a compliment not a conflict.

#### 2. **Foresight**
`withforesight.ai` -- premium, intelligent, proactive connotation. "The AI that sees what's coming." Slightly more premium/enterprise feel. Could also be `foresight.ai` if clear.

#### 3. **Vigil**
`vigiil.ai` or `vigilapp.io` -- "AI that watches over your work so you don't have to." Strong, slightly dramatic. Excellent for the "always on" positioning. Memorable and unusual.

#### 4. **Dispatch**
`dispatch.ai` -- "Your AI dispatches what matters, when it matters." Clean. Journalistic energy. Implies speed + relevance. Strong visual identity potential.

#### 5. **Scout**
`scoutai.app` or `withscout.ai` -- "AI that scouts ahead." Friendly, active, proactive. Works across B2C and B2B. Slightly startup-name-y but has warmth that "Pulse" lacks.

**Recommendation:** **Dispatch** or **Vigil** if going premium. **Scout** or **Nudge** if going accessible/consumer. Check `.ai` and `.app` availability before committing.

---

### Brand Voice Definition

Pulse (or whatever it becomes) is not like other AI products. The brand voice reflects that.

#### Core voice attributes:

**1. Quietly confident**
Doesn't shout about AI. Doesn't use the words "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," or "next-generation." Earns attention by being specific and useful. Like a brilliant colleague who just... knows things.

*Example:*
 "Our revolutionary AI-powered platform delivers next-generation proactive intelligence."
 "We noticed you haven't followed up on that proposal. It's been 6 days."

**2. Human-first**
Writes like a real person, not a product. Uses contractions. Uses "you." Occasionally uses humour. Never sounds corporate.

*Example:*
 "Users can configure notification preferences within the settings dashboard."
 "Tell us what matters to you. We'll handle the rest."

**3. Earned authority**
Doesn't hedge. Doesn't say "may help" or "could potentially." Makes claims and stands behind them.

*Example:*
 "This may help you stay on top of your work."
 "You won't miss the follow-up. We'll make sure of it."

**4. Dry wit (used sparingly)**
A light touch of personality in the right places. Not trying to be funny, just human.

*Example:*
"You asked us to remind you if you hadn't followed up in 3 days. It's been 7. We're not judging."

**5. Outcome-obsessed**
Features are never the hero. The outcome is the hero. What changes in your life? What doesn't happen that would have otherwise?

---

### Brand Voice Don'ts

-  "Seamlessly" (banned)
-  "Cutting-edge" (banned)
-  "Empower" (banned)
-  "Leverage" (in marketing copy -- not in B2B sales material)
-  "AI-powered" as the lead (it's table stakes in 2026)
-  Anything that sounds like a press release

---

### Tagline Options

1. "Most AI waits for you. [Name] doesn't." *(The original -- still excellent)*
2. "The AI that checks in." *(Simpler, conversational)*
3. "Your context, always loaded." *(More technical audience)*
4. "Never drop a ball again." *(Outcome-first, direct)*
5. "AI that reaches out, not waits around." *(Slightly playful)*

**Best for launch: Option 1.** It's a contrast frame (vs existing AI tools), it's memorable, and it immediately communicates the differentiation.
