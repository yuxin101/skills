## 1. Primary School Teachers

### Who They Are
Classroom generalists teaching all or most subjects to ages 5-12. Single-teacher model means they carry everything: literacy, numeracy, science, humanities, social-emotional learning, and often art/PE if specialist teachers aren't available. Class sizes typically 20-30 students.

---

### Daily / Weekly Rhythm

**Typical Day (7:45 AM - 6:00 PM)**

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 7:15-7:45 AM | Arrive, set up classroom, print materials, check emails |
| 7:45-8:15 AM | Morning duty (yard supervision, door greeting) or final prep |
| 8:15-8:30 AM | Register, morning routine, attendance |
| 8:30-10:30 AM | Core literacy block (phonics, reading groups, writing) |
| 10:30-11:00 AM | Recess duty (often rostered -- no break) |
| 11:00-12:30 PM | Numeracy block / maths |
| 12:30-1:15 PM | Lunch duty OR lunch break (if covered) |
| 1:15-2:00 PM | Science / HSIE / specialist lesson |
| 2:00-3:00 PM | Arts, sport, inquiry, or catch-up |
| 3:00-3:15 PM | Dismissal, parent conversations at door |
| 3:15-5:30 PM | Marking, prep, meetings, emails, admin |
| Evening | Often 1-2 hrs of lesson planning or marking at home |

**Weekly add-ons:**
- 1-2 staff meetings per week (often after school, 45-90 min)
- Parent communication: usually in bursts (30 min emails after school)
- Planning: most teachers block one afternoon/weekend for next-week prep
- Assessment: ongoing, spikes dramatically at end of term

**Actual hours worked:** Research consistently shows 50-54 hours/week for primary teachers. In-classroom time is roughly 25-30 hrs; the rest is prep, marking, admin, communication.

---

### Before Tasks (Preparation)
- **Daily:** Printing materials, setting up learning stations, writing objectives on board, checking which students need specific support, reviewing reading group notes
- **Weekly:** Writing lesson plans (often in a school-mandated template), preparing manipulatives, setting up group rotations, differentiating work for ability groups
- **Before assessments:** Creating or adapting tests, preparing observation checklists, notifying parents of assessment week
- **Before parent-teacher interviews:** Compiling student work samples, preparing talking points, reviewing marks book, checking any welfare concerns
- **Before excursions:** Permission slips, risk assessments, medication forms, organizing group allocations, briefing parent helpers
- **Before report-writing period:** Reconciling marks book, noting progress against curriculum outcomes, gathering work samples for evidence

---

### After Tasks (Follow-up & Admin)
- **Daily:** Marking written work (can take 30-90 min depending on task), updating marks/observations in grade book, responding to parent notes/emails
- **Weekly:** Updating reading records, logging welfare observations, submitting attendance data, updating learning programs (some schools require weekly submission)
- **End of term:** Writing student reports (typically 20-30 children  full-page narrative comments), parent-teacher conferences, curriculum documentation
- **Ongoing:** IEP contributions for students with needs, referrals to counselors/specialists, meeting follow-up actions
- **Annual:** Student transitions -- writing handover notes for next year's teacher; NAPLAN (Australia) / SATs (UK) / standardized test data entry

---

### Time-Sensitive Triggers

| Trigger | Frequency | Lead Time Needed |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Report writing deadline | End of each term (4x/year) | Need 2-3 weeks to write well |
| Parent-teacher interviews | Typically week after reports | 1 week of booking/prep |
| Curriculum planning submission | Term start / end | 1-2 weeks |
| Standardized testing (NAPLAN, SATs) | Once or twice per year | 4-6 weeks practice |
| Excursion permission + risk assessment | Varies -- set by teacher | 3-4 weeks before |
| Report card system closes | Hard deadline, school-set | Often 1 week before end of term |
| Staff performance review | Annual | 2-4 weeks of portfolio prep |
| Professional development obligations | Varies; often annual CPD hours | Ongoing |
| New student enrolment integration | Rolling | Immediate |
| Welfare referrals (mandatory reporting) | Triggered by incident | Same day |

---

### Stress / Anxiety Points

- **The report writing crunch:** Every single teacher dreads this. Writing 25 individual, meaningful, non-repetitive narrative reports in 1-2 weeks while still teaching full-time. The system closes on a specific date. There's no extension.
- **Never enough prep time:** Only 266 min/week of dedicated planning time on average (US data). Everything else happens before 8 AM, at lunch, or at home.
- **Parent communication anxiety:** Particularly difficult parent conversations, or the fear that an email will be screenshot and shared.
- **Behaviour incidents:** Managing a disruptive student takes enormous mental load, spills into lesson plans falling apart, and requires documentation.
- **Differentiation overload:** A class of 28 may have 6-8 students on different learning plans or with identified needs. Keeping up with all of it simultaneously.
- **Things that fall through the cracks:** Reading group records not updated, a welfare concern that was noted mentally but never formally referred, a student consistently missing and no one has followed up with parents.
- **End-of-year transition:** Finding time to write handover notes for 25-28 students before the year ends.

---

### Data Sources / Platforms
- **Student Information System (SIS):** PowerSchool, Compass (AU), Sentral (AU), SIMS (UK), Arbor (UK)
- **LMS / Classroom:** Google Classroom, Seesaw (primary), Class Dojo, Microsoft Teams
- **Assessment/gradebook:** Embedded in SIS, or standalone (Markbook, teachers' own spreadsheets)
- **Parent comms:** Class Dojo messaging, Seesaw, SchoolStream, Skoolbag, email
- **Curriculum portals:** Government curriculum sites (ACARA for AU, TES/DfE for UK), Teachers Pay Teachers
- **Wellbeing/incident logging:** Compass, SIMON, or paper-based

---

### Proactive AI Opportunities

> **"How did it know?"** -- moments where Pulse surfaces exactly the right nudge at the right time.

**1. Report writing countdown**
> *"Hey Emma, reports are due in 9 days and you've got 14 students left to write. Based on your usual pace of 3-4 reports per sitting, you'll need about 4 sessions. You're free after school on Tuesday and Thursday -- want me to block both? I'll also remind you the Sunday before as a checkpoint."*

**2. Pre-parent interview prep**
> *"Parent interviews start Thursday. You haven't pulled work samples for 6 students yet -- want me to add that to your Wednesday afternoon? Also, the Thomas family asked about literacy in their last email -- might be worth reviewing Jake's reading level data before you meet them."*

**3. The forgotten duty reminder**
> *"Just a heads up -- you're on yard duty tomorrow at 10:30. You usually forget on Tuesdays. Also, the weather forecast shows 34C -- you might want to grab a hat."*

**4. Welfare referral prompt**
> *"You flagged Marcus as 'seems withdrawn' in your notes 8 days ago. He's had 3 late marks this week. Do you want me to log a welfare note and copy the counselor, or do you want to talk to him first?"*

**5. Curriculum submission warning**
> *"Your Term 2 learning program is due to the deputy by Friday. You submitted it 2 days before the deadline last term -- want me to set a Thursday morning reminder so you're not scrambling?"*

## 2. High School Teachers

### Who They Are
Subject specialists teaching one or two disciplines across multiple year levels. Typically 5-6 classes per day, 25-32 students per class, so they may be managing 120-200 students simultaneously. Much heavier marking load than primary. Extracurricular expectations (sport, debating, clubs) common.

---

### Daily / Weekly Rhythm

**Typical Day (7:30 AM - 7:00 PM)**

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 7:00-7:30 AM | Arrive, prep board/projector, print resources, check emails |
| 7:30-8:00 AM | Period 0 or admin time |
| 8:00-9:00 AM | Period 1 |
| 9:00-10:00 AM | Period 2 |
| 10:00-10:30 AM | Break -- often used for quick marking or student catch-ups |
| 10:30-12:30 PM | Periods 3 and 4 |
| 12:30-1:15 PM | Lunch -- often a meeting or student assistance |
| 1:15-3:15 PM | Periods 5 and 6 |
| 3:15-3:30 PM | Dismissal, student questions, parent at gate |
| 3:30-5:00 PM | Marking, prep, staff meetings, detention supervision |
| Evening | 1-3 hrs marking at home, especially if essay/assignment due |

**Weekly add-ons:**
- Staff meeting (1-2 hrs, usually Monday or Tuesday)
- Department/faculty meeting (fortnightly, 45-60 min)
- Extracurricular duty: sport coaching, drama rehearsal, exam supervision
- Parent evening (1-2 per term, often a full evening 5:00-9:00 PM)

**Marking reality:** A high school English teacher with 5 classes  28 students = 140 essays per assessment task. At 10 min per essay, that's 23+ hours of marking for a single task. This is why weekends disappear during assessment periods.

---

### Before Tasks
- **Before each lesson:** Creating or adapting lesson plans, preparing presentations, printing handouts, setting up practicals (science), selecting texts
- **Before assessment tasks:** Writing rubrics, setting tasks, getting head-of-department sign-off, entering assessment dates into school calendar, notifying students/parents
- **Before parent evenings:** Pulling grade data, preparing comments for 120-200 students, flagging concerns to counselor
- **Before exam blocks:** Creating exams, submitting to head of department for review, arranging invigilation rosters
- **Before curriculum planning:** Scope and sequence documents, resource ordering, alignment to national/state curriculum frameworks

---

### After Tasks
- **Daily:** Entering grades, responding to emails, updating attendance/behavior logs
- **After assessments:** Marking (large volume), recording grades, writing individual feedback, data analysis for class/cohort performance
- **After parent evenings:** Follow-up emails to parents flagged during interviews
- **After exam blocks:** Marking, grading, entering results, preparing analysis reports
- **End of year:** Grade submissions, exam certification (HSC/A-Level/IB), writing references for university applicants

---

### Time-Sensitive Triggers

| Trigger | Timing |
|---------|--------|
| Assessment submission dates | Set at start of year; teacher creates them |
| Marking return deadline (feedback to students) | Usually 2 weeks from submission |
| Grade entry deadline | School-set, often per-term |
| Parent evenings | 2-3 per year, calendar-set |
| Curriculum documents due | Start of each term |
| University reference letters | Peaks Oct-Nov (northern hemisphere); Jan-Feb (AU) |
| Exam timetable release | 6-8 weeks before exam block |
| HSC/A-Level marking (external) | If examiner: strict deadline, external |
| Accreditation/registration renewal (teacher) | Varies by country/state -- 3-7 year cycles |
| Staff review/appraisal | Annual |

---

### Stress / Anxiety Points
- **Marking avalanche:** Assessment tasks across 5-6 classes come in simultaneously. No way around it.
- **Scope creep in prep:** Every lesson ideally differentiated; in practice, impossible with 150+ students.
- **Student at-risk students:** A struggling Year 11 who's falling apart emotionally. The teacher feels responsibility but lacks time.
- **University reference pressure:** Requests pile up in Term 4 and teachers feel obligated to write excellent letters for everyone who asks.
- **Email overwhelm:** Parent emails expecting same-day response, with no protected time to respond.
- **Extracurricular obligation vs. marking:** Coaching Saturday sport while 90 essays sit unmarked.
- **The exam you haven't written:** It's due to the deputy in 3 days and you haven't started.

---

### Data Sources / Platforms
- **SIS:** PowerSchool, Compass, Sentral, SIMS, Arbor
- **LMS:** Canvas, Google Classroom, Schoology, Moodle, Microsoft Teams
- **Assessment tools:** Turnitin (plagiarism), Rubric tools, Google Forms
- **Parent comms:** SIS-integrated portals, email, SchoolStream
- **Curriculum:** ACARA, TES, state education department portals
- **University applications (AU):** UAC portal, QTAC; (UK) UCAS; (US) Naviance/Common App

---

### Proactive AI Opportunities

**1. Marking timeline manager**
> *"You've got 147 essays due in tomorrow. Based on the 2 weeks you have to return them, you'd need to mark 15/day starting Monday. Want me to block 2 hours each weekday afternoon? I'll skip the Wednesday coaching session."*

**2. Exam creation alert**
> *"Your Year 10 History exam is on the 14th. You usually submit to the HoD 10 days before for review -- that's this Friday. You haven't started building it yet. Want me to block tomorrow morning?"*

**3. Reference letter season**
> *"You've had 4 students ask for university reference letters this week. Early offers close November 30. Want me to set aside two Saturday mornings in October to draft them -- before the Term 4 crunch hits?"*

**4. Parent evening prep**
> *"Parent evening is next Tuesday. You have 167 students to prepare notes for. You've only pulled grade data for your Year 9 cohort. Want me to generate a summary sheet from your gradebook for the other classes?"*

**5. The at-risk nudge**
> *"Jayden has missed 6 of the last 8 lessons and his last two assignments are blank submissions. You haven't logged a welfare referral yet. Want me to draft one for you to review, or would you prefer to speak to the counselor first?"*

## 3. University Lecturers

### Who They Are
Academic staff responsible for teaching, research, and institutional service ("the holy trinity"). Split roughly 40% teaching / 40% research / 20% admin at research universities; up to 80% teaching at teaching-focused institutions. Heavy autonomy but enormous hidden workload.

---

### Daily / Weekly Rhythm

Lecturers don't have a fixed daily schedule -- it varies radically by semester. But a composite real week during teaching semester looks like:

| Time Block | Activity |
|------------|----------|
| Monday AM | Lecture preparation (2 hrs per 1 hr lecture is a conservative estimate; new courses take 7-10x) |
| Monday PM | 2-hour lecture, then office hours (1-2 hrs) |
| Tuesday | Research block (if protected) -- writing, experiments, reading literature |
| Tuesday PM | Department/committee meeting |
| Wednesday AM | Marking/grading student work (essays, code, lab reports) |
| Wednesday PM | Tutorial or seminar delivery (smaller group, discussion-based) |
| Thursday | Email triage, research, thesis supervision meetings |
| Friday | Admin catch-up, conference paper writing, grant applications |
| Weekend | Research writing, grant deadlines, conference prep -- often 4-8 hrs |

**Average hours:** Research confirms 50-55 hrs/week during teaching semester, with spikes to 60-70+ during grant submission season or end-of-semester grading.

**Breakdown by task (per week, teaching semester):**
- Lecturing: 3-6 hrs
- Lecture prep: 6-15 hrs
- Grading: 3-15 hrs (spikes at assignment deadlines)
- Office hours: 2-5 hrs
- Research (writing, reading, experiments): 15-25 hrs
- Committees and admin: 3-10 hrs
- Supervision (PhD/Masters): 2-5 hrs
- Email: 2-4 hrs minimum

---

### Before Tasks
- **Before each lecture:** Content research, slide preparation, reading contemporary literature, creating examples, preparing demonstrations or case studies. A new lecture can take 6-14 hrs to prepare.
- **Before tutorials/seminars:** Setting discussion questions, reading student submissions, preparing facilitation guide
- **Before assessment tasks:** Designing rubrics, setting questions aligned to learning outcomes, submitting for moderation/peer review
- **Before thesis supervision meetings:** Reading student's latest draft, preparing feedback, tracking progress against milestones
- **Before research grant deadlines:** Months of proposal writing, budget preparation, collaboration agreements, ethics applications
- **Before conference presentations:** Paper writing or polishing, slide decks, travel booking
- **Before course launches:** LMS setup, uploading materials, setting up discussion boards, coordinating guest speakers

---

### After Tasks
- **Post-lecture:** Responding to student questions via email/LMS, updating notes based on what worked/didn't
- **Post-assessment:** Grading (can be 20-80 hrs for large courses), entering grades into SIS, uploading feedback, handling grade disputes
- **Post-exam:** Marking (often standardized; still intensive), grade moderation with colleagues, final grade submission
- **Post-grant submission:** Waiting, responding to reviewer queries, then either celebrating or resubmitting
- **Post-conference:** Writing up proceedings, follow-up collaborations, expenses claims
- **Year-end:** Annual performance review (research outputs, teaching evaluations, service contributions), workload allocation negotiations

---

### Time-Sensitive Triggers

| Trigger | Timing |
|---------|--------|
| Grade submission deadline | Hard deadline, ~1 week after exam/final assessment |
| Grant application deadlines | ARC (AU): March; NSF (US): varies; ESRC (UK): rolling rounds |
| Ethics application renewal | Annual or project-tied |
| Course outline/syllabus submission | 4-6 weeks before semester start |
| Conference abstract submission | Rolling; often 4-6 months before conference |
| Journal review turnaround | Usually 3-4 weeks from assignment |
| PhD milestone reviews (progress reports) | 6-monthly for supervisors |
| Research output reporting (ERA, REF) | Annual to multi-year cycles |
| Teaching evaluation surveys | End of each teaching period |
| Contract/promotion applications | Annual cycle; requires months of documentation |
| Accreditation (program-level) | 5-7 year cycles, intense preparation year |

---

### Stress / Anxiety Points
- **The "publish or perish" treadmill:** Research output pressure never stops. Every week without a publication advancing feels like falling behind.
- **Grant rejection:** Months of work, rejected. Must start again. Deeply demoralizing.
- **End-of-semester grading avalanche:** Hundreds of assignments + hard grade submission deadlines + research doesn't pause.
- **Thesis supervisors caught between student needs and their own work:** A PhD student in crisis takes enormous time and emotional energy.
- **Committee/service work expanding:** Every "yes" to a committee drains research time. Hard to say no when early-career.
- **Email volume during finals:** Students panicking, asking for extensions, disputing grades -- dozens per day.
- **Teaching evaluation anxiety:** Student feedback counts toward promotion. A bad semester feels like a threat to career.
- **The grant that's due in 72 hours and you haven't started the budget section.**

---

### Data Sources / Platforms
- **LMS:** Canvas, Moodle, Blackboard, Brightspace (D2L)
- **SIS/Gradebook:** Banner, PeopleSoft, Ellucian, university-specific systems
- **Research management:** Pure, Symplectic Elements, ResearchGate, ORCID
- **Grant portals:** ARC Research Management System (AU), Grants.gov (US), Funding Service (UK), Horizon Europe Portal
- **Reference management:** Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote
- **Ethics applications:** University ethics portals (highly varied)
- **Student communication:** Institutional email, LMS messaging, Slack/Teams increasingly
- **Performance/output tracking:** ERA (AU), REF (UK), university internal systems

---

### Proactive AI Opportunities

**1. Grading deadline countdown**
> *"Final essays were submitted 3 days ago. You have 89 submissions to grade and your institution's deadline is in 11 days. That's about 8 per day. You have two back-to-back lectures Tuesday -- want me to protect Wednesday morning as a grading block?"*

**2. Grant deadline early warning**
> *"ARC Discovery Project applications close March 14 -- that's 38 days away. You started a draft in December. The budget section is incomplete and you haven't attached your CV or track record statement yet. Want me to map the remaining tasks across the next 5 weeks?"*

**3. PhD milestone tracker**
> *"Your student Kai is due for their 12-month progress review in 3 weeks. You haven't had a supervision meeting in 6 weeks. Want me to schedule a meeting this week and set a reminder to review their draft chapter before you meet?"*

**4. Conference season prep**
> *"The ASA conference abstract deadline is in 21 days. You mentioned wanting to submit your coastal data paper. You're presenting at AARE in 6 weeks too -- want me to set milestone dates for both so they don't collide?"*

**5. Teaching eval -> promotion window**
> *"Your mid-semester evaluations came in -- average 4.1/5. Strong qualitative comments on clarity. Your promotion application opens in 4 months. Want me to save these results to your promotion portfolio folder?"*

## 4. School Principals / Vice Principals

### Who They Are
Operational and instructional leaders of a school. Principals carry ultimate accountability for everything -- student outcomes, staff performance, safety, compliance, community relationships, budget, facilities. Vice principals often specialize (curriculum, discipline, operations). Day is almost entirely reactive; "managing interruptions" is the job description.

---

### Daily / Weekly Rhythm

**Typical Day (6:30 AM - 7:00 PM)**

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 6:00-7:00 AM | Arrive before staff, review day's schedule, emails, check overnight incidents |
| 7:00-7:45 AM | Gate duty or student arrival supervision |
| 7:45-8:15 AM | Brief with deputy/VP, check in on staff issues |
| 8:15-9:00 AM | Classroom walkthrough / instructional rounds |
| 9:00-10:00 AM | Parent meeting (often unplanned) or scheduled admin |
| 10:00-10:30 AM | Recess duty or staff check-in |
| 10:30-12:00 PM | Office: emails, calls, reports, district/departmental tasks |
| 12:00-1:00 PM | Meetings (staff, student welfare, external visitors) |
| 1:00-2:00 PM | Classroom observation or instructional coaching |
| 2:00-3:00 PM | Admin, HR tasks, budget review |
| 3:00-3:30 PM | Dismissal supervision |
| 3:30-5:30 PM | Staff meetings, parent calls, catch-up on admin |
| Evening | Budget documents, policy writing, email responses |

**Reality check:** A study of principals found they make 50-100 decisions per day. Interruptions are constant. The "planned schedule" is aspirational; the actual day is dictated by who walks in the door.

---

### Before Tasks
- **Before school year:** Staff rosters, timetabling, class lists, budget allocation, facilities checks, policy updates
- **Before term:** Staff meeting agendas, curriculum documentation review, safety compliance sign-offs
- **Before parent evenings:** Reviewing staff-parent allocation, preparing principal address, checking welfare flags
- **Before inspection/review visits:** Compiling data packs, preparing staff, self-assessment documentation
- **Before staff performance reviews:** Gathering observation notes, reviewing data, preparing review templates
- **Before board/school council meetings:** Preparing financial reports, strategic plan updates, minutes from previous meeting

---

### After Tasks
- **After incidents:** Documentation, parent notification, possible police/department reporting, staff debrief
- **After classroom observations:** Writing feedback for teachers, scheduling follow-up conversations
- **After board meetings:** Action item follow-through, financial reporting submission
- **After inspections:** Response documentation, improvement plan development
- **After staff reviews:** Follow-up plans, HR documentation, performance improvement plans if needed
- **Ongoing:** Department/district reporting (monthly), newsletter/communications, staff professional development oversight

---

### Time-Sensitive Triggers

| Trigger | Timing |
|---------|--------|
| School year planning (timetabling, staffing) | August-October for following year |
| Budget submission to district/board | Annual -- varies; often Term 1 |
| Staff performance reviews | Annual cycle |
| Inspection/school review | Every 3-5 years (OfSTED UK, NESA AU, etc.) |
| NAPLAN/standardized test administration | Set by government -- principals manage logistics |
| Report card sign-off | End of each term |
| Enrolment management (waitlists, new students) | Rolling, peaks Jan/Feb |
| Mandatory reporting obligations | Immediate on trigger |
| Safety drills (fire, lockdown) | Termly or annual, legally required |
| Staff contract renewals | Annual cycle, often Feb-March |
| Grant applications (school improvement funds) | Various; often Term 1 |
| School council elections | Annual |

---

### Stress / Anxiety Points
- **The parent who threatens to go to the media.** Or the Board. Or the Minister. Escalation management is a constant undertow.
- **A staff member who is underperforming** but the HR documentation isn't airtight enough to act.
- **Cash flow:** Many schools manage tight budgets with a lot of discretionary decisions. A broken HVAC in summer is a crisis.
- **An incident they didn't hear about until it was already on social media.**
- **Inspection anxiety:** OfSTED (UK), NESA (AU), State Department visits -- months of preparation stress.
- **Staff wellbeing vs. school performance:** Good teachers burning out. Hard conversations.
- **The email inbox that never empties.** Principals routinely receive 100-300 emails/day.
- **Legal exposure:** Duty of care incidents, child protection reports, workplace injury claims.

---

### Data Sources / Platforms
- **SIS:** PowerSchool, Compass, Sentral, Arbor, SIMS
- **HR systems:** EdSynch, Alesco (AU), MiCase (UK), SAP HR
- **Finance:** CASES21 (VIC), TechOne, School financial portals
- **Communication:** Skoolbag, SchoolStream, Operoo, parent email systems
- **Inspection/review portals:** OfSTED (UK), NESA (AU), state education department portals
- **Safety/incident:** Compass wellbeing module, Riskware, paper-based in smaller schools
- **Board governance:** Diligent, BoardPaq, or email chains

---

### Proactive AI Opportunities

**1. Inspection readiness tracker**
> *"Your OfSTED inspection window opens in 4 months. Last time you had 9 days' notice. Your self-evaluation form hasn't been updated since October and your safeguarding policy is due for annual review. Want me to set up a weekly prep checklist for the next 12 weeks?"*

**2. Report sign-off crunch**
> *"Report cards need your sign-off by Friday for 847 students. As of this morning, 12 teachers haven't submitted yet. I can send them an automated reminder from you -- want me to draft one?"*

**3. Staff review calendar**
> *"You have 34 staff performance reviews due before end of Term 2. You've completed 8. You have 6 weeks left -- that's about one per day. Want me to auto-schedule 30-minute blocks on your calendar starting Monday?"*

**4. Budget deadline**
> *"Your annual budget submission is due to the district office in 12 days. The capex section is incomplete and you haven't submitted the enrolment projections yet. Your business manager is on leave until Thursday -- want me to flag this and schedule a session Friday morning?"*

**5. Proactive parent alert**
> *"Three parents have emailed you about the Year 4 camp this week. The permission slip deadline is tomorrow and you're still missing consent from 14 students. Want me to trigger an automated reminder to those families?"*

## 5. Private Tutors

### Who They Are
Independent educators, often self-employed, working one-on-one or in small groups. May specialize in exam prep (SAT, ATAR, GCSE, IB), subject support, or early literacy/numeracy. Operate as a small business: they are teacher, scheduler, invoicer, marketer, and customer service rep simultaneously. Client base typically 10-40 active students, with high churn during exams and holidays.

---

### Daily / Weekly Rhythm

**Highly variable but a typical self-employed tutor's week:**

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| Morning | Admin: invoicing, scheduling requests, lesson planning, material prep |
| Midday | Gap (many students are at school) or younger/homeschooled students |
| 3:00-7:00 PM | Peak session hours (after school) -- 3-6 sessions back-to-back |
| 7:00-9:00 PM | More sessions (older students / adults), or admin catch-up |
| Weekend | Often fully booked; also admin, prep, lesson planning |

**Business reality:** 60-70% of tutoring sessions happen between 3:00-7:00 PM on weekdays and Saturdays. The tutor's "working hours" are anti-social hours.

**Income pressure:** Cancellations are revenue loss. No paid sick leave. No paid preparation time. Every holiday week = significant income drop.

---

### Before Tasks
- **Before each session:** Reviewing student's last session notes, identifying what to cover, sourcing or creating practice materials, checking if there's an exam coming
- **Before new students:** Intake assessment/diagnostic, scope of work discussion with parents, setting goals and timeline
- **Before exam periods:** Creating mock exams or sourcing past papers, intensifying schedule, tracking student's weak areas systematically
- **Business prep:** Creating/updating session report templates, invoices, intake forms, policies (cancellation, rescheduling)

---

### After Tasks
- **Post-session:** Writing session notes (what was covered, homework set, progress update), sending summary to parents (many expect this), logging in scheduling/billing software
- **Admin:** Sending invoices (weekly or monthly), chasing unpaid invoices, responding to new client inquiries, managing waitlist
- **After exam results:** Following up with students, updating case studies/testimonials for marketing, analyzing what prep worked
- **Tax/compliance:** Self-employment tax quarterly, expense tracking, potentially registering business, insurance renewals

---

### Time-Sensitive Triggers

| Trigger | Timing |
|---------|--------|
| Exam dates (GCSE, A-Level, IB, SAT, ATAR etc.) | Fixed calendar; different per country/year |
| Exam prep intake season | 3-6 months before major exams; Oct-March busy period |
| Term start/end | Client churn: students pause/restart at holidays |
| School report cards released | Parents see poor grades -> inquiry spike |
| Invoice due dates | Weekly or monthly (tutor-set) |
| Tax filing deadlines | Country-specific; quarterly BAS (AU), quarterly estimated tax (US) |
| Insurance renewal | Annual |
| Professional development / re-certification | Country-specific; some require CPD hours |
| Rate reviews | Annual |

---

### Stress / Anxiety Points
- **Last-minute cancellations:** "Can we skip this week?" from a family = lost income, wasted prep time.
- **Invoice chasing:** Awkward. Many tutors hate asking for money and let it slide until there's a large balance outstanding.
- **Scheduling Tetris:** One session reschedule ripples into three other changes. Managing 20-40 clients' availability manually is exhausting.

## 7. School Counselors / Psychologists

### Who They Are
Mental health, social-emotional, and academic support professionals embedded in schools. School counselors focus on academic advising, social-emotional learning, and crisis intervention. School psychologists focus more on psychological assessment (IQ/learning disability testing), behavioral intervention, and clinical support. Roles blur significantly in practice.

**Caseload reality:** ASCA recommends 1 counselor per 250 students. Actual ratios in the US average 1:408, in AU often 1:500+, meaning most counselors are chronically stretched.

---

| 3:00-3:30 PM | More documentation from the day |
| 3:30-5:00 PM | Admin, case notes, referral paperwork, parent calls |
| Evening | Often 30+ min additional documentation at home |

- **Before group sessions or classroom guidance lessons:** Preparing curriculum/lesson plan, setting up room, gathering materials

- **The referral that never gets actioned.** You referred a student to CAMHS (UK) / community mental health (AU/US) 8 weeks ago. Still on the waitlist. Student is getting worse.

- **Paperwork vs. kids time:** The entire job should be about helping students. Instead, 40-60% of time is paperwork.

## Cross-Role Patterns: Design Implications for Pulse

### Universal Pain Points Across All Education Roles
1. **Deadline blindness** -- They know deadlines exist; they just don't surface until the crunch. Every role has 5-15 time-sensitive triggers per term.
2. **Documentation debt** -- Notes, case records, reports, and forms pile up. The gap between "it happened" and "it's documented" is where things fall through.
3. **No genuine break** -- Lunch duties, lunchtime library, prep periods consumed by admin. Physical downtime is planned; cognitive downtime is rare.
4. **Reactive vs. proactive split** -- Everyone's day is hijacked by the immediate. Planning for the medium term (2-6 weeks ahead) is where the biggest value is.
5. **Siloed data** -- Their world is fragmented across SIS, LMS, email, a marks book, a physical planner, and mental notes. No one system has the whole picture.

### Key Pulse Design Principles for Education
- **Calendar intelligence:** Know the term calendar, not just the user's personal calendar. Academic year rhythms are predictable and should be built-in.
- **Ahead-of-time triggers:** Surface things 2-3 weeks early, not the day before. The goal is calm action, not panic action.
- **Caseload awareness:** For counselors, SPED teachers, and tutors -- the AI should track individual student timelines, not just bulk deadlines.
- **Tone calibration:** Teachers are stressed. The AI voice should be warm, practical, and non-judgmental. "You haven't done X yet" must never feel like criticism.
- **Small next action:** The AI shouldn't tell them everything that needs doing. It should surface the one next thing with the least friction. "Want me to draft it?" removes the biggest barrier.
- **Protected time suggestions:** Knowing a teacher's free periods and using them intelligently is a superpower.
