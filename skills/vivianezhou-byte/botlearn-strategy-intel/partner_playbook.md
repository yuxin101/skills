# Partner's Playbook
*How Partner thinks about strategy — built from HBS MBA + 10 years at TikTok*

---

## Core belief

**Strategy is about choice, not plan.** A real strategy means saying no to most things.
It also means nothing without implementation — analysis without execution is just consulting theater.

---

## Three levels of analysis (always run all three)

### Macro — Industry & Market
- Start with the industry structure: trends, tailwinds, headwinds, competitive landscape
- Find the **10x incremental market** — the space that is growing fast enough that a startup can win without taking share from incumbents
- Ask: is this company playing where the market is going, or where it already is?
- A company with mediocre execution in a 10x market beats a great company in a flat one

### Middle — Product & Customer
- Understand the company's **competitive edge at the product level**, not just the pitch level
- Go deep on customer feedback and interviews — what job are customers actually hiring this product to do?
- The value proposition is **dynamic, not static** — what wins today may not win in 18 months
- Ask: does the product create a habit, or just solve a one-time problem?

### Micro — Team & Founders
- Every company decision is made by human beings — study the founders first
- **Founder-market fit** is as important as product-market fit
- A founder's background and expertise predicts the company's future bets
- Ask: does this founder have unique insight into this problem, or did they just spot an opportunity?

### Micro — Economics & Cash
- For startups, the most urgent strategic question is often: **can they generate cash before they run out of it?**
- Understand the unit economics: does each customer make money or lose money at the transaction level?
- A company that needs aggressive fundraising just to stay in the game is not executing a strategy — it is surviving
- Ask: what is the path to cash flow positive, and how many funding rounds does it require?
- Burn rate and runway are not just finance metrics — they constrain every strategic choice the company can make

## Frameworks Partner always applies

- **Innovator's Dilemma** (Christensen): Is an incumbent being disrupted from below? Is this startup the disruptor or the next incumbent?
- **Low-end disruption**: Does this product win by being good enough for underserved customers at lower cost? That's often more durable than feature competition
- **Jobs to Be Done**: What specific job is the customer hiring this product to do? If you can't name it precisely, the strategy is fuzzy
- **Crossing the Chasm**: Is this company still in the early adopter phase? The gap between early adopters and early majority kills most startups

---

## Red flags Partner watches for

- Strategy that sounds like a plan ("we will expand to X, then Y, then Z") — that's a roadmap, not a strategy
- Moat claims that are actually just operational advantages (anyone can replicate with funding)
- Founder who can speak to the market but not to the customer
- Product positioning that changes every quarter — dynamic is fine, chaotic is not
- TAM slide that adds up every adjacent market — real TAM is the market you can actually win

---

## The one question that cuts through everything

**"If this company disappeared tomorrow, who would actually miss it — and why?"**
If the answer is vague, the strategy is vague.
