# Critical Thinking Framework Reference

Based on "Asking the Right Questions" by Neil Browne & Stuart Keeley

## Core Concepts

### Sponge Thinking vs. Gold-Mining Thinking

| Sponge Thinking | Gold-Mining Thinking |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Passively absorbing information | Actively engaging with information |
| Emphasizing "what is" | Emphasizing "why" and "whether it's credible" |
| Uncritical acceptance | Continuous questioning and evaluation |

**The Essence of Critical Thinking**: Proactively engaging in deep processing of what we see and hear, raising questions and making evaluations.

## Key Question Checklist

### Questions About Arguments and Reasons

1. **What are the thesis and conclusion?**
   - Thesis = The question the author wants to answer
   - Conclusion = The viewpoint the author wants you to accept

2. **What are the reasons?**
   - Reasons = Evidence and argumentation supporting the conclusion
   - Are the reasons sufficient?

3. **Which terms are ambiguous?**
   - Are key term definitions clear?
   - Are there multiple possible interpretations?

4. **What are the assumptions?**
   - Value assumptions: What does the author consider more important?
   - Descriptive assumptions: What does the author believe about how the world works?

### Questions About Evidence Effectiveness

5. **How effective is the evidence?**
   - Personal experiences
   - Typical cases
   - Expert opinions
   - Research results
   - Analogies

6. **Are there alternative causes?**
   - Could the result be caused by something else?
   - Correlation does not equal causation

7. **Are the statistics deceptive?**
   - Is the sample representative?
   - Choice of averages/percentages/absolute values

8. **What important information has been omitted?**
   - Counter-evidence
   - Applicable conditions
   - Long-term effects

### Questions About Conclusions

9. **What reasonable conclusions can be drawn?**
   - Avoid "yes-yes-yes/no-no-no" binary thinking
   - Consider limitations of conditions
   - Recognize over-generalization

## Common Fallacies Quick Reference

### 1. People-Related Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Counter-argument |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| Ad Hominem | Attacking the person instead of the argument | "Is the argument itself valid?" |
| Appeal to Authority | Authority doesn't equal correctness | "Is this authority in their field of expertise?" |
| Bandwagon | Everyone thinks this way | "Does popularity equal correctness?" |

### 2. Logic-Related Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Counter-argument |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| Straw Man | Distorting the opposing view | "What did they actually say?" |
| False Dilemma | Either/or | "Are there other possibilities?" |
| Slippery Slope | Unwarranted chain inference | "Is there evidence for each step?" |
| Circular Reasoning | Using conclusion to prove premise | "Does the reasoning itself need proof?" |

### 3. Evidence-Related Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Counter-argument |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| Hasty Generalization | Insufficient samples | "Can the sample represent the whole?" |
| Post Hoc | Sequence equals causation | "Is there another explanation?" |
| Gambler's Fallacy | Treating independent events as related | "Each is a new beginning" |

### 4. Emotion-Related Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Counter-argument |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| Appeal to Emotion | Emotion instead of argument | "Can emotions replace evidence?" |
| Appeal to Tradition | Tradition equals correctness | "Has the era changed?" |
| Appeal to Novelty | New equals better | "Is the new necessarily better?" |

## Evidence Type Effectiveness Ratings

| Evidence Type | Effectiveness Rating | Description |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Systematic research/Meta-analysis | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Highest credibility |
| Peer-reviewed academic research | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Highly credible |
| Government agency reports | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Usually reliable |
| Professional organization reports | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Depends on independence |
| Expert personal opinions | ⭐⭐⭐ | Requires additional support |
| Typical cases | ⭐⭐ | Cannot be generalized |
| Personal experiences | ⭐⭐ | Limited persuasiveness |
| Analogical argumentation | ⭐ | Requires similarity proof |

## Critical Reading Checklist

- [ ] Identify thesis: What type of thesis is it?
- [ ] Find conclusions: What does the author want you to believe?
- [ ] Analyze reasons: What evidence supports it?
- [ ] Examine terms: Are key terms ambiguous?
- [ ] Discover assumptions: Are explicit/implicit assumptions valid?
- [ ] Evaluate evidence: What is the evidence type and its effectiveness?
- [ ] Check logic: Is the argumentation structure reasonable?
- [ ] Identify fallacies: Are there logical fallacies?
- [ ] Consider alternatives: Are there other explanations?
- [ ] Examine data: Is the statistical data reliable?
- [ ] Look for omissions: Has important information been omitted?
- [ ] Draw cautious conclusions: What level of conclusion can be drawn?
