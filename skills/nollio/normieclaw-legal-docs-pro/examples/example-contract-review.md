## Clause-by-Clause Analysis

### Section 1 — Rent Escalation
**Risk Level:** 🟡 Review Recommended

> "Base Rent shall increase by 5% on each anniversary"

**What this means:** Your rent goes up 5% every year automatically. Starting at $3,600/month, you'd pay $3,780 in year 2 and $3,969 in year 3.

**Why flagged:** 5% annual escalation is above market. Typical commercial lease escalations in Denver run 2-3% annually, and some are tied to CPI (inflation index). Over 3 years, you'll pay approximately $4,300 more than you would at a 3% rate.

**Suggested edit:** "Base Rent shall increase by 3% on each anniversary of the Commencement Date" or "Base Rent shall increase annually by the greater of 2% or the percentage increase in the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U) for the Denver-Aurora-Lakewood metropolitan area."

---

### Section 2 — Triple Net (NNN)
**Risk Level:** 🟡 Review Recommended

> "Tenant shall be responsible for Tenant's Pro Rata Share of all Operating Expenses"

**What this means:** On top of your base rent, you pay a share of the building's property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and management fees. This could add $8-15/sq ft annually ($800-1,500/month) depending on the building.

**Why flagged:** NNN leases are common for commercial space, but this clause has no cap on Operating Expenses and no exclusions for capital improvements. The landlord could pass through the cost of a new roof or elevator repair as an "Operating Expense."

**Suggested edit:** Add: "Operating Expenses shall not include capital improvements, depreciation, mortgage payments, or costs reimbursed by insurance. Tenant's Pro Rata Share of Operating Expenses shall not increase by more than 5% annually over the prior year's actual expenses."

---

### Section 3 — Permitted Use
**Risk Level:** 🟢 Standard

Restricting use to general office purposes is standard. The consent requirement for changes is normal. No issues here.

---

### Section 4 — Maintenance and Repairs
**Risk Level:** 🟡 Review Recommended

> "Tenant shall be responsible for all repairs within the Premises, including but not limited to HVAC systems, plumbing, electrical systems"

**What this means:** You're on the hook for fixing everything inside your suite — including the HVAC, plumbing, and electrical. If the AC breaks, that's your bill, not the landlord's.

**Why flagged:** Making the tenant responsible for HVAC is aggressive. HVAC systems are building infrastructure and can cost $5,000-15,000 to repair or replace. In many commercial leases, HVAC is the landlord's responsibility or shared. Plumbing and electrical systems serving the Premises (not just fixtures) should also be landlord territory.

**Suggested edit:** "Tenant shall maintain the interior finishes of the Premises and shall be responsible for minor repairs under $500 per occurrence. Landlord shall be responsible for HVAC systems, plumbing, and electrical systems serving the Premises."

---

### Section 5 — Alterations
**Risk Level:** 🟡 Review Recommended

> "All approved alterations shall become the property of Landlord upon installation"

**What this means:** Anything you build out or install becomes the landlord's property. If you spend $20,000 on a custom buildout, you leave it behind when you go.

**Why flagged:** The ownership transfer is standard, but there's no tenant improvement allowance mentioned and no provision for removing your trade fixtures (equipment, furniture, signage). You should be able to take your stuff when you leave.

**Suggested edit:** Add: "Notwithstanding the foregoing, Tenant's trade fixtures, equipment, and removable personal property shall remain the property of Tenant, and Tenant shall have the right to remove the same upon expiration or termination, provided Tenant repairs any damage caused by such removal."

---

### Section 6 — Personal Guarantee
**Risk Level:** 🔴 Significant Risk

> "The individual signing on behalf of Tenant hereby personally and unconditionally guarantees all obligations"

**What this means:** If your business can't pay rent, the landlord can come after your personal bank accounts, home, car, and other personal assets. This pierces the protection of your LLC or corporation. The guarantee survives even after the lease ends.

**Why flagged:** This is the most dangerous clause in this lease. Personal guarantees are sometimes required for new businesses without credit history, but "unconditional" with no cap and no time limit is extremely aggressive. You formed a business entity specifically to separate business and personal liability — this clause eliminates that protection.

**Suggested edits (negotiate in order of preference):**
1. **Remove entirely:** "Section 6 is deleted in its entirety." (Best outcome)
2. **Cap it:** "Personal Guarantee shall be limited to [6 months' rent] and shall expire after the first 12 months of the Lease Term, provided Tenant is not in default."
3. **Burn-off provision:** "Personal Guarantee shall automatically terminate after 18 months of timely rent payments."

---

### Section 7 — Relocation
**Risk Level:** 🔴 Significant Risk

> "Landlord reserves the right to relocate Tenant to comparable space within the building upon 30 days' written notice"

**What this means:** The landlord can force you to move to a different suite in the building with just 30 days' notice. They pay moving costs, but you have no say in the matter.

**Why flagged:** This gives the landlord unilateral power to disrupt your business. "Comparable space" is subjective — comparable in square footage doesn't mean comparable in location, visibility, or layout. You could be moved from a street-facing suite to an interior room. Clients who know your current location would be confused. This is uncommon in stronger tenant markets.

**Suggested edits:**
1. **Remove entirely** (best option)
2. **Add protections:** "Comparable space shall mean space of equal or greater square footage, similar configuration, and comparable visibility. Tenant shall have the right to approve or reject any proposed relocation. If Tenant rejects the proposed space, this clause is void."

---

### Section 8 — Default and Remedies
**Risk Level:** 🔴 Significant Risk

> "Landlord may accelerate all remaining Rent for the balance of the Term"

**What this means:** If you're late on rent by 6 days and don't fix it within 10 days, the landlord can demand the entire remaining lease balance immediately. If you're 18 months into a 36-month lease, that's 18 months of rent (~$65,000+) due all at once.

**Why flagged:** The 5-day grace period is extremely short (10-15 days is more typical). Rent acceleration is a nuclear option that shouldn't be the first remedy. Combined with the personal guarantee, this means a single missed payment could trigger a $65,000+ personal liability.

**Suggested edits:**
- Extend grace period: "If Tenant fails to pay Rent within **15 days** of the due date..."
- Remove acceleration: Replace with "Landlord may charge a late fee of 5% of the overdue amount" as the first remedy, with termination only after repeated defaults
- Add cure period: "Landlord shall provide written notice of default. Tenant shall have **30 days** to cure any non-monetary default and **15 days** to cure any monetary default."

## Missing Protections

### 1. CAM Cap / Operating Expense Cap
**What's missing:** No limit on how much Operating Expenses can increase year-over-year.
**Why it matters:** Without a cap, your NNN costs could spike 20%+ in a single year if the building needs major repairs.
**Add:** Annual cap of 5% increase on controllable Operating Expenses.

### 2. Right to Sublease
**What's missing:** No reasonable consent standard for subletting.
**Why it matters:** If your business shrinks or changes, you're stuck paying for space you don't need with no way to offset costs.
**Add:** "Landlord's consent to subletting shall not be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed."

### 3. Tenant Improvement Allowance
**What's missing:** No buildout allowance from the landlord.
**Why it matters:** Most commercial leases include a TI allowance ($20-50/sq ft) for the tenant to customize the space. On 1,200 sq ft, that's $24,000-60,000 you're leaving on the table.
**Add:** "Landlord shall provide a Tenant Improvement Allowance of $___/sq ft."

### 4. Early Termination Option
**What's missing:** No way to exit the lease early.
**Why it matters:** A 3-year commitment with no exit is risky for a growing or changing business. Most tenants negotiate a break clause.
**Add:** "Tenant may terminate this Lease after 18 months with 90 days' written notice and payment of a termination fee equal to 3 months' rent."

### 5. Force Majeure
**What's missing:** No provision excusing performance during extraordinary events.
**Why it matters:** A pandemic, natural disaster, or government order could prevent you from using the space. Without force majeure, you still owe full rent.
**Add:** Standard force majeure clause excusing or abating performance during events beyond either party's reasonable control.

This lease is heavily landlord-favorable. The three biggest issues — personal guarantee, relocation clause, and rent acceleration — should be addressed before signing. The missing protections (especially CAM cap and TI allowance) represent significant money being left on the table.
