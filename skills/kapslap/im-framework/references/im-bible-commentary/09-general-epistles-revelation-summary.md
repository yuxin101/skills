# General Epistles and Revelation — What Happens

## The General Epistles (Hebrews–Jude)

These letters are called "general" (or "catholic") because most aren't addressed to specific churches — they circulate widely.

**Hebrews** — No one is entirely sure who wrote it. The argument: everything in the Old Testament — the priesthood, the sacrifices, the tabernacle, the covenant — was real but provisional. It was the shadow; Christ is the substance. Christ is the "better" priest, offering a "better" sacrifice, establishing a "better" covenant. The book is addressed to Jewish Christians tempted to go back to the old system.

Chapter 11 is the "faith chapter" — a roll call of Old Testament heroes who trusted God without seeing the outcome: Abel, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Sarah, Moses, Rahab, David, the prophets. "These all died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off." Faith is defined as "the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen."

Chapter 12: life is a race. We're surrounded by a "great cloud of witnesses" — the heroes of faith who went before us. Run with endurance.

**James** — Traditionally attributed to Jesus's brother. The most practical letter in the New Testament. Faith without works is dead — "Show me your faith without works, and I will show you my faith by my works." Control your tongue. Don't show favoritism to the rich. Visit orphans and widows. "Pure religion" = caring for the vulnerable. Martin Luther famously called it an "epistle of straw" because it seemed to contradict Paul's emphasis on faith alone — but the tension is productive. Paul says faith saves you; James says real faith acts.

**1 Peter** — Written to persecuted Christians. You are "strangers and pilgrims" in this world. Suffering is participation in Christ's sufferings. It's not meaningless — it's a refining fire. "Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour."

**2 Peter** — Warning against false teachers. A reminder that Christ will return, though the delay may seem long: "One day is with the Lord as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day."

**1 John** — The letter of love. Written by the same author as John's Gospel (or his community). Three themes repeated: believe rightly (Jesus came in real flesh), live rightly (keep the commandments), and love rightly (love one another). The summit: "God is love; and he that dwelleth in love dwelleth in God, and God in him." Also: "There is no fear in love; but perfect love casteth out fear." The test of faith is not doctrinal precision alone but whether you love.

**2-3 John** — Brief personal letters. 2 John warns against false teachers who deny Christ came in flesh. 3 John commends hospitality and criticizes a church leader named Diotrephes who refuses to welcome traveling missionaries.

**Jude** — A short, fierce letter warning against false teachers who have infiltrated the community. Contend earnestly for the faith. Guard what has been entrusted to you.

## Revelation — The Apocalypse

The last book of the Bible, written by John (traditionally the same John as the Gospel, though scholars debate this) from exile on the island of Patmos during Roman persecution, probably around 95 AD. It is the most visually spectacular, terrifying, and debated book in the Bible.

**Chapters 1–3: Letters to seven churches.** The risen Christ — eyes like flame, voice like many waters, a sword coming from his mouth — dictates letters to seven churches in Asia Minor (modern Turkey). Each church gets a specific diagnosis: Ephesus has lost its first love; Smyrna is suffering but faithful; Laodicea is lukewarm ("I will spit you out of my mouth"). The letters are sharp, personal, and urgent.

**Chapters 4–5: The throne room.** John is taken to heaven and sees God on a throne surrounded by twenty-four elders, four living creatures, lightning, thunder, and a sea of glass. A scroll sealed with seven seals — the future, or God's plan — sits in God's hand. No one is worthy to open it. John weeps. Then: "the Lion of the tribe of Judah has conquered!" But when John looks, he sees not a lion but a *lamb* — "as though it had been slain." The wounded lamb is the one with ultimate authority. This image — power expressed through vulnerability — is the theological heart of Revelation.

**Chapters 6–18: Judgment.** Seven seals are opened, seven trumpets are blown, seven bowls of wrath are poured out. War, famine, plague, earthquake, cosmic collapse. A great dragon (Satan) wages war on heaven and is cast down. Two beasts arise — one from the sea (political power), one from the land (religious/ideological power). They demand total allegiance and economic participation (the "mark of the beast," 666). Babylon the Great — a symbol of corrupt civilization, the empire that traffics in everything including "the souls of men" — is destroyed.

These chapters are violently symbolic. They have been interpreted as: (a) coded references to the Roman Empire (the original context), (b) a map of future events (the dispensationalist/Left Behind reading), (c) a recurring pattern of how empires rise and fall throughout history, or (d) a symbolic portrayal of the spiritual battle between good and evil in every age. Most serious scholars hold some combination of these.

**Chapters 19–20: Final battle.** Christ returns, defeats the beasts, and Satan is bound. A thousand-year reign (the "millennium" — its nature is hotly debated). Final judgment: the dead are raised and judged. Death itself and Hades are thrown into the lake of fire. "The last enemy that shall be destroyed is death."

**Chapters 21–22: The new creation.** The climax of the entire Bible. "I saw a new heaven and a new earth." A holy city — the New Jerusalem — *descends from heaven to earth*. Not souls going up to heaven, but heaven coming down. "God himself shall be with them, and be their God. And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain."

There is no temple in the city — God's presence fills everything. A river of life flows from God's throne. The tree of life (from Genesis, from Eden) is there — on both sides of the river, bearing twelve kinds of fruit, with leaves "for the healing of the nations." Eden is restored, but expanded: not a garden for two people but a city for all peoples. The last words of the Bible: "Come, Lord Jesus."

## Why It Matters

The General Epistles insist that faith is lived — in love (1 John), in works (James), in endurance (1 Peter, Hebrews), in vigilance against distortion (Jude, 2 Peter). Revelation insists that history is not random — it has a direction, and that direction is toward the full presence of God with humanity, the defeat of everything that destroys, and the healing of everything that hurts. The Bible ends not with escape from the world but with the world's transformation — a city where God dwells among people, where the river flows, and where the leaves never stop healing.
