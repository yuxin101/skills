# Content Format Templates

Detailed, ready-to-use structural templates for every self-help content type.

---

## 1. FULL BOOK OUTLINE

```
TITLE: [Promise + Paradox or Question]
SUBTITLE: [The specific transformation / "How to X"]
TAGLINE: [One sentence. What changes for the reader.]

PART I — THE PROBLEM
  Chapter 1: [Name the enemy — the belief, pattern, or system holding them back]
  Chapter 2: [Why the common solutions fail]
  Chapter 3: [The hidden cost of staying stuck]

PART II — THE SHIFT
  Chapter 4: [The core reframe — new lens for seeing the problem]
  Chapter 5: [The science behind the shift]
  Chapter 6: [Stories of those who made it]

PART III — THE PRACTICE
  Chapter 7: [Daily habit / ritual #1]
  Chapter 8: [Daily habit / ritual #2]
  Chapter 9: [The hard part — what gets in the way and how to push through]

PART IV — THE LIFE
  Chapter 10: [What relationships look like on the other side]
  Chapter 11: [What work looks like on the other side]
  Chapter 12: [Your next chapter — the invitation forward]

EPILOGUE: Author's personal note
ACKNOWLEDGMENTS
RESOURCES / NOTES
```

## 2. BOOK INTRODUCTION (The "Why This Book")

Length: 800–1,500 words

```
OPENING HOOK (para 1–2)
  → Open mid-scene: a story. Your story, or a composite that mirrors the reader's story.
  → The moment of before. Something was broken, missing, or wrong.

THE PROBLEM NAMED (para 3–4)
  → "If you've ever felt [X], you're not alone."
  → Name the specific frustration the book addresses. Be precise.

WHY EXISTING ANSWERS FAIL (para 5–6)
  → Acknowledge what the reader has already tried.
  → Respect their intelligence. "You've heard the advice. It's not enough."

THE PROMISE (para 7)
  → What this book offers instead.
  → Not a magic fix — a different framework.

WHO THIS BOOK IS FOR (para 8)
  → Be specific. Not "everyone." Name the reader.

THE AUTHOR'S JOURNEY (para 9–10)
  → Why you (or this book) is the guide. What did you learn the hard way?
  → Vulnerable and earned, not boastful.

HOW TO USE THIS BOOK (para 11)
  → Reading guide if needed. Short. Practical.

CLOSING LINE
  → An invitation. Warm. Forward-looking.
  → "Let's begin."
```

## 3. SINGLE CHAPTER DRAFT

Length: 1,500–3,000 words

```
1. CHAPTER HOOK (200–300 words)
   → Open with a scene, a paradox, a surprising statistic, or a question.
   → No "In this chapter, I will explain..." — just start.

2. CORE PREMISE (100–150 words)
   → State the chapter's ONE idea, clearly.
   → "Here's what I've come to believe: [idea]."

3. THE SCIENCE/PROOF (200–400 words)
   → 1–3 studies, historical facts, or psychological frameworks.
   → Name researchers, institutions, years where possible.
   → Always translate to human terms.

4. PRIMARY REFERENCE STORY (300–600 words)
   → One story following: Hook → Context → Conflict → Turn → Truth → Bridge
   → Real person or clearly labeled composite.

5. INSIGHT REFRAME (150–250 words)
   → The moment of "I never thought of it that way."
   → Often a metaphor or image that carries the idea.

6. PRACTICAL BRIDGE (200–400 words)
   → 2–3 scenarios from everyday life.
   → "This shows up as... when you..."

7. ACTION STEPS (100–200 words)
   → 1–3 numbered, specific, doable actions.
   → Not vague ("be grateful") but concrete ("write three things...")

8. CHAPTER CLOSER (50–100 words)
   → One final image, sentence, or micro-story.
   → Should linger. Poetic without being purple.
```

## 4. MICRO-CHAPTER / INSIGHT (1–2 pages)

Length: 400–700 words

```
TITLE: [A short provocative phrase]

HOOK: One sentence or a two-sentence scene.

THE IDEA: State it plainly in 2–3 sentences.

THE EVIDENCE: One fact, one story, one quote. No more.

THE APPLICATION: "What this means for you is..."

CLOSER: One final line that lands.
```

---

## 5. SECTION OPENER (Between parts of a book)

Length: 150–300 words

```
→ A short poem-like passage, a quote from a historical figure, or a brief scene.
→ Sets the emotional tone for the section to follow.
→ No explanation — just atmosphere and invitation.
→ End with: "This is where we go next."
```

---

## 6. CLOSING CALL-TO-ACTION (Book ending / chapter ending)

Length: 100–200 words

```
→ Call back to the opening hook — where was the reader then vs. now?
→ Name the change that is now possible.
→ Give one final action, small enough to do today.
→ Express belief in the reader. Genuine, not hollow.
→ End with a short, declarative sentence. Period.
```

## 7. CHAPTER TITLE FORMULAS

| Formula | Example |
|---|---|
| The [Counterintuitive Noun] | The Productive Pause |
| Why [Common Belief] Is Wrong | Why Discipline Is the Wrong Goal |
| The [Number] [Things] Nobody Tells You | The Three Things Nobody Tells You About Confidence |
| [Verb] Like [Archetype] | Think Like a Cartographer |

| What [Surprising Thing] Taught Me About [Big Topic] | What Losing Taught Me About Winning |
| The [Metaphor] You've Been Missing | The Map You've Been Missing |

## 8. OPENING HOOK PATTERNS

See `references/opening-hooks.md` for 30+ examples. Core patterns:

1. **Scene-drop**: Open mid-action, mid-thought, mid-crisis.
2. **Paradox open**: "The more I chased success, the further it ran."
3. **Question open**: "When was the last time you felt fully alive?"
4. **Fact surprise**: "In 2003, researchers at Harvard made a discovery that rewrote how we think about the brain."
5. **Character intro**: "Maria had done everything right. And that was exactly the problem."
6. **Universal moment**: "You know that feeling at 3am when..."
7. **Historical scene**: "It was 1940, and Churchill had just received the worst news of the war."

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

- ❌ Starting with a dictionary definition ("Webster's defines courage as...")
- ❌ Promising what the chapter cannot deliver
- ❌ Using bullet points as a substitute for thought
- ❌ Action steps that are too vague ("work on your mindset")
- ❌ Stories without a bridge back to the reader
- ❌ Ending a chapter on a flat summary instead of an image
- ❌ Starting two consecutive chapters the same structural way
