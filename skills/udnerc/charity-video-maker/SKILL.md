# Charity Video Maker — Nonprofit Cause and Awareness Videos

The nonprofit's annual report is a 32-page PDF that the development director spent three months producing, the board reviewed with the attention span of someone scrolling through a menu they've seen before, and the donors — the people the report exists to engage — opened, looked at the financial pie chart on page 4, confirmed their name appeared in the donor list on page 28, and closed it, absorbing approximately 6% of the information the report contained while missing the story on page 12 about a single mother whose children now attend the afterschool program that the donor's $500 helped fund, a story so compelling that it would have renewed the donation instantly if anyone had actually read it. Charity video content exists because nonprofit storytelling is the highest-stakes communication challenge in media: the audience has no obligation to care, the subject matter is often uncomfortable, the budget is always limited, and the outcome of success or failure is measured not in revenue but in human lives — children who get fed or don't, diseases that get researched or don't, communities that get rebuilt or don't — making every production choice a resource-allocation decision where every dollar spent on video is a dollar not spent on the mission, a tension that demands maximum impact per minute of content. This tool transforms nonprofit missions into polished cause and awareness videos — beneficiary-story documentaries showing the human impact of donations, campaign-launch videos rallying support for specific initiatives, annual-impact reports translated from PDFs into watchable narratives, donor-appreciation content recognizing the people who make the work possible, awareness-campaign videos educating the public about causes that need attention, and the emergency-appeal videos that mobilize resources when a crisis demands immediate response. Built for nonprofit marketing teams creating fundraising content, development directors producing donor communications, volunteer coordinators recruiting through storytelling, advocacy organizations raising public awareness, foundation program officers documenting grantee impact, and anyone whose cause deserves the visual storytelling that turns empathy into action.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Beneficiary Story — The Human Face of Impact
"Create a 3-minute beneficiary story video for a food bank. Opening (0-15 sec): a kitchen. A woman preparing dinner — simple, deliberate, the care visible in how she arranges plates for three. 'Maria makes dinner every night for her two children. This sounds ordinary. Six months ago, it wasn't.' The before (15-60 sec): Maria's voice — over footage of daily life. 'I lost my job in March. By May, we were choosing between groceries and electricity. I chose electricity because the kids needed lights to do homework. We ate rice and beans for three weeks straight.' Show the reality without exploitation: the empty fridge (Maria chose to show it), the careful budgeting on a notepad, the children doing homework at the kitchen table unaware of the stress their mother carries. 'I didn't know food banks existed for people like me. I thought they were for homeless people. I wasn't homeless — I was employed for 12 years. Then I wasn't.' The turning point (60-120 sec): the food bank. Maria walking in for the first time. 'The hardest step I've ever taken was walking through that door. I cried in the parking lot for ten minutes first.' The experience — shown from Maria's perspective. 'They didn't treat me like a charity case. They said "welcome" like I was a neighbor. Because I was.' The food: not canned goods on a shelf but fresh produce, meat, dairy. 'I expected canned beans. They gave me chicken, vegetables, milk, eggs. My daughter said "Mom, we have real food again" and I had to leave the room so she wouldn't see me cry.' The ongoing support: 'The food bank didn't just give me groceries. They connected me with a job-training program. They helped me apply for benefits I didn't know I qualified for. The food was the door — the support behind it was the house.' The now (120-155 sec): Maria in her kitchen — the same kitchen, but different. The fridge is full. She's cooking something more complex than rice and beans. 'I got a job in September. I don't need the food bank anymore. But I volunteer there every other Saturday.' Show Maria at the food bank — on the other side of the counter now, handing food to someone who looks the way she looked six months ago. 'I remember the parking lot. I remember crying. When I see someone walk in for the first time with that look on their face, I say "welcome" the same way they said it to me.' The ask (155-180 sec): 'Maria's story is one of 4,200 families served this year.' The numbers on screen: 4,200 families, 340,000 meals distributed, 89% of families report improved food security within 3 months. '$25 provides a week of groceries for a family of three.' 'The food bank doesn't just feed people. It catches them. And then it helps them stand back up.' Donate button. URL. Logo."

### 2. Campaign Launch — Rallying Support for an Initiative
"Build a 90-second campaign launch video for a clean-water initiative. The problem (0-15 sec): a child carrying a yellow water jug on a dirt path. The jug is almost as big as the child. 'This is a 2-mile walk. She makes it twice a day. The water she carries will make her family sick. She knows this. There is no alternative.' The scale (15-35 sec): the number: 703 million. 'Seven hundred and three million people lack access to clean drinking water.' Map visualization: regions highlighted. 'Two-thirds of them live in sub-Saharan Africa. The distance between their home and clean water averages 3.7 miles.' 'The health impact: 1.2 million deaths per year from waterborne diseases. Most are children under 5.' The solution (35-65 sec): 'A well costs $8,000 to drill and serves 250 people for 20 years.' Show the drilling: a team, equipment, a community watching. The moment water appears — the celebration, the disbelief, the joy. 'That's $32 per person for 20 years of clean water. Less than a dollar a year.' Show the after: the child from the opening — now at a well 200 meters from home. Filling the same yellow jug but smiling. Going home in 5 minutes instead of 2 hours. 'The 4 hours she spent walking are now 4 hours for school.' The campaign (65-82 sec): 'Our goal: 50 wells in 12 months.' The counter on screen: 0/50. '$400,000 provides clean water to 12,500 people.' 'Every $8,000 donated triggers the next well. You'll see the GPS coordinates, the drilling photos, and the community celebration.' CTA (82-90 sec): the child's face — looking directly at the camera. 'Clean water isn't a miracle. It's a well. And a well costs $8,000.' Donate URL. Campaign name. '#50Wells.'"

### 3. Annual Impact Report — PDF to Video Translation
"Produce a 2-minute annual impact video summarizing a nonprofit's yearly results. Opening (0-8 sec): the organization's logo. The year: 2025. 'One year. Here's what your support made possible.' The numbers (8-50 sec): animated, building on screen as narrated. 'Students served: 1,847.' Show a classroom — real students, real engagement. 'Graduation rate: 94% — compared to the district average of 71%.' 'College acceptance: 88% of seniors.' 'First-generation college students: 62% of those accepted.' 'Each number represents a person. Here are three of them.' The stories (50-100 sec): three students, 15 seconds each. Student 1: 'Jaylen. First in his family to graduate high school. Starting engineering at [University] this fall.' Photo: Jaylen in cap and gown. Student 2: 'Aisha. Arrived speaking no English 3 years ago. Graduated with honors. Full scholarship to [College].' Photo: Aisha with her family. Student 3: 'Marcus. Failed 9th grade. Our tutoring program helped him catch up. He graduated on time and is training to be an EMT.' Photo: Marcus in uniform. 'Three names out of 1,847. Each one a story. Each one made possible by this community.' The financials (100-130 sec): 'Total revenue: $2.4 million.' Pie chart: individual donations (52%), grants (28%), events (15%), corporate partnerships (5%). 'Program spending: 84 cents of every dollar went directly to student services.' 'We publish our full financials on our website because transparency isn't a policy — it's a promise.' The thank you (130-110 sec): 'To our 1,200 individual donors. To our 14 corporate partners. To our 89 volunteers who gave 6,400 hours.' 'You didn't just fund a program. You funded Jaylen's engineering degree, Aisha's scholarship, and Marcus's second chance.' Close (110-120 sec): the students — a group photo, waving. 'Thank you for 2025. Let's do it again.' Year: 2026. Donate URL. Logo."

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Lead with a person, not a statistic** — Maria cooking dinner engages before "4,200 families" contextualizes. The AI structures person-first narratives.
2. **Show dignity, not desperation** — The beneficiary is the hero, not the victim. The AI frames beneficiaries with agency and respect.
3. **Make donation amounts concrete** — "$25 = one week of groceries" is actionable. The AI renders donation equivalencies when impact_counter is enabled.
4. **Include the transformation arc** — Before → turning point → after shows impact as a story. The AI structures three-act beneficiary narratives.
5. **End with a specific, bounded ask** — "$8,000 for one well" is clearer than "please donate." The AI closes with concrete donation targets.
