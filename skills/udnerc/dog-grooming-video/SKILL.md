# Dog Grooming Video Maker — Dog Grooming and Pet Care Tutorial Videos

The dog is wet, the bathroom looks like a crime scene committed entirely with water and fur, and the groomer — who is not a groomer but a pet owner with a YouTube tab open and optimism that has not yet been corrected by experience — is attempting to hold a 60-pound Labrador still with one hand while operating clippers with the other, a task that requires roughly three more hands than any human possesses. Professional dog grooming videos are YouTube's most satisfying pet content because the transformation is dramatic and visible: a matted, overgrown, slightly miserable-looking dog walks in and a fluffy, sculpted, distinctly happier dog walks out, and the process between those two states involves skills that look simple on screen and are absolutely not simple in practice. This tool transforms grooming sessions into polished tutorial videos — breed-specific technique breakdowns with coat-type diagrams showing where to clip, scissor, and blend, tool-identification overlays naming each blade guard and brush type, step-by-step bath-to-blowout sequences with water temperature guides, before-and-after reveals with matched angles, behavioral tips for handling nervous or fidgety dogs, and the satisfying final spin that shows the finished groom from every angle. Built for professional groomers building their online presence, mobile grooming businesses creating marketing content, pet-care influencers producing tutorial libraries, veterinary clinics demonstrating at-home maintenance between professional grooming appointments, dog breeders showing breed-standard grooming, and pet owners whose dogs tolerate exactly seven minutes of brushing before staging a dramatic escape.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Full Groom — Golden Retriever Bath to Finish
"Create a 7-minute full grooming tutorial for a Golden Retriever. Opening (0-15 sec): the Golden before grooming — slightly overgrown, feathering on legs tangled, ears fluffy. 'A Golden Retriever in full coat is a beautiful thing. A Golden Retriever in full coat that hasn't been groomed in 8 weeks is a beautiful mess. Here's the reset.' Pre-bath prep (15-60 sec): brush-out first — slicker brush through the body coat, working in sections. 'Always brush before the bath. Wet mats become concrete. Dry mats are removable.' Show the mat-breaking technique on a leg feather: hold the base of the mat against the skin, work the slicker through the tip, gradually moving toward the root. 'Never pull a mat from the skin outward — always work tip-to-root.' Undercoat rake through the body — show the fur coming out. 'This is the undercoat. It sheds naturally but a rake session removes what's loose and prevents the mats from forming.' The pile of removed undercoat on the table — 'This came from one dog. This is normal. Golden Retrievers are fur factories.' Bath (60-160 sec): water temperature check — 'Lukewarm. Test on your inner wrist like you would a baby's bottle. Dogs overheat easily and hot water dries the skin.' Wet thoroughly — 'Double-coated dogs are waterproof by design. Getting a Golden truly wet takes 3-4 minutes of direct water pressure.' Shampoo: diluted in a mixing bottle. 'Never apply shampoo concentrate directly — it's impossible to rinse out of a double coat.' Lather in sections, working the shampoo down to the skin. Show the technique on the chest — the densest area. 'If you can't feel the skin through the lather, the shampoo isn't reaching where the dirt is.' Rinse completely — 'Rinse until you think you're done, then rinse for two more minutes. Shampoo residue causes itching and hot spots.' Conditioner on the feathering — legs, chest, tail. 'Conditioner on the feathering prevents tangles between grooms.' Rinse. Blow-dry and brush (160-280 sec): high-velocity dryer — 'This is not a human hair dryer. The force blows the water out of the coat instead of heating it dry.' Show the technique: work from the back forward, section by section. The water visibly blasting out of the coat (satisfying content). 'The coat should be bone dry. Any moisture left in the undercoat creates mildew smell and hot spots.' Brush while drying: slicker brush follows the dryer, each section brushed smooth as it dries. Close-up of the coat transforming from wet and flat to dry and fluffy. Trim (280-370 sec): ear shaping — thinning shears to neaten the ear edges. 'Never use straight scissors on ear feathering — thinning shears create a natural look.' Show the technique: comb the ear hair out, thin the edges in a curved line following the ear leather. Foot trim: clip the hair between the paw pads with small clippers. 'This prevents ice balls in winter and mud collection year-round.' Scissor the foot outline into a round, neat shape — 'The "cat foot" — tight and tidy.' Feathering: thinning shears on the leg feathering, chest, and belly to neaten without removing the breed-standard coat. 'A Golden should look groomed, not clipped. The feathering is the breed's signature.' Sanitary trim. Tail: scissor the tail fan to an even edge. Nail trim (370-400 sec): show the quick identification on a light nail — 'See the pink center? Stop 2mm before that.' Dremel smoothing after clipping. 'The Dremel rounds the edges. A clipped nail without grinding is a scratching weapon.' Final reveal (400-420 sec): the finished Golden — slow rotation on the grooming table. Before-and-after split screen. 'Eight weeks of growth, 90 minutes of work, one extremely fluffy result.' The dog's reaction: tail wagging, happy shake. 'The shake is inevitable. Accept it. It means they're done and they know it.'"

### 2. Puppy's First Groom — Introduction and Desensitization
"Build a 5-minute video about a puppy's first grooming experience, focused on desensitization. Opening: puppy on the grooming table — wide-eyed, curious, slightly trembling. 'This is a first groom. The goal today is not perfection — it's creating a positive association. Every puppy who enjoys grooming as an adult had a groomer who took it slow the first time.' Desensitization approach (0-60 sec): let the puppy explore the table. Treats constantly. 'The treats are not rewards for being still. They're creating a positive association with the table, the sounds, the tools. Classical conditioning, not obedience training.' Turn on the clippers near (not on) the puppy. Treat. Touch the clipper body (turned off) to the puppy's side. Treat. Turn on and touch. Treat. 'Each step introduces one new sensation. Rushing this creates a dog that fights grooming for life.' Face and feet (60-140 sec): the two most important areas to desensitize. Touch the puppy's feet — hold each paw, press the pads, spread the toes. Treat after each paw. 'Dogs who hate nail trims weren't handled as puppies. This 60 seconds of paw handling prevents 12 years of nail-trim battles.' Touch the face — around the muzzle, under the chin, near the eyes. Treat. 'A dog who lets you touch its face lets you clean its ears, brush its teeth, and wipe its eye boogers. Invest now.' Gentle bath (140-220 sec): warm water, low pressure, one hand always on the puppy. 'The first bath should be warm, brief, and full of treats. The puppy doesn't need to be spotless — it needs to not hate water.' Gentle shampoo, gentle rinse. Wrap in a towel immediately — 'The towel cocoon. Almost every puppy calms down when wrapped.' Towel dry mostly, then a LOW setting blow dryer from a distance. 'If the puppy shows stress (panting, whale eye, trying to escape), stop. Switch to towel drying. A wet puppy who likes grooming is better than a dry puppy who's traumatized.' Light trim (220-280 sec): just face, feet, sanitary. Thinning shears only — 'No clippers on the body during a first groom. The vibration is too much stimulation on top of everything else.' Neaten the paw pads, trim around the eyes, sanitary area. Each snip followed by a treat. 'The puppy doesn't need a breed cut today. It needs to leave this table thinking grooming is a treat-dispensing activity that involves mild inconvenience.' Closing (280-300 sec): puppy on the ground, playing, happy. Before-and-after (minimal visual change). 'The before and after doesn't look dramatic because that wasn't the point. The real result is invisible: a puppy that will sit calmly on a grooming table for the next 15 years.' Text overlay: 'First groom checklist: patience > perfection.'"

### 3. Breed-Specific — Poodle Continental Clip
"Produce a 6-minute breed-standard Poodle grooming video showing the continental clip. Opening: Standard Poodle in full grown-out coat — 'The Poodle continental clip looks ridiculous to people who don't know its history and magnificent to people who do. Every pom-pom has a purpose. Here's how it's done.' Coat prep (0-60 sec): bath the Poodle, blow-dry with a stand dryer while brush-fluffing — 'Poodle coat must be clean and straight before clipping. The curls hide length. Blow-drying reveals the true coat length.' Show the transformation: curly wet coat → straight fluffy coat after drying. 'This is the canvas.' The clip pattern (60-90 sec): diagram overlay on the dog showing the continental clip zones — shaved face, shaved feet, shaved hindquarters with rosettes on the hips, full mane on the front, bracelets on the legs, pom on the tail. 'The continental clip originated with working water retrievers. The shaved areas reduced drag in water. The pom-poms kept joints warm. Function became fashion.' Face clip (90-150 sec): #15 or #30 blade — shave the muzzle clean. Show the direction: with the grain on the cheeks, against the grain under the chin for closeness. 'The clean face is the Poodle's signature. It shows the elegant head structure.' Around the eyes: careful, short strokes. 'The dog must be still for this. If the dog moves, you stop. Non-negotiable.' Feet (150-190 sec): shave each foot clean with a #15 blade — between toes, around the nails. 'Clean feet on a Poodle serve the same purpose as clean face — it shows the tight, arched foot structure that's part of the breed standard.' Show the before and after: fuzzy foot → clean, sculpted foot. Hindquarters and rosettes (190-270 sec): shave the hindquarters from the hip to the hock. 'This is where the continental gets its shape.' Leave two round rosettes on each hip — 'The rosettes are shaved with a template or freehand. Freehand takes years of practice. A template takes courage to trust.' Show the rosette shaping: round, symmetrical, positioned over the hip joint. Mane and topknot (270-340 sec): scissor the mane into a rounded shape — 'The mane is the lion's share of the grooming time. Even scissoring, following the body contour, creating a smooth rounded silhouette.' Topknot: banded or free, scissored into a rounded pom above the ears. 'The topknot should balance the face. Too small looks pinched. Too large looks like a hat.' Leg bracelets and tail pom (340-370 sec): shape the bracelets on the lower legs — round, even, matching on all four legs. Tail pom: round, centered on the tail tip. 'The tail pom should be visible from the side when the tail is carried up. Size proportional to the bracelets.' Final reveal (370-360 sec): the finished continental clip — a full 360° turn on the grooming table. Show the silhouette from each angle. 'Forty-five minutes of clipper work. Ninety minutes of scissoring. A dog that looks like a topiary. And honestly? Magnificent.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show the brush-out before the bath** — Wet mats become impossible. The AI sequences pre-bath brushing before any water contact.
2. **Include breed-specific coat diagrams** — Where to clip, scissor, and blend differs by breed. The AI renders zone diagrams when breed_diagram is enabled.
3. **Label every tool and blade number** — "#10 blade" and "slicker brush" mean nothing to beginners without visuals. The AI overlays tool identification when tool_labels is enabled.
4. **Film the before-and-after at matched angles** — Same position, same lighting, dramatic transformation. The AI generates matched-angle comparison frames.
5. **Show the dog's body language** — Stressed signals (lip licking, whale eye, yawning) are teachable moments. The AI highlights behavioral cues when you describe them in the prompt.
