## 1. The Extraction Pipeline

Voice matching is an engineering process, not artistic intuition. Follow this pipeline:

**Input sources** (ranked by signal quality):

1. Recorded conversations / calls with the client (transcribed)
2. Podcast appearances or conference talks (transcribed)
3. Tweet threads and social media posts (raw, unedited voice)
4. Slack messages or internal comms (casual register)
5. Published blog posts (edited, may differ from natural voice)
6. Formal writing (lowest signal, most edited)

The critical insight: start from **transcripts**, not notes. Written notes filter out voice markers. Transcripts preserve how someone actually strings words together, their default rhythms, their verbal tics, their sentence lengths.

**Process**:

1. Collect 3-5 sources, prioritizing transcripts
2. Read each source line-by-line, highlighting voice markers
3. Cross-reference: markers that appear across multiple sources are reliable
4. Markers that appear in only one source may be context-dependent (note the context)
5. Build the voice guide from cross-referenced markers
6. Draft a test paragraph and validate with the client

## 2. Interview Techniques

If you're the one interviewing the client (to extract insights for the newsletter):

**Recorded interview workflow** (best for planned newsletter issues):

- Schedule 30-45 minute calls
- Prepare 5-7 open-ended questions tied to the newsletter topic
- Let the client talk. Your job is to prompt and redirect, not discuss.
- Record everything. Transcribe after.
- The transcript gives you both content (their insights) and voice (their patterns).

**Instant messaging workflow** (best for reactive/timely content):

- Use Slack, WhatsApp, or voice notes
- Send a specific prompt: "What's your take on [event/trend]? Voice note is fine."
- Voice notes are gold: fast for the client, preserve natural speech patterns
- Compile responses into a draft, then polish

**Written brief workflow** (best for white papers, deep-dive pieces):

- Client provides: target audience, objectives, key points, tone preferences
- You provide: draft outline for approval, then full draft
- Best when the client wants control over direction but not execution

## 3. Voice Marker Categories

When analyzing sources, look for these categories:

### Vocabulary

- **Default synonyms**: "big" vs "massive" vs "significant" vs "huge"
- **Technical depth**: Do they say "latency" or "delay"? "Throughput" or "speed"?
- **Fillers in speech**: "honestly", "basically", "literally", "look"
- **Forbidden words**: Terms they never use (often corporate jargon they actively avoid)

### Sentence structure

- **Average length**: Count words in 10-15 representative sentences
- **Complexity**: Simple declarative? Compound with qualifications? Nested clauses?
- **Fragments**: Do they use sentence fragments for emphasis?
- **Questions**: Do they use rhetorical questions? How often?

### Rhythm and pacing

- **Paragraph length**: Short (1-2 sentences) or long (4-5)?
- **Variation**: Consistent length or alternating short/long for emphasis?
- **Lists**: Do they enumerate points or write prose?
- **Emphasis markers**: Bold, italics, ALL CAPS, underscores?

### Tone and register

- **Formality level**: Casual conversational vs. professional vs. academic
- **Humor style**: Dry, self-deprecating, dark, punny, none
- **Confidence level**: Hedged ("I think", "maybe") vs. declarative ("This is", "Full stop")
- **Emotional range**: Reserved, enthusiastic, contrarian, measured

### Metaphor domain

- Where do they pull analogies from? Sports, engineering, cooking, military, music, nature?
- Do they use the same metaphors repeatedly?
- Are metaphors simple or extended?

### Structural habits

- How do they open pieces? (Anecdote, bold claim, question, statistic)
- How do they close? (Summary, call to action, open question, callback)
- Do they use section headers? What style?
- How do they handle transitions?

## 4. Voice Guide Template

```
VOICE GUIDE: [Person's name]
Created: [date]
Sources used: [list]

## Core voice characteristics
- Register: [casual / professional / mixed]
- Avg sentence length: [N] words
- Humor: [style or none]
- Confidence: [hedged / declarative / mixed]

## Vocabulary
- Uses: [word1], [word2], [phrase1]
- Avoids: [word1], [word2]
- Technical level: [first principles / assumes expertise / adapts to audience]

## Structural patterns
- Opens with: [pattern]
- Closes with: [pattern]
- Paragraphs: [length tendency]
- Uses [lists / prose / mixed]

## Signature moves
- [Specific pattern with example from source material]
- [Specific pattern with example]
- [Specific pattern with example]

## Things that would sound wrong
- [Anti-pattern]: would sound like [wrong voice] instead of [right voice]
- [Anti-pattern]: [why it breaks the voice]
```

## 5. Common Pitfalls

**Writing your voice, not theirs.** The most common failure. After a few paragraphs, writers drift back to their own defaults. Use the voice guide as a per-paragraph checklist.

**Over-polishing.** Some clients have a rough, direct voice. Editing for "better writing" can destroy the voice. If they speak in fragments, let the fragments stand.

**Confusing formality with voice.** Making someone sound "more professional" is not voice matching. Some clients are deliberately informal. Respect that.

**Anchoring on one source.** A client's conference talk voice may differ from their newsletter voice. Cross-reference multiple sources.

**Ignoring what they don't say.** Voice is defined as much by absence as presence. If someone never uses exclamation marks, adding one breaks the voice.

## 6. Iteration Protocol

After the first draft:

1. Send to client with specific questions:
   - "Does paragraph 3 sound like you?"
   - "Would you use this word: [word]?"
   - "Is this the metaphor you'd reach for?"
2. Don't ask "does this sound right?" (too vague, gets vague answers)
3. Track corrections in the voice guide (add/modify markers based on feedback)
4. By draft 3, the voice guide should be stable
5. Save the final voice guide for future issues (voice doesn't change often)
