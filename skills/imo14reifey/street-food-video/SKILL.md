# Street Food Video Maker — Street Food and Food Market Videos

The vendor has been making the same dumplings at the same stall for twenty-seven years, the line wraps around the corner at 6 AM, there are no English signs, no Yelp listing, and no Instagram account — and yet somehow this stall appears on every "Top 10 street food" list because a traveler with a phone captured the moment the dumpling hit the oil and the internet did the rest. Street food content is the most universally watchable video genre because it combines three things humans can't look away from: fire, skill, and food — a vendor flipping roti canai at impossible speed, a wok erupting in flames over a jet-engine burner, a knife turning a cucumber into a translucent ribbon in four seconds. The appeal crosses every language barrier because the food tells its own story. This tool transforms handheld market footage, vendor close-ups, and street-food tastings into cinematic street food videos — slow-motion wok tosses with flame trails, overhead shots of assembly-line preparation at speed, first-bite reaction moments with genuine surprise, vendor-portrait segments that show decades of craft in a face and a pair of hands, market-ambiance montages with the sounds and energy of crowded food streets, and price-reveal graphics that make viewers say "HOW MUCH?" in disbelief. Built for travel food vloggers documenting culinary discoveries, street-food channels building vendor-spotlight libraries, tourism boards promoting local food culture, food-market organizers creating promotional event content, food-documentary producers capturing artisan techniques, and anyone who has stood in a market watching a vendor do something extraordinary and thought "I need to film this."

## Example Prompts

### 1. Street Food Tour — Night Market Episode
"Create an 8-minute night market food tour. Opening (0-15 sec): wide shot of the market entrance at dusk — neon signs flickering on, steam rising from dozens of stalls, the roar of crowd noise and sizzling oil. Walk through the entrance: 'Bangkok's Jodd Fairs. 200 stalls. I have room for maybe 7 dishes. Let's make them count.' Stall 1 — Pad Thai (15-90 sec): the wok station. Overhead shot of the vendor's setup — ingredients in steel bowls, the wok seasoned black from years of use. Order placed. Show the full cook: oil in wok, shrimp in (sizzle), noodles tossed (wrist-flick slow-motion), egg cracked directly into the wok (it sizzles on contact), tamarind sauce added, final toss with bean sprouts. Plated in 90 seconds. Price: ฿60 ($1.70) — price graphic on screen. First bite: 'The wok hei on this — you can't get this flavor on a home stove. The noodles are charred at the edges, the shrimp is barely cooked, which means it's perfectly cooked. $1.70.' Stall 3 — Mango Sticky Rice (180-240 sec): the dessert vendor. Show the assembly: sticky rice shaped by hand, mango sliced (the knife work — one continuous cut, the mango fans out), coconut cream ladled over, crispy mung beans sprinkled. 'The coconut cream is warm. The mango is cold. That temperature contrast is intentional and it's genius.' ฿80 ($2.30). Stall 5 — Grilled Pork Skewers (300-360 sec): the charcoal grill — show the smoke, the vendor fanning the coals, skewers lined up across the grate. Close-up of the caramelization — the sugar in the marinade bubbling and browning. Pull a skewer off: the meat tears with visible juice. Dipping sauce: 'This tamarind-chili sauce would make a shoe edible. On this pork, it's unnecessary but I'm using it anyway.' ฿20 per skewer ($0.57). Running total (360-380 sec): animated tally of everything eaten so far — 7 dishes, total cost, comparison to a single dish at a restaurant back home. 'I've eaten seven dishes, I'm uncomfortably full, and I've spent less than a sandwich at the airport.' Market atmosphere closing (380-480 sec): montage of stalls we didn't stop at — the roti lady spinning dough, the coconut ice cream vendor cracking a coconut, the deep-fried insect stall (slow-mo reaction shot of tourists). The crowd, the lights, the energy. 'Night markets aren't about any single dish. They're about 200 stalls and the agreement that eating is the activity, not the interruption.' End card with market name and location pin."

### 2. Single-Vendor Spotlight — Artisan Technique
"Build a 4-minute vendor spotlight on a hand-pulled noodle maker. This is about the craft — one person, one skill, decades of practice. Opening: the vendor's hands — close-up, flour-dusted, stretching a rope of dough. No face yet. 'These hands have pulled 200,000 bowls of noodles. I counted. (I didn't count. But the math works.)' The pull: show the full technique in real time first — fold, stretch, fold, stretch — the dough doubling each time: 1 strand → 2 → 4 → 8 → 16 → 32 → 64 → 128. Count them on screen as they multiply. Then replay the critical moment in slow motion: the stretch at maximum extension, the strands separating like a waterfall. 'From one piece of dough to 128 strands in 8 pulls. Each strand the same thickness. No machine does this.' The vendor (60-120 sec): pull back to show the person. Quick interview or narrated bio: how long they've been doing this, who taught them, how many bowls per day. Show the morning routine — the dough being mixed at 5 AM, the broth that's been simmering overnight, the simple stall setup. The bowl (120-200 sec): follow one bowl from noodle pull to finished dish. Noodles hit the boiling water (2 minutes). Broth ladled over — show the clarity and the aromatics floating. Toppings: sliced beef, green onion, chili oil drizzle (slow-motion of the red oil spreading across the surface). The bowl placed in front of a customer — steam rising, first chopstick pull (the noodles stretch before they break — that's the texture test). First slurp: 'The noodles are elastic, the broth is deep, and the chili oil is the kind that builds heat slowly until you're sweating and ordering another bowl.' Price and context (200-240 sec): price on screen — ¥15 ($2.10). 'A bowl this good in New York is $18 and the noodles came from a machine.' Closing: the vendor pulling another batch, the line getting longer, the simple stall against the city backdrop. 'Mastery doesn't need a Michelin star. Sometimes it just needs a folding table and 30 years of practice.' Warm, documentary aesthetic — handheld camera, natural light, kitchen sounds, no background music during the noodle-pulling sequences (the dough slapping is the soundtrack)."

### 3. Food Market Promo — Tourism/Event
"Produce a 60-second promotional video for a weekend food market. Target: local residents who haven't visited yet. Energy, variety, community. Opening: drone shot descending into the market — stalls arranging at sunrise, vendors unloading (time-lapse of the empty lot transforming into a market, 10 seconds). Quick-fire montage (10-40 sec): one dish from each of 12 stalls — each gets exactly 2 seconds. A pizza emerging from a wood-fired oven. A taco being assembled. Steam rising from a dumpling basket being opened. A crepe being spread on a hot plate. A knife slicing through a brisket (slow-motion, juice running). A scoop of gelato being shaped. A lobster roll being overfilled. A vendor tossing noodles in a wok with a 3-foot flame. A churro being dipped in chocolate. Fresh juice being blended. A cheese being stretched (the pull, the stretch, 3 seconds because cheese pulls deserve it). A vendor handing a dish to a child, who smiles. People (40-50 sec): not just food — faces. Families at picnic tables, a couple sharing a dish, friends clinking drinks, a solo diner photographing their plate (we've all been there), a kid with chocolate on their face, a vendor laughing with a customer. The market is the community. Details (50-55 sec): live music in the corner, a craft booth next to the food, fairy lights at dusk. Closing (55-60 sec): the market at golden hour — full, warm, alive. Logo + 'Every Saturday 10 AM - 8 PM' + location + Instagram handle. Energetic, warm, community feel — fast cuts, saturated colors, natural audio blended with an upbeat acoustic track."

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show the full cook, not just the finished dish** — Raw ingredients hitting hot oil, the wok toss, the flame eruption — the process is the content. The AI sequences your cooking footage into a complete preparation arc when you describe the cooking steps.
2. **Include price with conversion** — "฿60 ($1.70)" on screen creates instant value context for international viewers. The AI generates price-overlay graphics with local and converted currency when you specify both amounts.
3. **Capture the vendor's hands before their face** — Hands kneading, chopping, tossing, plating — show the skill first, then reveal the person. The AI opens vendor segments with hand close-ups when you describe the technique before the portrait.
4. **Use market ambient audio as the base layer** — The crowd noise, the sizzling, the vendor calls — these sounds are half the atmosphere. The AI mixes natural market audio under the music when food_asmr is enabled, creating an immersive soundscape.
5. **End with the total cost comparison** — "Seven dishes, $12 total" compared to a single restaurant dish back home is the viral stat. The AI generates an animated cost-tally graphic at the point in your prompt where you describe the total.
