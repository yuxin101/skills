# Nutrition Tips Video Maker — Diet and Healthy Eating Education Videos

The label says "all natural," the influencer says it's a superfood, the package has a picture of a green leaf on it, and the ingredient list contains seventeen items, four of which are different names for sugar — because the food industry discovered that splitting sugar into maltodextrin, dextrose, brown rice syrup, and "evaporated cane juice" keeps any single one from appearing as the first ingredient. Nutrition content on YouTube is a battlefield between registered dietitians citing peer-reviewed research and wellness influencers who learned everything they know from another influencer who learned it from a supplement brand's marketing department. The creators who build lasting trust are the ones who cite their sources, show the nutrition label instead of just talking about it, and say "the research is mixed" when the research is actually mixed instead of pretending certainty for engagement. This tool transforms nutrition science, meal-planning strategies, and dietary education into polished videos — animated macronutrient breakdowns showing protein-carb-fat ratios as filling bar charts, grocery-store walkthrough comparisons with price-per-gram calculations, meal-prep sequences with calorie and macro labels on each container, ingredient-label close-ups with problematic additives highlighted, portion-size visual comparisons using household objects, and the evidence-based approach that separates nutrition education from diet-culture marketing. Built for registered dietitians creating patient-education libraries, nutrition coaches building course content, healthy-cooking channels adding educational segments, fitness creators explaining the nutrition side, meal-prep influencers documenting their weekly process, and anyone who understands that "eat less processed food" is simultaneously the simplest and hardest nutrition advice to follow.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Label Reading — How to Decode Nutrition Labels
"Create a 7-minute nutrition label reading tutorial. Opening (0-15 sec): a grocery shelf — 'Two granola bars. One says "healthy." One says "natural." One has 22 grams of sugar. The other has 24. The label tells you the truth the front of the package never will.' Serving size (15-60 sec): zoom into a label — 'First line: serving size. This bag of chips says 140 calories. Sounds reasonable. Serving size: 12 chips. The bag contains 6 servings. If you eat the bag — and you will — that's 840 calories.' Show the math animated on screen. 'The serving size is a legal fiction that makes the numbers look small.' Calories and macros (60-150 sec): animated breakdown — protein, carbs, fat. 'Protein: 4 calories per gram. Carbs: 4 calories per gram. Fat: 9 calories per gram. That's why a tablespoon of olive oil (14g fat = 126 calories) has more calories than a tablespoon of sugar (12g carbs = 48 calories).' Show three products side by side with macro breakdowns highlighted. Sugar detective (150-250 sec): the ingredient list — 'Ingredients are listed by weight, most to least. If sugar is first, the product is mostly sugar.' But: 'Companies split sugar into 5 different names so none of them is first.' Show a label with highlighted sugar aliases: high fructose corn syrup, dextrose, maltodextrin, brown rice syrup, evaporated cane juice, agave nectar. 'Count them all. Add them together. That's your real sugar content.' Show a "healthy" yogurt with 28g sugar — 'That's 7 teaspoons. A glazed donut has 10g. This yogurt has nearly 3x the sugar and a picture of a strawberry field on the label.' The % Daily Value (250-320 sec): 'Based on a 2,000-calorie diet. If you eat 1,600 or 2,500, the percentages are wrong for you.' Show the recalculation. Rule of thumb: '5% or less = low. 20% or more = high. Sodium at 38% means this single serving gives you over a third of your daily salt.' Red flags (320-380 sec): animated list — partially hydrogenated oils (trans fat even if label says 0g — 'If serving size is small enough, they can round down to 0'), artificial colors by number (Red 40, Yellow 5), 'natural flavors' (a legal term that means almost nothing). Green flags: short ingredient lists, ingredients you recognize, whole foods listed first. Closing: side-by-side of two seemingly similar products — 'Same shelf. Same price. One has 6 ingredients you can pronounce. One has 34. The label is the only honest part of the package.'"

### 2. Meal Prep — Weekly Prep for Busy People
"Build a 6-minute weekly meal prep video with macro counts. Opening: Sunday morning, kitchen counter with ingredients — 'Four hours of cooking today means zero decisions about food Monday through Friday. Here's the plan for 2,000 calories and 150g protein per day.' The plan (0-40 sec): weekly meal grid on screen — breakfast, lunch, dinner, snacks for 5 days. Each meal labeled with calories and macros. 'Every meal designed to be reheated in 3 minutes or eaten cold. No "assemble a salad at your desk" nonsense.' Protein batch (40-120 sec): chicken breast — season 5 lbs (show the seasoning: salt, pepper, garlic powder, paprika), sheet pan at 425°F, 22 minutes, internal temp 165°F (show the thermometer). While that cooks: ground turkey in a skillet for taco bowls. 'Two proteins. Fifteen minutes of active work. Ten meals covered.' Show the cooked proteins being portioned into containers. Carb batch (120-180 sec): rice in the rice cooker (4 cups dry = enough for the week), sweet potatoes cubed and roasted alongside the chicken. 'Cook your carbs in bulk. Individual portions of rice take the same time as a week's worth.' Veggie batch (180-240 sec): broccoli, bell peppers, zucchini — toss with olive oil, roast at 400°F for 20 minutes. 'Roasting > steaming for flavor and texture. Steamed broccoli is punishment. Roasted broccoli is a choice.' Assembly (240-320 sec): the assembly line — 10 containers on the counter. Each one: protein + carb + vegetables. Macro label overlay on each container: Chicken + rice + broccoli = 480 cal, 42g protein, 45g carbs, 12g fat. Turkey taco bowl = 510 cal, 38g protein, 52g carbs, 14g fat. Stack and refrigerate — show the fridge with organized containers by day. Breakfast prep (320-360 sec): overnight oats — 5 jars assembled in 3 minutes. Macro label: 380 cal, 18g protein, 52g carbs, 10g fat. 'Add protein powder to the oats for an extra 25g protein.' Closing: the fridge stocked, weekly totals on screen — 'Sunday: 4 hours. Monday-Friday: 3 minutes per meal. Weekly grocery cost: $62. That's $12.40 per day, 3 meals plus snacks, at 2,000 calories and 150g protein.'"

### 3. Myth Busting — 5 Nutrition Myths Debunked
"Produce a 5-minute nutrition myth-busting video. No hedging — direct answers with research citations. Opening: 'Five things you believe about nutrition that are either wrong, outdated, or more complicated than the internet told you.' Myth 1 — Eating fat makes you fat (0-60 sec): 'Excess calories make you fat. Fat is 9 cal/gram vs 4 for carbs and protein, so it's easier to overeat — but the macronutrient itself doesn't cause fat gain.' Show the study: 'A 2020 meta-analysis of 53 studies found no difference in weight loss between low-fat and low-carb diets when calories were equated.' Citation on screen. 'Eat avocados. They're fine.' Myth 2 — You need 8 glasses of water per day (60-120 sec): 'The original recommendation from 1945 included water from food. A tomato is 95% water. An apple is 86%. You're drinking more water than you think.' The real guideline: 'Drink when you're thirsty. If your urine is light yellow, you're hydrated. If it's dark, drink more. That's it.' Myth 3 — Breakfast is the most important meal (120-180 sec): 'The studies that showed breakfast eaters were healthier had a confound: people who eat breakfast also tend to exercise more, smoke less, and drink less alcohol.' Intermittent fasting research: 'Skipping breakfast and eating from noon to 8 PM shows equivalent or better metabolic markers in multiple controlled trials.' The truth: 'Eat breakfast if you're hungry. Skip it if you're not. It's a meal, not a moral imperative.' Myth 4 — Detox diets cleanse toxins (180-240 sec): 'Your liver and kidneys detox your body 24/7. They don't need a $50 juice cleanse to do their job.' Show the liver's detox pathway animated. 'The word "toxins" in a marketing context is meaningless. Ask: which toxin? How is it measured? What's the mechanism of removal? If the answer is vague, it's marketing.' Myth 5 — Carbs are bad (240-300 sec): 'Your brain runs on glucose. Your muscles store glycogen for energy. Carbs are not optional for most humans.' The distinction: 'A sweet potato and a candy bar are both carbs. The sweet potato has fiber, potassium, and vitamins. The candy bar has sugar and regret. Context matters.' Closing: 'Nutrition is complicated. Anyone who gives you a simple rule for a complex system is either simplifying for your benefit or selling you something. Usually the second one.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Show the actual nutrition label on screen** — Zoomed-in labels with highlighted sections are more educational than narrated numbers alone. The AI frames label close-ups with animated highlights on the key data points you describe.
2. **Use visual portion comparisons** — "A serving of peanut butter is 2 tablespoons" means more when shown next to an actual tablespoon. The AI generates portion-size comparisons using household objects when you describe serving sizes.
3. **Cite the research with author/year** — "A 2020 meta-analysis of 53 studies" is credible; "studies show" is not. The AI renders citation overlays when citations is enabled and you provide specific study references.
4. **Include macro labels on meal-prep containers** — Calories, protein, carbs, and fat per container lets viewers fit the meals into their own plans. The AI overlays macro graphics on container footage when macro_labels is enabled.
5. **Debunk with the mechanism, not just the conclusion** — "Your liver detoxes 24/7 via these pathways" explains WHY detox diets are unnecessary. The AI pairs your mechanism explanations with animated biological diagrams.
