# Crowdfunding Video Maker — Kickstarter and Campaign Pitch Videos

The crowdfunding campaign page has been live for six hours, the creator has refreshed the dashboard forty-three times, and the counter shows $127 from three backers — two of whom are the creator's parents and one is their college roommate who backed it "for moral support but honestly doesn't understand what it does." The crowdfunding video is the single most important asset in determining whether the next backer is a stranger who discovered the campaign through the platform's algorithm, because Kickstarter and Indiegogo both confirm that campaigns with videos raise significantly more than those without, and the quality of that video — specifically the first 15 seconds — determines whether a casual browser becomes a pledged backer or a bounce statistic. This tool transforms crowdfunding campaigns into polished pitch videos — problem-demonstration openings that make the viewer feel the need before the solution appears, product-revelation sequences showing the item in use rather than as a render, founder-story segments building the trust that convinces strangers to give money to someone they've never met, reward-tier breakdowns showing exactly what each pledge level delivers, timeline-and-fulfillment transparency addressing the "when will I get it" question every backer asks, and the social-proof integration that shows early supporters, press coverage, or prototype testing to reduce the perceived risk of backing something that doesn't exist yet. Built for hardware creators launching products on Kickstarter, indie game developers funding through crowdfunding, creative projects seeking patron support, social-impact campaigns building community funding, product designers validating market demand through pre-orders, and anyone who needs to convince a stranger that this thing deserves to exist and their $49 pledge is what makes it happen.

## Example Prompts

### 1. Product Launch — Kickstarter Campaign Video
"Create a 3-minute Kickstarter campaign video for a smart water bottle. The problem (0-25 sec): a person at their desk — the water bottle untouched since morning. 'You bought a water bottle to drink more water. It's 3 PM and you haven't touched it. The bottle did its job — it held water. It didn't do your job — reminding you to drink it.' The stats: '75% of Americans are chronically dehydrated. Not because water is unavailable. Because remembering to drink it competes with every other thing demanding your attention.' The product (25-80 sec): reveal the bottle — clean design, subtle LED ring around the cap. 'This is [Product]. It tracks your hydration, glows when you need to drink, and syncs with your health app.' Show it working: the LED pulsing gently — blue for hydrated, amber for reminder, red for dehydrated. The person picks it up, drinks, the LED returns to blue. 'The reminder is passive — a gentle glow, not an alarm. It works because it doesn't interrupt. It suggests.' The app: quick screen recording — daily intake tracked, weekly trends, personalized target based on weight and activity level. 'The bottle knows how much you've consumed by weight sensor. No buttons to press. No manual logging. Drink from it and it tracks.' What makes it different (80-120 sec): comparison — 'Smart water bottles exist. They all require you to press a button or open an app to check your hydration. That's not solving the problem — it's adding a step to the problem.' The differentiator: passive tracking + ambient reminders. 'The glow happens whether or not you're looking at your phone. It sits on your desk and communicates with light, not notifications.' Materials: double-wall stainless steel, BPA-free, dishwasher safe (except the smart cap). Battery: 30 days per charge. 'It's a premium water bottle first and a smart device second.' The founder (120-150 sec): the person behind it — direct to camera. 'I'm [name]. I designed this because I was the person with the untouched water bottle every afternoon.' Show the prototyping process — 3D prints, early models, the evolution from ugly prototype to final design. 'This is prototype 1. It looked terrible and worked perfectly. This is prototype 7. It looks beautiful and works perfectly.' Rewards (150-170 sec): the tiers — animated. Early Bird: $39 (retail $59). Standard: $45. Bundle (2-pack): $75. 'The early-bird price is the crowdfunding deal — 34% off retail, available only during the campaign.' Fulfillment (170-180 sec): 'Manufacturing is ready. We have a production partner in [location]. Estimated delivery: [month].' Show the factory or the production samples. 'We're not pre-prototyping. We're pre-ordering. The product is finished. The funding enables the production run.'"

### 2. Creative Project — Film/Book/Music Campaign
"Build a 2-minute campaign video for an independent film seeking funding. Opening (0-10 sec): a scene from the film — the most visually compelling 10 seconds of existing footage. No context, no title, just the image and the emotion. 'Start with proof that this film is real and worth watching.' The filmmaker (10-50 sec): direct to camera — the director. 'This film is about [one-sentence logline]. We've been working on it for two years. We have a cast, a crew, and 60% of the footage shot. We need your help to finish it.' Show the behind-the-scenes: the set, the crew working, the director with the actors. 'Crowdfunding a creative project is selling belief. The viewer needs to believe the creator can finish what they started. Show the evidence.' The story (50-90 sec): the film's trailer — 40 seconds of the best footage, edited to tease without revealing. Show the tone, the performances, the visual quality. 'The trailer is the proof of concept. It answers: is this worth watching? If the answer is yes, the next question is: is this worth funding?' Why crowdfunding (90-120 sec): 'Independent film means independent funding. The studios passed because [honest reason — too niche, too different, too new]. That's exactly why this story matters.' The specific need: 'We need $45,000 to complete post-production: editing, sound design, original score, and color grading.' Show the breakdown — pie chart animated. 'Every dollar goes to finishing the film. No salaries. No overhead. Pure production.' Rewards and close (120-120 sec): digital download, screening invitation, producer credit, set visit for the final shoot day. 'Back this film and your name appears in the credits.' End on the strongest footage. The title. The campaign URL."

### 3. Social Impact — Community Project Campaign
"Produce a 2-minute campaign video for a community garden fundraiser. Opening: the lot — empty, overgrown, littered. 'This is the corner of [Street] and [Street]. Right now it's a vacant lot. In 6 months, we want it to be a community garden feeding 200 families.' The community (0-40 sec): the neighborhood — show the people, the existing community spaces, the food desert reality. 'The nearest grocery store is 2.3 miles away. For families without cars, that's a bus ride with grocery bags. A community garden on this corner puts fresh food within walking distance.' The faces: 3 community members, each 8 seconds. 'I would grow tomatoes and peppers — the vegetables my kids actually eat.' 'I grew up farming. I haven't had my hands in soil in 20 years.' 'This lot has been empty since I was a kid. It's time it became something.' The plan (40-80 sec): animated site plan — the lot transforming into garden plots, paths, a tool shed, a community gathering area. 'Forty raised beds. A composting station. A tool library. A gathering space for workshops.' The budget: $12,000. Soil and raised beds: $4,000. Irrigation: $2,500. Fencing and paths: $2,000. Tools: $1,500. First-year seeds and plants: $1,000. Community events: $1,000. 'Every dollar builds something permanent.' The ask (80-100 sec): '$12,000 builds a garden that feeds 200 families for years. That's $60 per family served.' Rewards: $25 gets a thank-you seed packet. $50 gets a named plant marker. $100 gets a dedicated raised bed. $500 gets a bench with a dedication plaque. Close (100-120 sec): the empty lot again — then a dissolve to the animated finished garden. 'This is what this corner looks like today. This is what it looks like with your help.' Campaign URL. 'Every garden starts with one seed. Be the first.'"

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Open with the problem, not the product** — The viewer needs to feel the pain before they see the solution. The AI structures problem-first narratives.
2. **Show the product working, not just rendered** — Real footage of a prototype beats a 3D render. Physical proof reduces backer risk perception.
3. **Include the founder on camera** — Backers fund people, not just products. The AI includes founder segments for trust building.
4. **Animate reward tiers clearly** — Each tier, its price, and what it includes — visually. The AI renders tier breakdowns when reward_tiers is enabled.
5. **Address fulfillment directly** — "When will I get it?" is every backer's first question. The AI includes timeline and manufacturing-readiness segments.
