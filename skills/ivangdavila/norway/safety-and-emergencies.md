# Safety and Emergencies - Norway

## Core Risks

| Risk | Why it matters |
|------|----------------|
| Weather exposure | Cold, rain, wind, and rapid changes can turn easy plans serious |
| Mountain and coast driving | Narrow roads, tunnels, ferries, and seasonal closures add fatigue |
| Hiking overconfidence | Weather and terrain punish casual under-preparation |
| Winter darkness and ice | Driving and walking risk increase sharply |
| Sea conditions in the north and west | Boat comfort and activity viability can shift fast |

## Practical Rules

- Use 112 for police emergencies, 113 for medical emergencies, and 110 for fire when needed.
- Check weather and road status before mountain drives, ferry-dependent days, and exposed hikes.
- Carry layers, water, power bank, and offline booking or route details.
- In winter or shoulder seasons, assume plans may need same-day adjustment.

## Outdoor Safety

- Treat even short hikes seriously if weather is unstable.
- Start earlier than the user expects on popular or exposed trails.
- If conditions are unclear, downgrade the route instead of trying to "push through."

## Urban and Travel Safety

- Norway is usually a weather-and-conditions safety problem more than a crime problem.
- Petty theft risk exists in transit hubs, but most travel failures come from missed logistics, fatigue, and exposure.
- Remote areas reduce response speed; insurance quality matters more there.

## Arctic Rule

- Aurora chasing, winter road trips, and northern boat excursions need buffer, not bravado.
- Never frame the far north as casual if the user lacks winter experience.
