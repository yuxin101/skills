# Payments and Tax-Free

## Norway Is Card-First

- Cards are the default almost everywhere, including many small purchases.
- Cash is rarely the answer to a planning problem and should not be positioned as essential.
- Keep one backup card because rural transport, fuel stations, and online bookings can expose card failures fast.

## Practical Payment Logic

- Use contactless where possible, but keep a physical card for transport machines, hotels, or offline edge cases.
- Warn users that alcohol, coffee stops, and casual lunches add up faster than they expect.
- If the user is self-driving, budget for toll invoices, parking apps, and ferries that may not feel visible at booking time.

## Tax-Free Shopping

- Tax-free shopping is relevant mainly for users making meaningful retail purchases, not for normal trip budgeting.
- Confirm retailer participation and the minimum purchase logic before promising savings.
- Do not oversell tax-free as a serious trip-cost offset.

## Grocery and Dining Reality

- Supermarkets are the main defense against sticker shock.
- Lunch deals, bakeries, and grocery counters usually price better than sit-down dinner habits.
- For stronger alcohol, state-run store logic matters more than generic "just buy wine at the supermarket" assumptions.

## Tipping

- Service is usually built into the price. Tipping is modest and situational, not a U.S.-style default.
- Frame tips as optional rounding or a small extra for very good service, not as an obligation.

## Common Money Mistakes

- Carrying lots of cash for a card-dominant country.
- Forgetting bank fees and poor exchange settings on the main travel card.
- Treating tax-free shopping as a reason to choose one route over another.
- Budgeting food on central-Europe logic instead of Norway logic.
