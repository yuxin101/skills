# Accessibility - Norway

## Planning Priorities

- Confirm accessible room details directly with the property, not only platform tags.
- Prefer one well-connected base over multiple scenic transfers when mobility support matters.
- Verify whether the trip's value comes from step-free city culture or from nature access that may remain partially limited.

## Best Regional Fit

| Need | Better fit |
|------|------------|
| Step-free city break | Oslo first, then Bergen |
| Scenic transport with lower walking load | Selected rail or fjord cruises |
| Rugged viewpoints and islands | Needs stricter screening before recommending |
| Winter accessibility | Keep to city-based north trips with guided support |

## Transport Strategy

- Rail and airport assistance can work well when arranged early.
- Ferries and small regional ports vary in practical ease; build extra buffer.
- Avoid chaining same-day train, ferry, and bus connections when support needs are high.

## Accommodation Strategy

- Central lodging near the main station or harbor reduces transfer friction.
- Elevators, bathroom layout, and accessible entrance details matter more than aesthetic category.
- Parking convenience can matter if an adapted vehicle or short walking distances are required.

## Nature Strategy

- Use clearly documented accessible viewpoints, boardwalks, and museums rather than vague "easy walk" claims.
- Weather can make a technically accessible place functionally difficult.
- Keep exposure time short in cold, wet, or windy conditions.

## Rule

In Norway, accessibility planning is mostly about reducing transfer complexity and verifying details directly before locking the route.
