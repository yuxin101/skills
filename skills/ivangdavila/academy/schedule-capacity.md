# Schedule and Capacity

Most academies do not have a marketing problem first. They have a capacity design problem that makes growth unstable.

## Capacity Equation

Track capacity as:
- seats available
- seats sold
- minimum viable class size
- teacher hours available
- room or live-slot availability

Revenue plans that ignore one of these will break later.

## Before Opening a New Group

Check:
- who teaches it
- when it runs
- where it happens
- how many students are needed to keep margin healthy
- whether it steals students from another group

## Timetable Rules

- protect prime-time slots for highest-demand offers
- keep transitions realistic between classes, rooms, or calls
- do not create dozens of edge-case schedules that only work for one family
- standardize make-up rules so support does not negotiate every absence manually

## Utilization Signals

Watch for:
- classes consistently near zero occupancy
- one teacher overloaded while others stay underused
- too many cancellations in a specific slot
- waitlists in one level and empty seats in another

These are structural signals, not just daily annoyances.

## Multi-Program Conflicts

Programs compete for:
- rooms
- teacher attention
- admin follow-up
- student energy and family schedule

When conflicts appear, rank offers by:
1. margin
2. strategic importance
3. retention value
4. staffing difficulty

## Decision Rule

If a scheduling fix makes operations simpler but hurts the student experience badly, it will return later as churn.

Choose the simplest plan that still feels reliable and fair to students and teachers.
