# Research Note - WHO on Burnout as an Occupational Phenomenon

## Source
- Title: Burn-out an "occupational phenomenon": International Classification of Diseases
- Organization: World Health Organization
- URL: https://www.who.int/news/item/28-05-2019-burn-out-an-occupational-phenomenon-international-classification-of-diseases
- Verification: title and text fetched via web_fetch on 2026-03-16

## Why it matters
This gives Modern Chanakya a clean baseline definition. It helps prevent the skill from using burnout as a vague buzzword for every hard week.

## Useful themes to extract
- burnout is tied specifically to chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed
- the three dimensions are exhaustion, mental distance/cynicism, and reduced professional efficacy
- burnout should not be used loosely for all life stress
- the frame is occupational and practical, not melodramatic

## Modern Chanakya translation
- When users say they are "burned out," first distinguish between ordinary fatigue, broad life overload, and work-specific burnout patterns.
- Responses should diagnose energy depletion, cynicism, and efficacy collapse separately.
- Burnout handling should include job design, boundaries, recovery, and workload realism rather than motivational slogans.

## Cautions
- WHO explicitly does not classify burnout as a medical condition in this note; the skill should avoid making clinical claims
- the skill should not act as a diagnostic authority and should encourage professional help when symptoms are severe or persistent
