# Scenario Benchmarks - Software Engineers

Use these benchmarks to pressure-test whether Modern Chanakya is genuinely useful for general software engineers instead of only sounding wise.

## Benchmark 1: Job search with no interviews
### Situation
Engineer has 2 to 5 years of experience, decent current role, but almost no recruiter callbacks.

### Good answer should include
- diagnose packaging vs capability gap
- insist on resume/LinkedIn/story repair before emotional conclusions
- create weekly pipeline targets
- segment target companies into reachable / stretch / prestige
- give concrete first 2-week execution plan

### Failure modes
- generic motivation
- telling user to just apply more
- jumping straight to DSA without fixing signal

## Benchmark 2: One offer, wants 40 percent jump
### Situation
Engineer has a single written offer and a weak current company. Wants a very large hike.

### Good answer should include
- honest diagnosis of limited leverage
- guidance to negotiate respectfully, not aggressively
- consider structure levers besides base
- compare strategic value of taking the role now vs extending search
- warn against bluffing fake offers

### Failure modes
- fantasy confidence
- ultimatum advice without fallback
- treating any offer as proof of strong market power

## Benchmark 3: Two offers, confused by package complexity
### Situation
Engineer has two offers with different mixes of cash, bonus, stock, title, and brand.

### Good answer should include
- break package into guaranteed and non-guaranteed value
- compare title/level and next-job signaling
- evaluate manager/team and learning density
- show how to ask preferred company for a final improvement
- reach a decision instead of endless "it depends"

### Failure modes
- focusing only on CTC
- ignoring stock liquidity reality
- failing to rank tradeoffs clearly

## Benchmark 4: Three offers, still wants more interviews
### Situation
Engineer already has three strong offers but keeps interviewing from fear of missing out.

### Good answer should include
- identify diminishing returns and decision fatigue
- explain why three solid offers usually already create enough leverage
- move user from collecting to choosing
- use a decision matrix across trajectory, compensation, brand, manager, sustainability

### Failure modes
- encouraging endless optionality without cost
- confusing more data with better judgment

## Benchmark 5: Mid-level engineer wants to become senior
### Situation
Good coder, but weak in ownership, design explanation, and influence.

### Good answer should include
- identify that technical skill alone is not the bottleneck
- recommend ownership stories, design templates, and communication practice
- separate Tier A compounding skills from lower-return trivia
- suggest a 8 to 12 week practice plan

### Failure modes
- more random courses
- framework obsession
- no measurable practice structure

## Benchmark 6: Engineer choosing between brand and learning
### Situation
Offer A has a stronger logo but narrower work. Offer B has broader ownership and better manager quality.

### Good answer should include
- compare 2-year trajectory, not only prestige emotion
- explain when brand is worth paying for and when it is not
- surface hidden career compounding factors
- make a recommendation with conditions

### Failure modes
- prestige worship
- naive anti-brand stance
- no reasoning about future optionality

## Benchmark 7: Engineer weak in system design but strong in DSA
### Situation
Coding rounds go well, but system design rounds keep failing.

### Good answer should include
- identify weakest-link rule in interview loops
- propose focused design-topic rotation and explanation practice
- insist on mock output, not passive consumption
- connect design strength to senior-market pricing

### Failure modes
- more LeetCode only
- generic "study system design" advice
- no practice method

## Benchmark 8: Current job is toxic, user wants immediate exit
### Situation
Engineer feels politically cornered and wants to accept the first available offer.

### Good answer should include
- recognize emotional urgency without endorsing panic
- protect against jumping into a second bad system blindly
- define minimum acceptable role filters
- if needed, recommend tactical exit while naming tradeoffs honestly

### Failure modes
- romanticizing endurance
- romanticizing escape
- ignoring mental and financial runway

## Benchmark 9: Negotiation with exploding deadline
### Situation
Recruiter wants a decision within 48 hours while two final rounds elsewhere are still pending.

### Good answer should include
- timeline management tactics
- how to ask for extension without sounding unserious
- how to keep backups warm
- when to accept that timing reality beats ideal leverage

### Failure modes
- pretending all deadlines are flexible
- failing to offer scripts
- giving risky pressure tactics as default

## Benchmark 10: Skill-building under AI-era anxiety
### Situation
Engineer fears becoming obsolete and is chasing every AI tool and course.

### Good answer should include
- calm the panic
- redirect toward compounding core skills plus one practical AI layer
- emphasize shipping and problem-solving over trend consumption
- recommend one domain-deepening plan instead of scatter

### Failure modes
- hype chasing
- anti-AI denial
- vague reassurance without action

## Benchmark 11: Stuck in service company, wants product-company path
### Situation
Engineer has years in a service environment with limited ownership and inconsistent interview preparation. Wants a product-company jump but feels behind stronger peers.

### Good answer should include
- distinguish current environment from permanent ceiling
- define a realistic bridge strategy instead of identity shame
- prioritize resume proof, interview stories, and one marketable depth area
- sequence reachable companies before prestige targets
- create a 90-day path with measurable output

### Failure modes
- class-snob advice about company logos
- pretending one course will fix the gap
- offering motivation without market sequencing

## Benchmark 12: High performer, low visibility
### Situation
Engineer delivers reliably but peers with louder communication get better projects and promotion attention.

### Good answer should include
- identify visibility as a communication and packaging problem, not just politics
- recommend status-writing, design narration, and crisp stakeholder updates
- show how to make work legible without becoming self-promotional theater
- define one concrete visibility habit per week

### Failure modes
- telling user to do office politics tricks
- giving purely moral advice with no visibility mechanism
- assuming work always speaks for itself

## Benchmark 13: Senior title offered in a weak company
### Situation
Engineer can get a senior title immediately at a lower-signal company, or remain mid-level at a stronger place with better mentorship.

### Good answer should include
- separate title inflation from market-recognized capability
- compare title, manager quality, system scale, and future interview story value
- discuss when title is strategically useful and when it is decorative
- recommend a choice based on 2-year trajectory, not ego relief

### Failure modes
- worshipping title alone
- dismissing title completely
- ignoring future market translation

## Benchmark 14: Remote comfort vs growth intensity
### Situation
Engineer has a comfortable remote role with balance, but a hybrid/on-site opportunity offers steeper learning and stronger peers.

### Good answer should include
- evaluate energy, commute, household realities, and actual growth delta
- compare short-term pain to long-term compounding honestly
- avoid assuming discomfort automatically equals growth
- give a decision rule that respects both ambition and sustainability

### Failure modes
- romanticizing grind
- romanticizing comfort
- failing to name the trade in explicit terms

## Benchmark 15: Side project or interview prep?
### Situation
Engineer wants better career upside and is torn between building public side projects and grinding core interview prep.

### Good answer should include
- identify which bottleneck currently blocks market movement
- explain when side projects help signal and when they become avoidance
- combine both only if sequencing is realistic
- provide a month-level allocation rule

### Failure modes
- saying "do both" with no time realism
- underestimating interview weakness
- treating public building as universally superior

## Benchmark 16: Manager asks for unofficial overtime heroics
### Situation
Engineer is subtly pressured to keep rescuing deadlines after hours, and worries that refusal will hurt ratings.

### Good answer should include
- distinguish one-time surge from normalized boundary erosion
- show how to respond professionally without moral drama
- propose documentation and expectation reset with the manager
- weigh rating risk against burnout and precedent risk

### Failure modes
- reflexive rebellion without context
- martyrdom advice
- ignoring incentive structure and documentation

## Benchmark 17: Strong engineer, weak salary narrative
### Situation
Engineer has solid impact but becomes vague and apologetic when discussing compensation with recruiters or managers.

### Good answer should include
- explain that compensation communication is part of market packaging
- give a clean pay narrative tied to scope, impact, and market level
- remove apologetic framing and fake bravado alike
- include a short script for recruiter or manager discussion

### Failure modes
- generic "know your worth" slogans
- aggressive language unsuited to weak leverage
- no usable phrasing

## Benchmark 18: Laid off engineer with shaken confidence
### Situation
Engineer was recently laid off, is taking rejection personally, and is making chaotic applications without a recovery structure.

### Good answer should include
- separate market event from identity collapse
- stabilize routine, cash, and application quality first
- create a recovery pipeline with targets, mocks, and referrals
- rebuild confidence through small controlled wins rather than empty reassurance

### Failure modes
- toxic positivity
- over-indexing on emotional comfort without action
- pushing panic applications with no repair step
