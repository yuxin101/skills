# Compensation and Offer Negotiation

Use this framework when the user wants more compensation, better package structure, or clearer leverage in a job move.

## First principle
Compensation negotiation is usually won before the negotiation call.
It depends on:
- market signal
- interview performance
- alternatives
- timing
- calm execution

## The four leverage states

### 1. No offer, weak current position
Advice:
- do not obsess over negotiation scripts yet
- first build interview pipeline and packaging
- focus on becoming wanted

### 2. One offer, weak fallback
Advice:
- negotiate, but modestly and respectfully
- optimize for structure, joining bonus, title, or review timing if base cannot move much
- avoid hard ultimatums unless willing to lose the offer

### 3. One offer, decent current job
Advice:
- stronger leverage because you can genuinely stay
- ask for improvement with calm confidence
- do not signal desperation

### 4. Multiple solid offers
Advice:
- this is the best negotiation environment
- compare like with like
- use concrete alternatives, not vague market talk
- protect relationships while still asking clearly

## What to negotiate besides base salary
Many engineers negotiate only total CTC and miss the structure.

Possible levers:
- base salary
- joining/sign-on bonus
- equity / ESOP quantity
- refresh cycles
- performance review timing
- title / level mapping
- relocation or remote-work support
- notice-period flexibility
- joining date
- guaranteed first-year minimums where relevant

## Engineer package reading checklist
Ask:
- What part is guaranteed cash?
- What part is variable or discretionary?
- Is the stock liquid or mostly storytelling?
- Is the title actually stronger, or only cosmetically flattering?
- Will the level affect next-job pricing?
- Does the package create golden handcuffs without real upside?

## Negotiation preparation
Before the conversation, collect:
- target compensation range
- minimum acceptable outcome
- ideal outcome
- strongest reasons you are worth more
- competing options or credible fallback
- which levers matter most

The user should know the difference between:
- wish
- target
- walk-away point

## What to say
Good negotiation language is:
- calm
- specific
- easy to help
- non-threatening unless absolutely necessary

### Strong patterns
- "I’m excited about the role. Based on the scope, my interview process, and the market range I’m seeing, I was hoping we could get closer to X."
- "Compensation matters, but I’m looking at the full package. Is there flexibility on base, sign-on, or level?"
- "I do have another process / option at a stronger level, so I want to make sure I evaluate this fairly."
- "If base is constrained, what other parts of the package can we improve?"

### Weak patterns
- threatening too early
- bluffing fake offers
- oversharing financial need
- arguing emotionally about fairness
- talking too much after making the ask

## Silence rule
After making a reasonable ask, stop filling the air.
Over-explaining weakens posture.

## Multiple-offer usage rule
If the user has multiple offers, use them to create clarity first, pressure second.

Sequence:
1. understand real package differences
2. decide preferred company
3. ask preferred company to improve
4. keep backup options warm
5. avoid burning trust for small gains

## Deciding whether to reveal exact competing numbers
Usually:
- reveal enough to establish seriousness
- do not reveal more than needed
- exact numbers can be useful when they strongly support your case
- avoid turning the conversation into a pure auction if long-term trust matters

## When not to push harder
Do not keep squeezing when:
- the company has moved meaningfully already
- the role is uniquely good for trajectory
- the recruiter is trying to help and policy limits are real
- a small extra gain risks losing a major strategic win

## When to push harder
Push more when:
- package is clearly below market and the company wants you badly
- other offers are materially better
- scope and expectations imply a higher level than offered
- the company is using urgency to suppress comparison

## Timeline management
Negotiation is partly calendar warfare.

Useful moves:
- ask for a little more time if needed
- cluster final rounds when possible
- avoid exploding offers too early in the search
- do not let one company force premature closure if better processes are close

## Decision rule: best package vs best path
The best package is not always the best path.
Use this ranking order when the user is confused:
1. role trajectory
2. manager/team quality
3. compensation structure and upside
4. brand and learning density
5. convenience factors

## Errors Modern Chanakya should prevent
- negotiating from fear
- chasing vanity CTC
- accepting weak equity stories blindly
- using aggression when calm specificity would work better
- losing a strategically superior role over ego-sized differences

## Final counsel style
Tell the user the truth:
- If leverage is weak, say it directly.
- If they have real leverage, tell them to use it with discipline.
- If three strong offers already exist, shift focus from collecting more to choosing better.
