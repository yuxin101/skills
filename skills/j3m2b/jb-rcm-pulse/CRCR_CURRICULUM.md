## THE 3 SEGMENTS OF THE REVENUE CYCLE

```
PRE-SERVICE          →    TIME OF SERVICE     →    POST-SERVICE
(Before the visit)        (During the visit)       (After the visit)

Scheduling                Point-of-service             Claims submission
Pre-registration          collections                  Payment posting

Financial counseling     Financial conversations      AR follow-up
Pre-authorization        Informed consent             Patient collections

```

Every unit in this curriculum maps to one of these three segments.

## UNIT 1: Revenue Cycle in Health Care (Week 1)
**Theme: Big picture — how the pieces connect**

### Learning Objectives
- Define revenue cycle and its importance to healthcare financial stability
- Identify the 3 segments: pre-service, time-of-service, post-service
- Understand how each department in a healthcare organization impacts revenue cycle
- Define key stakeholders: provider, payer, patient

### Core Concepts
**What is Revenue Cycle?**
The process of managing all financial transactions from patient pre-registration through final payment — spanning scheduling, registration, coding, billing, and collections. Every step that touches money flow between patient, provider, and payer.

**Why Revenue Cycle Matters:**
- Revenue funds hospital operations, staff salaries, equipment
- Broken revenue cycle = hospital can't serve patients
- Revenue cycle errors directly affect patient care access
- RCM is a competitive differentiator for health systems

**Key Stakeholders in the Revenue Cycle:**
- Patient (responsible for co-pays, deductibles, out-of-pocket)
- Provider (delivers care, documents services)
- Payer (insurer, Medicare, Medicaid — pays based on contracts)
- Employer (provides payer benefits to employees)
- Government (Medicare, Medicaid — regulated, tax-funded)

**Healthcare Settings that Use RCM:**
- Hospital inpatient (IP)
- Hospital outpatient (OP)
- Physician practice / clinic
- Ambulatory surgery center (ASC)
- Home health / hospice
- Long-term care / SNF

**JB/SparkChange Angle:**
SparkChange operates in the technology/automation layer — sits between the EHR and the payer. Understanding the full flow is prerequisite to knowing where SparkChange's products create value.

## UNIT 2: Pre-Service — Financial Care (Weeks 2-4)
**Theme: Everything that happens before the patient walks in the door**

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the scheduling and pre-registration process

### Week 2: Scheduling Through Registration (Week 2)
**Core Concepts:**

**Scheduling:**
- Scheduled vs. unscheduled patients
- Information collected at scheduling: name, DOB, insurance, reason for visit
- Provider credentials and insurance network status
- Appointment reminders (reduces no-shows)
- Scheduling systems: EHR-integrated vs. standalone

**Pre-Registration:**
- Collecting demographics before the visit
- Verifying insurance coverage before the appointment
- Benefits investigation: covered services, prior auth requirements, patient responsibility estimates
- Why pre-registration reduces claim denials

**Insurance Verification — The Critical Step:**
- Commercial payers: BCBS, UHC, Aetna, Cigna, Humana, regional plans
- Government payers: Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, VA
- Verification: Who, What, When, How
  - WHO: Patient is the policy holder or dependent?
  - WHAT: Coverage effective dates, exclusions, pre-auth requirements
  - WHEN: Verify before every visit — coverage can change
  - HOW: Real-time electronic (preferred) vs. phone verification (slow)
- Eligibility vs. benefits: Different questions
  - Eligibility: Is the patient covered?
  - Benefits: What is covered? What is patient responsibility?

### Week 3: Prior Authorization (Week 3)
**Core Concepts:**

**What is Prior Authorization?**
- Payer approval required BEFORE delivering a specific service or medication
- Goal: Confirm medical necessity and cost-effectiveness for the payer
- Failure to obtain = automatic denial, patient may be liable

**What Requires Prior Auth (varies by payer):**
- Advanced imaging (MRI, CT, PET scans)
- Specialty drugs / infusions
- Surgeries (especially inpatient)
- Genetic testing
- Durable medical equipment (DME)
- Home health / skilled nursing
- Some lab panels

**The Prior Auth Process:**
1. Provider orders service
2. Staff submits prior auth request to payer
3. Payer reviews: clinical documentation, medical necessity
4. Approval or denial issued (timeframes vary by state/payer)
5. Auth number must appear on the claim

**Prior Auth Frustration (2025-2026):**
- CAQH Index: 41% of provider time spent on prior auth
- State-level reform laws (Toby, Ohio, AZ leading)
- CMS final rule (2024): Medicare Advantage must speed up decisions
- Automation: AI-assisted prior auth is growing (SparkChange opportunity)

**JB Checkpoint Q:** A surgeon schedules an inpatient knee replacement. Prior auth is obtained. Surgery happens. Insurance still denies. Why?

### Week 4: Financial Counseling & Patient Communications (Week 4)
**Core Concepts:**

**Patient Financial Communications Best Practices (HFMA):**
- Standardized financial communication process
- Written estimates before service
- Clear explanation of patient responsibility
- Multiple payment options offered
- Financial assistance programs when applicable

**Financial Counseling Conversations:**
- Who delivers: registration staff, financial counselors, patient access liaisons
- When: Pre-service (phone), at registration, at discharge
- What to communicate: estimated cost, insurance coverage, payment options
- Language: Plain language, avoid financial jargon

**Good Faith Estimates (GFE) — No Surprises Act:**
- Required for uninsured / self-pay patients
- Must be provided 1-3 business days before service
- Must include expected charges for all providers involved
- Scope: Shoppable services first (then all services)

**Charity Care:**
- Required for non-profit hospitals (501(c)(3))
- Financial assistance policies (FAP) must be posted
- Sliding fee scale based on income
- Many patients qualify but don't apply — outreach opportunity

**Payment Options:**
- In-person payment at registration
- Payment plans (interest-free standard)
- Credit card on file (reduces receivables)
- Online payment portals
- Third-party financing (CareCredit, etc.)

## UNIT 3: Time of Service — Financial Care (Weeks 5-6)
**Theme: Collecting money when the patient is right in front of you**

### Week 5: Point-of-Service Collections
**Core Concepts:**

**Why POS Collections Matter:**
- Best time to collect — patient is physically present
- Patient responsibility (deductible, co-pay, co-insurance) is known by this point
- Decreases AR days significantly
- Industry benchmark: 70%+ of patient responsibility should be collected at or before service

**What to Collect at POS:**
- Co-pay (flat fee per visit — known from insurance card)
- Deductible balance (if unmet — needs eligibility verification)
- Co-insurance (percentage of cost — requires benefits verification)
- Past-due balances from previous visits
- Co-payments for upcoming scheduled services

**Point-of-Service Collection Best Practices:**
- Collect co-pays BEFORE the patient sees the provider
- Verify insurance BEFORE the visit — know the patient's responsibility estimate
- Ask for payment at check-in, not check-out
- Script conversations: "Your insurance has a $500 deductible. We've verified your coverage. Today you'll be responsible for [estimate]. How would you like to pay?"
- Never surprise a patient with a bill after service

**Patient Estimates — How to Get Them Right:**
- Real-time eligibility → benefits → calculate patient responsibility
- Use payer fee schedules or Medicare as a benchmark
- Show itemized list of expected charges
- Include all providers who will bill (physician, anesthesiologist, facility)

**JB Checkpoint Q:** Your registration staff says "we asked for payment and the patient got upset and left." Walk me through how you'd coach that conversation and what the new process should look like.

### Week 6: Documentation, Coding, and Charge Capture at Time of Service
**Core Concepts:**

**Charge Capture:**
- Process of recording services rendered into a billing system
- Must match the medical record exactly
- Missing charges = revenue leakage
- Charge master (CDM): The hospital's master list of all billable items

**Charge Master (CDM) vs. Fee Schedule:**
- CDM: What the hospital CHARGES (chargemaster)
- Fee Schedule: What the payer AGREES TO PAY (negotiated rates)
- Medicare Fee Schedule (MPFS): Government-mandated rates for Medicare
- CDM rates are often meaningless — payers pay their contracted rates regardless

**Proper Charge Capture:**
- Every service rendered → charge entered
- Accuracy over speed: Wrong codes = denials
- Timeliness: Charges should be entered same day as service
- Modifier use: Correct use of modifiers changes reimbursement

**Medical Necessity and Documentation:**
- Payer requires medical necessity documentation to justify tests/procedures
- Physician must document: Why was this test necessary?
- Ordering physician ≠ Performing physician (billing different)
- Standing orders for routine tests must be linked to specific diagnoses

## UNIT 4: Claims Submission and Reimbursement (Weeks 7-9)
**Theme: Getting the bill right and getting it to the payer**

### Week 7: Clean Claims and Claims Submission
**Core Concepts:**

**Clean Claim Definition (HFMA/CAHQ):**
A clean claim = no error, no missing information, can be processed immediately by the payer without additional information or development.

**Dirty Claim Problems:**
- Missing or invalid patient information (name, DOB, policy number)
- Incorrect insurance information / outdated coverage
- Missing or incorrect provider NPI
- Unbundled codes / improper modifier use
- Lack of prior authorization / referral number
- Diagnosis-procedure mismatch (ICD-10 → CPT mismatch)
- Duplicate submissions

**Claims Scrubbing:**
- Software checks claims against payer-specific rules before submission
- Catches errors before they cause denials
- Best practice: Scrub against EACH payer's specific edits
- Clearinghouse layer: Waystar, Change Healthcare, TriZetto, Availity

**The Clearinghouse's Role:**
1. Receives claim from provider
2. Validates data format and required fields
3. Applies payer-specific edits
4. Submits to appropriate payer (or rejects back to provider)
5. Tracks acknowledgment (accepted/rejected by payer)

**EDI (Electronic Data Interchange):**
- Standard format for submitting claims electronically (837P for professional, 837I for institutional)
- Required by most payers for timely processing
- Paper claims: rarely accepted, slow, error-prone

**Timely Filing:**
- Medicare: 1 calendar year from date of service
- Most commercial payers: 90 days to 1 year
- Medicaid: 6-12 months (varies by state)
- payers: varies by contract
- MISSED TIMELY FILING = Denial with no appeal — permanent write-off

**JB Checkpoint Q:** A claim is submitted on day 364 of the 365-day timely filing window. It gets rejected by the clearinghouse on day 365 for a missing modifier. What happens?

### Week 8: Reimbursement Methodologies
**Core Concepts:**

**Fee-for-Service (FFS):**
- Per-service payment
- Volume incentivized (more services = more pay)
- Risk: Unnecessary services ordered to generate revenue
- Medicare Part B, most commercial payers historically

**Medicare Fee Schedule (MPFS):**
- Federal schedule setting Medicare payment rates
- Updated annually (Physician Fee Schedule Final Rule)
- Based on RBRVS (Resource-Based Relative Value Scale)
- Geographic adjustment (GPCIs by locality)

**How Medicare Pays Different Settings:**
| Setting | Payment Model |
|---------|--------------|
| Hospital IP | DRG (Diagnosis Related Groups) — flat rate per case |
| Hospital OP | APC (Ambulatory Payment Classifications) — per service |
| Physician Office | MPFS — RBRVS-based per CPT code |
| Home Health | HHA (Home Health Agency) episodic PPS |
| SNF | RUGs / PDPM — per diem based on resident classification |
| ASC | ASC grantor — facility fee per CPT |

**DRG (Diagnosis Related Groups):**
- Inpatient hospital payment methodology
- Flat rate per admission — covers room, board, nursing, routine supplies
- Patient classification determines DRG assignment
- Severity of illness within DRG → MS-DRG weighting
- 3M encoder software assigns MS-DRG from ICD-10-CM/PCS codes

**Capitation:**
- Fixed amount per patient per month — regardless of services used
- Provider bears utilization risk
- Rare in US healthcare outside of some Medicare Advantage plans
- The opposite incentive of FFS

**Value-Based Payment / Risk-Based Contracts:**
- Payment tied to quality + cost outcomes
- Quality metrics: readmission rates, patient satisfaction, clinical outcomes
- Downside risk: Penalties for poor performance
- Upside: Shared savings if under budget
- ACO (Accountable Care Organization) model

**JB Checkpoint Q:** A hospital admits a Medicare patient for a hip replacement. The DRG pays a flat $14,500 for the entire stay. The hospital's cost to deliver the care is $11,000. What is the hospital's margin on this case? What happens if the patient develops a complication and costs $18,000 to treat?

### Week 9: Payer Contracts and Payer Relationships
**Core Concepts:**

**Payer Mix:**
- % of revenue from Medicare, Medicaid, Commercial, Self-pay
- Medicare pays ~85 cents on the dollar of cost (typically)
- Medicaid pays even lower (70-80 cents on the dollar)
- Commercial payers pay more — cross-subsidization happens here
- Payer mix directly affects hospital financial health

**Contracted vs. Non-Contracted Providers:**
- In-network: Provider has contracted rates with payer
- Out-of-network: No contracted rate → payer pays lesser of billed charge or usual/customary rate
- No Surprises Act protects patients from balance billing in certain situations

**Contract Negotiation Fundamentals:**
- Volume (how many patients does this payer bring?)
- Market share and leverage
- Quality metrics (CMS Stars, HEDIS measures)
- Cost to collect (higher cost = justify higher rates)
- Reference-based pricing as negotiating tactic

**Payer Medical Policies:**
- Each payer has written policies on what's medically necessary
- Based on clinical guidelines, published literature, CMS guidelines
- Payer denies claims citing their medical policy
- Provider can appeal — appeal process documented in payer contracts

**Key Payer Relationship Metrics:**
- Clean claim rate by payer
- Denial rate by payer
- Timely filing compliance
- First-pass resolution rate
- Net payment per claim

## UNIT 5: Payment, Adjustments, and Collections (Weeks 10-11)
**Theme: Money coming in — what happens when it does**

### Week 10: ERA, Payment Posting, and Adjustments
**Core Concepts:**

**ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice):**
- Electronic explanation of how a claim was adjudicated
- Shows: allowed amount, paid amount, adjustments, patient responsibility
- Replaces paper EOBs (explanation of benefits)
- Must be reconciled against actual payment (ERA ≠ payment)

**EFT (Electronic Funds Transfer):**
- Direct deposit of payer payment to provider bank account
- Combined with ERA: full electronic payment reconciliation
- Manual posting: Tedious, error-prone, expensive (~$4-6 per claim)

**Auto-Posting:**
- Software automatically posts ERA data to patient accounts
- Matches payment to the correct claim automatically
- Major reduction in labor cost per claim
- Most modern practice management / RCM systems have this

**Contractual Adjustments (Allowables):**
- Difference between what you charged and what the payer contracted to pay
- Also called: Allowances, Payer Adjustments, Contractual Write-offs
- Must be posted when payment is received — cannot bill the patient for this
- Example: Billed $500, allowed $200, paid $160 (patient responsibility)
  - Contractual adjustment: $300
  - Patient responsibility: $160
  - Actual payment received: $160

**Prompt Pay Discounts:**
- Some payer contracts: additional discount if paid within 30 days
- Not automatic — must be tracked
- P&L impact: small but real

**Patient Adjustments / Charity:**
- Bad debt: Patient unable or unwilling to pay → written off as bad debt
- Charity care: Financial assistance write-off per FAP policy
- Bankruptcy: Must follow specific accounting treatment

**Write-Off Categories:**
- Contractual adjustments (cannot bill patient)
- Charity / financial assistance (can bill but chose not to)
- Bad debt (patient non-payment after collection efforts)
- Administrative / timely filing write-offs
- Timely filing: Not billed timely → must be written off

**JB Checkpoint Q:** A payer EOB shows: Billed $1,200, Allowed $480, Paid $384, Patient Responsibility $96. Break down exactly where every dollar goes and what account it's posted to.

### Week 11: Patient Collections and Financial Experience
**Core Concepts:**

**Patient Responsibility Growing Problem:**
- High-deductible health plans (HDHPs): Average deductible $1,500-$3,000 individual
- Patients responsible for much larger share of bill
- Hospitals collect 30-50% of patient responsibility historically
- New HDHP: Patients responsible for first $3,000-$7,000 before insurance kicks in

**Patient Collections Best Practices:**
1. Collect co-pays at check-in (before service)
2. Verify insurance and provide estimate before service
3. Ask for payment at time of service (POS collections)
4. Offer payment plans for larger balances
5. Send statements within 3-5 days of adjudication
6. Follow up at 30, 60, 90 days
7. Offer financial assistance (charity care) when applicable

**Statement Cycles:**
- First statement: Days 0-5 post-adjudication
- Second statement: Days 30
- Third statement: Days 45-60
- Phone call: Days 45-60
- Final notice / collection referral: Days 90-120

**Payment Plans:**
- Industry standard: Interest-free payment plans
- Common threshold: If balance >$500, offer payment plan
- Typical plan: Monthly payment over 6-12 months
- Document payment plan agreement in writing

**Early-Out vs. Late-Out Collections:**
- Early-out: Patient balance $0-$1,000 — internal staff, first 60-90 days
- Late-out / bad debt: Balance >$1,000 and no response — sent to collection agency
- Collection agencies: Typically 25-40% commission
- Patient credit report impact: Collections stay 7 years

**Credit Card on File (CCoF):**
- Patient authorizes credit card on file at registration
- Can process automatically after insurance payment
- Significantly increases collection rates
- Industry data: 95%+ collection rate with CCoF vs. 50% without

## UNIT 6: AR Follow-Up and Denial Management (Weeks 12-14)
**Theme: The money that didn't come — how to get it**

### Week 12: Accounts Receivable and AR Follow-Up
**Core Concepts:**

**AR (Accounts Receivable) — The Lifeblood Metric:**
- Money owed to the hospital for services rendered
- AR days (DSO): How long it takes to collect what's owed
- Industry benchmark: <40 days
- AR is an asset — but uncollected AR is a write-off waiting to happen

**AR Aging Report — The Primary Management Tool:**
```
Current:     0-30 days   → Monitor, normal follow-up
31-60 days:  → First reminder, check payer status
61-90 days:  → Aggressive follow-up, escalation
90+ days:    → Write-off candidate or bad debt referral
```

**AR Follow-Up Process:**
1. Pull AR aging report — identify accounts by age
2. Sort by payer (each payer has different follow-up requirements)
3. Verify claim was submitted (check clearinghouse acknowledgment)
4. Verify timely filing was met
5. Call payer: "What is the status of claim #______?"
6. Document every conversation in the patient account
7. Escalate: Supervisor → Manager → Payer Relations → Legal (if needed)

**Common AR Problems:**
- Timely filing deadlines missed
- Payer lost or never received claim
- Claim in adjudication (waiting for payer decision)
- Patient responsibility not collected
- Coding errors causing denial
- Authorization issues discovered post-service

**Key AR Metrics:**
| Metric | Definition | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| DSO / AR Days | Total AR / (Annual Revenue / 365) | <40 days |
| 90+ AR % | AR over 90 days / Total AR | <15% |
| First-Pass Resolution | % denials resolved without rework | 70%+ |
| Net Collection Rate | Collected / Expected Collectible Revenue | 95%+ |
| Cost to Collect | $ spent / $100 collected | <$3 |

### Week 13: Denial Management — Root Cause and Prevention
**Core Concepts:**

**Denial vs. Rejection:**
- Rejection: Clearinghouse-level error — claim never reached payer. Can be corrected and resubmitted.
- Denial: Payer processed and refused payment. Requires formal appeal process.

**Denial Categories (by HFMA standard):**
1. **Pre-Timing / Pre-Claim** — Registration errors, eligibility issues
2. **Medical Necessity** — Service not deemed necessary by payer
3. **Clinical Documentation** — Missing or incomplete documentation
4. **Coding / Charge Entry** — Wrong code, unbundling, modifier issues
5. **Authorization / Referral** — Prior auth not obtained or not on claim
6. **Duplicate** — Same claim or service submitted twice
7. **Timely Filing** — Submitted past deadline

**Top 5 Denial Codes (CARCs + RARCs):**
- **CO-4** (CARC): Procedure code inconsistent with modifier → unbundling or modifier error
- **CO-16** (CARC): Claim lacks information → most common, easiest to fix, needs additional info
- **CO-18** (CARC): Duplicate claim or service → resubmit with appeal citing original claim #
- **CO-22** (CARC): Coordination of benefits → secondary payer info missing
- **CO-45** (CARC): Charges exceed fee schedule → CDM error or payer fee schedule error

**Denial Root Cause Analysis:**
- Track every denial by: Payer, Denial Code, Service Type, Provider, Date
- Find the pattern — 80% of denials usually come from 3-5 root causes
- Example: 40% of denials = missing prior auth for MRI scans → fix pre-auth workflow
- Example: 25% of denials = modifier 25 missing on E/M + procedure same day → staff training

**Denial Prevention — Front-End Is Cheaper:**
1. Real-time eligibility verification (before every visit)
2. Pre-authorization automation
3. Claim scrubber (pre-submission)
4. Staff training on modifiers and coding
5. CDM review annually
6. Provider documentation audits quarterly

**Cost of a Denial:**
- Average cost to work a denial: $25-$50 per claim (staff time)
- Average denial rate: 5-10% of claims
- A hospital with $100M revenue: $5-10M in denials → $250K-$500K in rework cost
- Prevention is always cheaper than rework

**JB Checkpoint Q:** You run a denial root cause analysis. 35% of all denials are CO-16 (claim lacks information). 80% of those are from the same 3 doctors. What is the problem likely to be and how do you fix it at the source?

### Week 14: Denial Appeals and Resolution
**Core Concepts:**

**Denial Appeal Levels:**
```
Level 1: First Appeal (Peer-to-Peer or Written) → Medical Director Review
Level 2: Second Appeal → Appeal Committee
Level 3: Independent Review (IRO) → External third-party reviewer
Level 4: CMS Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) → Federal level (expensive, slow)
Level 5: Medicare Appeals Council → HHS / Federal
```

**Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Review:**
- Provider calls payer Medical Director to discuss denial
- Must be requested within timeframe (usually 30-60 days)
- Best chance of overturning medical necessity denials
- Be prepared: Clinical documentation, peer-reviewed literature, guidelines
- Most effective for: Medicare Advantage, complex cases

**Writing an Effective Appeal Letter:**
1. Patient name, DOB, account number
2. Date of service, specific service denied
3. Payer denial reason cited
4. Why the service WAS medically necessary (clinical facts)
5. Supporting literature or clinical guidelines
6. What you are requesting (full payment, reconsideration)
7. Provider signature and credentials

**Winning Appeals:**
- Timeliness: File appeals on time — missing deadlines = lost
- Documentation: The medical record is your evidence
- Payer policy: Cite the exact policy that supports your position
- Clinical guidelines: Peer-reviewed literature strengthens argument
- Persistence: 30-40% of first appeals are overturned

**Denial Metrics That Matter:**
- Gross denial rate (total denials / total claims)
- Net denial rate (after appeals resolved)
- First-pass denial overturn rate
- Average days to overturn
- Denial rate by payer (some payers deny more than others)

**JB Checkpoint Q:** A payer denies a knee replacement as "not medically necessary" for a 68-year-old patient with documented arthritis. You have an orthopedic surgeon who disagrees. Walk me through the appeal process you would run, in order, and tell me what your strongest argument would be.

## UNIT 7: Regulatory, Compliance, and Reimbursement (Weeks 15-17)
**Theme: The rules of the road — what you must do**

### Week 15: HIPAA Compliance in Revenue Cycle
**Core Concepts:**

**HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act):**
- Enacted 1996 — protects patient health information
- Administrative Simplification: Standardized transactions (EDI), unique identifiers
- Privacy Rule: Protects PHI — applies to all PHI holders
- Security Rule: Technical, physical, administrative safeguards for ePHI
- Breach Notification Rule: Must notify HHS and patients within 60 days of discovery

**PHI (Protected Health Information):**
- Any individually identifiable health information
- 18 identifiers: Name, DOB, SSN, Address, Phone, Email, Medical record #, etc.
- In RCM: Every claim, every billing record, every EOB contains PHI
- PHI must be handled with minimum necessary standard

**Minimum Necessary Standard:**
- Use/disclose only the minimum PHI needed for the task
- Example: Billing department gets only the fields needed to submit the claim
- Don't pull a full medical record for AR follow-up

**Business Associate Agreements (BAAs):**
- Required when outsourcing any function that touches PHI
- Vendors: collection agencies, billing services, IT vendors, cloud providers
- BAA = vendor agrees to protect PHI to HIPAA standards
- Without a BAA: Cannot share PHI with that vendor

**Revenue Cycle-Specific HIPAA Risks:**
- Emailing patient statements (must be encrypted)
- Faxing claims / remits (wrong fax number = breach)
- Staff accessing records they don't need for their job
- Lost / stolen laptops with patient data
- Improper disposal of paper records
- Hackers targeting healthcare billing data (lucrative on dark web)

**HIPAA Enforcement and Penalties:**
| Tier | Description | Annual Penalty |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Tier 1 | Didn't know | $100-$50,000/violation |
| Tier 2 | Reasonable cause | $1,000-$50,000/violation |
| Tier 3 | Willful neglect (corrected) | $10,000-$50,000/violation |
| Tier 4 | Willful neglect (not corrected) | $50,000+/violation |

### Week 16: CMS Regulations and Government Payers
**Core Concepts:**

**Medicare Overview:**
- Federal health insurance: Primarily 65+, disabled, ESRD
- Part A (Hospital Insurance): Inpatient, skilled nursing, home health, hospice
- Part B (Medical Insurance): Outpatient, physician, DME, lab
- Part C (Medicare Advantage): Private plans, runs as replacement to A+B
- Part D (Prescription Drug): Outpatient prescription coverage

**Medicare Fee Schedule (MPFS):**
- Annual Physician Fee Schedule Final Rule published by CMS
- Three Conversion Factors in 2025: Anesthesia (unchanged), E/M (slight increase), rest (slight decrease)
- Geographic Practice Cost Indices (GPCIs) adjust by locality
- Resource-Based Relative Value Scale (RBRVS): RVUs × GPCI × Conversion Factor

**Medicaid:**
- Joint federal-state program (federal match + state share)
- Income-based eligibility (varies by state)
- State Medicaid programs have significant variation
- Managed Medicaid: States contract MCOs to manage care
- Medicaid expansion (ACA): States that opted in cover more adults

**Recovery Audit Contractors (RACs):**
- CMS contracts with RACs to audit Medicare claims post-payment
- Looking for: Overpayments (most common), underpayments
- 3-year lookback window for most audits
- RACs are paid on contingency: They get a % of what they find
- Providers can appeal RAC decisions (QIC → ALJ → etc.)

**OIG (Office of Inspector General) Compliance:**
- Annual OIG Work Plan: Lists audit focus areas
- Fraud, waste, and abuse investigations
- Stark Law: Physician self-referral prohibition
- Anti-Kickback Statute: No inducements for referrals
- Corporate Compliance Programs: OIG guidance for healthcare orgs

**No Surprises Act (NSA) — 2022:**
1. Good Faith Estimates: Uninsured / self-pay patients get written estimate
2. Surprise Billing Protection: Out-of-network emergency + ancillary services
3. Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR): Providers/payers contest payment amounts
4. Advanced Explanation of Benefits: Scheduled services — payer informs patient of network status + estimates

**Price Transparency (CMS Rules):**
- Jan 2021: Hospitals must publish machine-readable CDM (chargemaster)
- July 2024: Shoppable services must show standard charges in plain language
- Penalties: $300/day for small hospitals (no maximum cap as of 2024)
- Most hospitals still non-compliant — enforcement increasing

### Week 17: Healthcare Fraud, Abuse, and Corporate Compliance
**Core Concepts:**

**Fraud (Intentional):**
- Knowingly submitting false claims
- Examples: Billing for services not rendered, upcoding, kickbacks for referrals
- Criminal and civil penalties: Fines + imprisonment
- False Claims Act (qui tam): Whistleblower can sue on behalf of government

**Abuse (Unintentional but Improper):**
- Practices inconsistent with sound fiscal, business, or medical practices
- May result in overpayments without intent
- Often leads to fraud if continued without correction
- Examples: Unbundling without intent, lack of medical necessity documentation

**Key Anti-Fraud Laws:**
1. **False Claims Act (FCA):** Civil penalties for presenting false claims to federal government. Qui tam provisions allow whistleblowers. Penalties: $13K-$27K per claim + 3x damages
2. **Stark Law (Anti-Kickback):** Physicians cannot refer Medicare/Medicaid patients for Designated Health Services (DHS) to entities they have a financial relationship with. Civil penalties only.
3. **Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS):** No inducements to generate Medicare/Medicaid business. Criminal + civil penalties. Safe harbors exist (bona fide employment, discounts, etc.)
4. **Civil Monetary Penalties Law (CMPL):** Additional penalties for various healthcare fraud scenarios

**Corporate Compliance Programs (OIG Guidance):**
- 7 elements of an effective compliance program:
  1. Written policies and procedures
  2. Compliance officer and compliance committee
  3. Training and education
  4. Effective lines of communication
  5. Enforcement of standards / disciplinary guidelines
  6. Internal monitoring and auditing
  7. Prompt response to detected violations / corrective action

**Revenue Cycle-Specific Compliance Risks:**
- Upcoding: Billing higher-level E/M than documentation supports
- Unbundling: Billing separately what should be billed as one
- Duplicate billing: Billing same claim or service twice
- Medically unnecessary services: Ordered for revenue rather than clinical need
- Credit balance / overpayment failures: Not returning known overpayments
- HIM / coding errors: Honest mistakes that still cause overpayments

### Week 20: Performance Improvement and Operational Excellence
**Core Concepts:**

**PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) Applied to RCM:**
1. **Plan:** Identify a specific RCM problem with measurable baseline
   - Example: Denial rate is 9%, goal is <5%
   - Root cause: 40% of denials are authorization-related

2. **Do:** Implement the fix on a pilot basis
   - Example: Pre-auth workflow for all MRI orders starting Monday

3. **Check:** Measure at 30, 60, 90 days post-implementation
   - Example: Denial rate drops to 6% at 60 days

4. **Act:** Adopt the fix broadly if successful; adjust and re-test if not
   - Scale to all high-dollar imaging

**Building a Revenue Cycle Steering Committee:**
- Members: RCM Director, CFO, HIM/Coding Manager, IT, Clinical representatives
- Cadence: Monthly minimum
- Agenda: KPI review → Denials trends → Project pipeline → Compliance risks
- Output: Action items with owners and deadlines

**Staffing and Productivity:**
- FTE benchmarking: FTE per $1M net revenue
- Key roles: Patient access, billing, coding, follow-up, cash posting, AR management
- Productivity standards: Claims processed per biller, AR accounts per follow-up rep
- Remote work becoming standard for billing/coding roles post-COVID

**Charge Description Master (CDM) Annual Review:**
- Must be done annually (CMS requirement)
- Compare internal charges to Medicare/Medicaid rates
- Find: Underpriced items (revenue leakage) and overpriced items (competitiveness)
- Update drug acquisition costs annually
- Clinical staff (not just finance) should review for accuracy

**Clinical Documentation Improvement (CDI):**
- Concurrent review of physician documentation during hospitalization
- Goal: Accurate ICD-10 coding and DRG assignment
- MS-DRG weight depends on accurate, complete documentation
- CDI specialist reviews records and queries physicians in real-time
- Better documentation = Higher weighted DRG = More accurate reimbursement
