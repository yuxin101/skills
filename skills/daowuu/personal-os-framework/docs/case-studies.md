# Case Studies

## Case Study 1: The Decision That Saved Three Days

A team was debating whether to rebuild their API from scratch or continue patching the legacy system. The debate had been going on for two weeks.

They implemented Decision Log. Every team member wrote up their position with:
- What they recommended
- Why they recommended it
- What they expected to happen if their recommendation was chosen
- What alternatives they considered

Within three days of implementing Decision Log:
- A clear decision emerged from the written arguments
- The team chose the rebuild path
- Within six weeks, the rebuild was complete
- A post-mortem showed the person who advocated for rebuild was right about the expected outcomes

The Decision Log didn't just record the decision. It created a structured debate that surfaced the key arguments faster than any meeting could.

## Case Study 2: The Weekly Review That Caught a Stale Project

A product manager had 12 active projects. Every week, they ran a 15-minute review using the Review Operator template.

In week 7, the review surfaced a project that hadn't been updated in 6 weeks. The project was marked "in progress" but no one had worked on it for over a month.

Without the review, the project would have continued to sit stale, consuming mental overhead. With the review, the team:
- Identified the project as no longer needed
- Formally closed it
- Reassigned resources to active projects

The cost of the stale project wasn't just the overhead. It was the uncertainty it created: people weren't sure if it was still active, so they avoided conflicting with it.

## Case Study 3: Routing Rules Prevented Duplicate Work

A researcher was drowning in notes. Every time they had an idea, they wrote it down. But they couldn't find their notes later, so they wrote new notes.

Six months of notes existed across five different systems. When they tried to implement an idea from March, they discovered they'd had the same idea in January -- and had implemented a different idea instead.

They implemented Routing Rules. Now, every note goes to Inbox first. Weekly, they review Inbox and route items:
- Ideas that are actionable become tasks
- Reference material goes to Knowledge
- Decisions get logged with rationale

Three months later, the researcher has:
- One place to look for notes
- A clear understanding of what's actionable
- A growing Knowledge base that actually gets referenced

## Case Study 4: The Memory Distiller That Created a Second Brain

An executive had years of meeting notes. Every meeting, they captured key points. But they'd never look at the notes again.

They implemented Memory Distillation. Notes sit in Inbox for 7 days. Then they review:
- Is this worth keeping?
- What's the key insight?
- How would I categorize this?

Items that pass review get:
- Clean title
- Extracted tags
- Written summary
- Moved to Knowledge

Six months later, the executive's Knowledge base has 200 entries. When preparing for a board meeting, they searched the Knowledge base and found three relevant notes from meetings two years ago. The notes would have been lost without distillation.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Decisions Log creates accountability** -- Writing down your reasoning makes it easier to evaluate decisions later.

2. **Reviews catch stale items** -- Without regular review, stale projects consume mental overhead and create uncertainty.

3. **Routing prevents chaos** -- Every note goes somewhere. Routing rules ensure notes can be found later.

4. **Memory Distillation creates value from noise** -- Most raw notes are noise. The valuable insights get lost. Distillation surfaces the valuable stuff.

5. **The system only works if maintained** -- These case studies all involve consistent use. The framework is a tool, and tools only work when used.
